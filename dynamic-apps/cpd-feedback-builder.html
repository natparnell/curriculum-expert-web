Effective Feedback: Key Principles

The WWW/EBI Framework

WWW/EBI is a widely used structured feedback model. "What Went Well" highlights specific strengths, reinforcing successful strategies. "Even Better If" frames areas for improvement constructively, focusing on actionable next steps rather than deficits. This approach ensures feedback is balanced, motivating, and forward-looking.

EEF Research on Effective Feedback

The Education Endowment Foundation identifies feedback as one of the highest-impact strategies, adding up to +6 months of progress when delivered well. Key findings include:

  • Feedback should be specific, accurate, and clear.
  • It should relate to the learning intentions and success criteria.
  • It should encourage and support further effort, not just correct errors.
  • Feedback is most effective when it causes thinking rather than an emotional response.
  • The timeliness of feedback matters: it should be given while still relevant.
Growth Mindset in Feedback

Feedback informed by growth mindset research emphasises effort, strategy, and progress over fixed ability. Avoid phrases like "You are a natural at this" and instead use "You worked hard on this and it shows." Encourage learners to see mistakes as part of the learning process. Frame improvement targets as achievable through specific actions, not innate talent.

Specific vs Generic Feedback

Generic feedback such as "Good work" or "Try harder" gives learners little actionable information. Effective feedback is specific: it names exactly what was done well and precisely what should be improved. For example, instead of "Good analysis," try "You clearly linked your point to the quotation and explained its effect on the reader." Specificity helps learners understand what to replicate and what to change.
