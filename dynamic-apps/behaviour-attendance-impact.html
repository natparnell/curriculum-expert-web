Impact on Learning: What the Research Shows

  • Students with 97%+ attendance are 5 times more likely to achieve grade 5 or above in English and Maths GCSEs than those below 90%.
  • Each additional day of absence is associated with a measurable drop in attainment. Even a 1% decrease in attendance can affect outcomes.
  • A pupil with 90% attendance over their school career misses the equivalent of an entire school year of learning.
  • At 90% attendance, pupils miss around 50 lessons every year, including key introductions to new topics, revision sessions, and assessments.
  • Pupils who are persistently absent (below 90%) are significantly less likely to achieve expected grades and are more likely to become NEET (Not in Education, Employment, or Training) after Year 11.
  • National data consistently shows that the attainment gap between persistent absentees and regular attenders is equivalent to two full GCSE grades across every subject.
  • Absence has a compounding effect: missed lessons create knowledge gaps that make subsequent lessons harder to access, leading to disengagement.
  • Schools with the strongest attendance cultures typically see higher attainment, better behaviour, and improved wellbeing outcomes across all year groups.

Understanding Attendance

Why Every Day Matters

Each school day builds on the last. Lessons are carefully sequenced so that missing even one day means missing the foundation for what comes next. Unlike a job where tasks can wait, learning moves forward every day. Catch-up is possible, but it takes significantly more effort than simply being present in the first place.

Persistent Absence

A pupil is classified as a persistent absentee if their attendance drops below 90%. This means missing 19 or more days in a year. Nationally, around 1 in 5 pupils falls into this category. Despite the name, it does not require long continuous absence; frequent odd days off can quickly add up past the threshold.

Below 90% attendance triggers additional monitoring and may lead to formal action by the local authority.

Legal Requirements

Parents and carers have a legal duty under the Education Act 1996 to ensure their child attends school regularly. Failure to do so can result in:

  • Fixed penalty notices (currently up to £160 per parent, per child)
  • Education Supervision Orders
  • Prosecution, with fines of up to £2,500 or a custodial sentence in severe cases

Term-time holidays are not an entitlement. Headteachers may only authorise leave in exceptional circumstances.

Tips for Improving Attendance

  • Establish a consistent morning routine and prepare the night before.
  • Seek medical appointments outside school hours where possible.
  • Communicate early with school if barriers to attendance emerge.
  • Recognise and celebrate good attendance at home.
  • Access pastoral or wellbeing support if anxiety is a factor.
  • Avoid booking holidays during term time.
  • If your child is unwell, speak with school to understand when they can return; many minor illnesses do not require a full day off.
