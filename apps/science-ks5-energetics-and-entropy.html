The terms in a Born-Haber cycle

Hess's law states that the total enthalpy change for a reaction is independent of the route taken. The cycle equates the direct route (enthalpy of formation, ΔfH) with the indirect route through gaseous atoms and ions:

ΔfH = ΔatH(metal) + ΔatH(non-metal) + ΣIE + ΣEA + ΔLEH

Lattice enthalpy (formation)

Enthalpy change when one mole of an ionic solid forms from its gaseous ions. Always exothermic (negative).

Enthalpy of atomisation, ΔatH

Enthalpy to form one mole of gaseous atoms from an element in its standard state. Always endothermic.

First ionisation energy, IE₁

Energy to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms. Endothermic.

First electron affinity, EA₁

Enthalpy when one mole of gaseous atoms each gain an electron. Usually exothermic; second EA is endothermic.

Enthalpy of solution, hydration and dissolving

When an ionic solid dissolves, the lattice must break apart (endothermic, the reverse of lattice enthalpy) and the gaseous ions become hydrated (exothermic):

ΔsolH = −ΔLEH + ΣΔhydH

  • Enthalpy of hydration, ΔhydH: enthalpy when one mole of gaseous ions dissolves in water to form aqueous ions. More negative for smaller, more highly charged ions.
  • Enthalpy of solution, ΔsolH: enthalpy when one mole of solid dissolves fully in water. A small positive or negative value (the lattice and hydration terms nearly cancel).

Entropy, S

Entropy is a measure of the disorder of a system: the number of ways the energy and particles can be arranged. The more ways, the higher the entropy. Units are J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ (note: joules, not kilojoules).

To predict the sign of the entropy change, ΔS, look mainly at the change in number of moles of gas:

  • More moles of gas on the right means ΔS is positive (more disorder).
  • Fewer moles of gas on the right means ΔS is negative.
  • Changes of state matter too: solid to liquid to gas all increase entropy.

ΔSsystem = ΣS(products) − ΣS(reactants)

Gibbs Free Energy & Feasibility

ΔG = ΔH − TΔS

A reaction is feasible (spontaneous) when ΔG ≤ 0. Enter the standard enthalpy and entropy changes, then drag the temperature slider to watch ΔG cross zero. The app reports the temperature at which the reaction becomes feasible.

Reading the ΔG against T graph

Rearranging ΔG = ΔH − TΔS shows it is a straight line: ΔG on the y-axis, temperature on the x-axis, intercept ΔH and gradient −ΔS.

  • ΔH positive, ΔS positive: endothermic but increasing disorder. Not feasible when cold, becomes feasible above the feasibility temperature (line slopes down through zero).
  • ΔH negative, ΔS negative: exothermic but decreasing disorder. Feasible when cold, stops being feasible above the feasibility temperature.
  • ΔH negative, ΔS positive: feasible at all temperatures.
  • ΔH positive, ΔS negative: never feasible.

Setting ΔG = 0 gives the feasibility temperature: T = ΔH / ΔS (with ΔH in J mol⁻¹ so the units match).

Exam-style Practice

OCR A H432
Question 1[3 marks]
Define the term lattice enthalpy and explain why it cannot be measured directly.

Lattice enthalpy is the enthalpy change when one mole of an ionic solid is formed from its gaseous ions under standard conditions (1 mark).

It cannot be measured directly because the formation of a lattice from isolated gaseous ions cannot be carried out experimentally (1 mark). It is instead found indirectly using a Born-Haber cycle and Hess's law (1 mark).

Question 2[3 marks]
Use the following data to calculate the lattice enthalpy of formation of potassium chloride, KCl.
ΔfH = −437; ΔatH(K) = +89; ΔatH(Cl) = +122; IE₁(K) = +419; EA₁(Cl) = −349 (all kJ mol⁻¹).

By Hess's law: ΔLEH = ΔfH − [ΔatH(K) + ΔatH(Cl) + IE₁ + EA₁] (1 mark for the cycle).

= −437 − (89 + 122 + 419 − 349)

= −437 − 281 = −718 kJ mol⁻¹ (1 mark working, 1 mark answer with sign and units).

Question 3[2 marks]
Predict and explain the sign of the entropy change for: 2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l)

ΔS is negative (1 mark).

There are 3 moles of gas on the left and 0 moles of gas (liquid water) on the right, so the number of gaseous moles falls and disorder decreases (1 mark).

Question 4[4 marks]
For the thermal decomposition of calcium carbonate, ΔH = +178 kJ mol⁻¹ and ΔS = +161 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹. Calculate the temperature above which the reaction becomes feasible.

At the feasibility temperature, ΔG = 0, so ΔH = TΔS and T = ΔH / ΔS (1 mark).

Convert ΔH to joules: 178 000 J mol⁻¹ (1 mark for matching units).

T = 178 000 / 161 = 1106 K (1 mark).

Above about 1106 K the reaction is feasible because ΔS is positive, so TΔS grows until it exceeds ΔH and ΔG becomes negative (1 mark).

Question 5[3 marks]
Explain, in terms of ionic radius and charge, why the lattice enthalpy of MgO is much more exothermic than that of NaCl.

Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ carry 2+ and 2− charges, whereas Na⁺ and Cl⁻ carry only single charges (1 mark).

The ionic radii of Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ are also smaller, so the ions sit closer together (1 mark).

Greater charge and smaller separation give a stronger electrostatic attraction between the ions, so more energy is released and the lattice enthalpy is more exothermic (1 mark).
