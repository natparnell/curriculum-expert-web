Reference: types of data, the right graph, and the measures

Types of data

Quantitative data is numerical (time in seconds, heart rate in beats per minute, distance in metres). Qualitative data is descriptive and based on opinion or observation (a coach's notes, a self-report of "felt tired"). GCSE PE values quantitative data for objective comparison.

Quantitative data splits further. Discrete data can only take set values (number of goals, level reached on the multi-stage fitness test). Continuous data can take any value within a range (a sprint time, a heart rate, a jump height).

Measures of central tendency and spread

Mean: add all values, divide by how many there are. Uses every value but is pulled by extremes. Median: the middle value when ordered (or the mean of the middle two). Resistant to extreme values. Mode: the most common value; the only average usable for categories. Range: highest minus lowest; a simple measure of spread.

Reading a heart-rate graph

Resting heart rate is the baseline before exercise. The anticipatory rise is the small increase just before activity starts, driven by adrenaline and the sympathetic nervous system. The working heart rate rises with intensity towards maximum (estimated as 220 minus age). Recovery is the return towards resting after exercise; a faster recovery indicates better aerobic fitness.

Normative data

Fitness-test results are judged against published normative tables for age and sex, which band scores from "poor" to "excellent". This lets a raw score be interpreted as a rating rather than read in isolation.
