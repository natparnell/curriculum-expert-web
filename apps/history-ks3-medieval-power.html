👑Who Held Power in Medieval Britain?

Anglo-Saxon England

Before the Conquest, England was ruled by an Anglo-Saxon king advised by the Witan (a council of nobles and bishops). Power was shared between the Crown, the great earls and the Church. Step forward to see how the Normans changed everything.

1215: the barons force the King's hand

King John had lost wars in France, taxed the barons heavily and quarrelled with the Pope. In 1215 the angry barons captured London and forced John to put his seal to the Magna Carta (the Great Charter) at Runnymede. Magna Carta

Sealed by King John at Runnymede, 15 June 1215

"To no one will we sell, to no one will we deny or delay, right or justice."

For the first time, a document said the King was not above the law. He could not jail free men or raise new taxes simply because he wished to. Power could be limited by agreement, and a council of barons could hold him to his word.

Why it mattered

John soon asked the Pope to cancel the charter and war broke out again. Yet the idea survived. Later kings reissued Magna Carta, and from these councils of barons grew the beginnings of Parliament: by 1295 knights and townsmen were summoned to advise the King and agree to taxes. The principle that rulers must answer to others had taken root.

The Black Death, 1348 to 1349: cause and consequence

The plague reached England in 1348 and killed roughly one in three people within two years. Move the slider to see the population fall, then follow the chain of consequences. A disaster of disease became a turning point that strengthened the bargaining power of ordinary working people.

The chain of consequence

Cause☠ Mass death

The plague kills around a third of the population. Whole villages are emptied and there are far fewer people to work the land.

Consequence Labour shortage

With so many dead, lords cannot find enough peasants to plough the fields and bring in the harvest. Workers are suddenly scarce and badly needed.

Consequence Wages rise

Surviving peasants can demand higher pay and better conditions, or move to a lord who will pay more. The value of ordinary labour shoots up.

Reaction Laws and revolt

Parliament passes the Statute of Labourers (1351) to freeze wages, and a hated Poll Tax follows. Resentment builds towards the Peasants' Revolt of 1381.

As the death toll climbs, drag the slider to see how a catastrophe of disease slowly handed working people more bargaining power than they had ever held before.
