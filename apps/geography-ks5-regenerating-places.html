Regenerating Places

Teaching aim: model how and why the economy and society of a place change over time, then run a regeneration simulator in which you read a declining place's profile, choose strategies within a budget, balance the views of competing players, and evaluate success across economic, social and environmental indicators.

01

Why places change and why some need regeneration

The economic structure of a place shifts between primary, secondary, tertiary and quaternary employment over time, a pattern summarised by the Clark-Fisher model. Where deindustrialisation, decline and deprivation take hold, places may need regeneration; others, with diverse high-value economies, do not.

Sectors of employment

Primary extracts raw materials (mining, fishing, farming). Secondary manufactures goods. Tertiary provides services (retail, health). Quaternary is knowledge-based (research, IT, biotech). A shift toward tertiary and quaternary work signals a maturing economy.

Measuring variation

Quantitative indicators: employment rate, median income, the Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMD), house prices and qualifications. Qualitative sources: lived experience, oral testimony and media representation. Together they reveal economic and social inequality between and within places.

Players and government

National government sets planning, infrastructure and deregulation. Local government and Local Enterprise Partnerships (LEPs) steer local growth. Developers, investors and communities each hold different priorities, and regeneration must balance them.

Strategies and evaluation

Approaches include retail-led, sport and leisure-led, science and business parks, rural diversification and rebranding and reimaging. Success is judged against economic, social and environmental measures, and remains contested between players.

06

Exam-style questions

Edexcel 9GE0 style. Attempt each, then reveal the indicative content.

Question 1 · 4 marks
Explain how the Clark-Fisher model accounts for the change in employment structure of a post-industrial place.
Reveal indicative content
The model shows employment shifting between sectors as an economy develops.
  • In the pre-industrial stage primary employment dominates (extraction and farming).
  • During industrialisation, secondary (manufacturing) employment rises and overtakes primary as mechanisation reduces primary labour needs.
  • In the post-industrial stage, deindustrialisation causes secondary employment to fall while tertiary (services) and quaternary (knowledge / research) employment rise to dominate, reflecting higher incomes, automation and global shifts in manufacturing.
Question 2 · 6 marks
Assess the value of using both quantitative indicators and qualitative sources to measure variation between places.
Reveal indicative content
Quantitative indicators (employment rate, median income, IMD, qualifications) are objective, comparable across places and over time, and reveal the scale of inequality. However they can mask internal variation and lived reality.

Qualitative sources (lived experience, oral testimony, media representation) capture perception, identity and the social meaning of place that numbers miss, but they are subjective and may be unrepresentative; media representation in particular can distort an outsider view (for example only filming the most deprived street).

Assessment: the two are complementary. Quantitative data establishes patterns and supports targeting of regeneration funds, while qualitative evidence explains the causes, validates priorities with communities and guards against decisions made on figures alone.
Question 3 · 12 marks
Evaluate the view that the success of regeneration should be judged primarily on economic measures.
Reveal indicative content
Supporting the view: economic measures (new jobs, employment rate, income, reduced vacancy, inward investment) are the usual justification for public spending and LEP involvement, are readily quantified, and underpin wider improvement because rising incomes can fund services and reduce deprivation.

Challenging the view: economic gains can be uneven; retail-led or sport-led schemes may create low-paid, insecure work or displace existing residents (gentrification), so social and demographic measures (IMD change, who benefits, affordable housing, net migration, community cohesion) matter. Environmental measures (brownfield reclamation, green space, air quality, carbon of new build) affect long-term liveability and sustainability.

Judgement: success is contested and should be evaluated across all three dimensions and by all players. Economic data is necessary but insufficient; a scheme that lifts headline jobs while worsening deprivation for existing residents or degrading the environment cannot be judged a full success. The relative weighting depends on the place's original needs and whose perspective is taken.
