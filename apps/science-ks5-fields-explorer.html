Gravitational field. A mass sets up a radial field that always points towards the mass: gravity is only ever attractive. Field strength is force per unit mass, g = F/m, and for a point mass g = GM/r² by Newton's law of gravitation F = GMm/r². The purple loops are equipotentials: lines of equal gravitational potential V = −GM/r, always negative and rising to zero at infinity. Field lines always cross equipotentials at right angles.

The gravitational – electric analogy

Core theory

Gravitational fields

Newton's law of gravitation between two point masses:

F = G M m / r²

Field strength (force per unit mass), a vector pointing towards the mass:

g = F / m = G M / r²

Gravitational potential (work done per unit mass moving from infinity):

V = − G M / r
  • Always attractive; field lines point inward.
  • Potential is always negative, zero at infinity.
  • Uniform field near a large surface: parallel, evenly spaced lines, constant g.

Electric fields

Coulomb's law between two point charges:

F = Q q / (4πε₀ r²)

Field strength (force per unit positive charge):

E = F / Q = Q / (4πε₀ r²)

Uniform field between parallel plates:

E = V / d
  • Attractive or repulsive; lines point away from + charge, towards −.
  • Same inverse-square form as gravity, so g and E behave alike.
  • Key difference: two signs of charge, only one sign of mass.
Why the analogy matters. Both are inverse-square fields, so the geometry of field lines and equipotentials is identical: only the sign and the source quantity change. Mass replaces charge, G replaces 1/(4πε₀). The decisive difference is that mass is single-signed, so gravity only attracts, whereas charge comes in two signs, so electric fields attract and repel. Switch the visualiser between the two modes and place a like-pair then an unlike-pair to see this.

Charge against time
The time constant. A capacitor stores charge by Q = CV and stores energy E = ½QV = ½CV². Charging or discharging through a resistor follows an exponential: discharge gives Q = Q₀ e^(−t/RC) and charging gives Q = Q₀(1 − e^(−t/RC)). The product RC is the time constant τ: the time for the charge to fall to 1/e (about 37%) of its initial value, marked by the dashed line. After about 5RC the capacitor is essentially fully charged or discharged.

The motor effect. A wire carrying current I across a magnetic field of flux density B feels a force F = BIL when the field and current are perpendicular (and F = BIL sinθ in general). For a single charge moving with velocity v the equivalent is F = BQv. Use Fleming's left-hand rule: thumb = force (motion), first finger = field, second finger = current. Reverse the current and the force reverses, as the arrow shows. This is also the basis of electromagnetic induction: when flux linkage changes, Faraday's law gives an induced EMF, and Lenz's law makes that EMF oppose the change producing it.
