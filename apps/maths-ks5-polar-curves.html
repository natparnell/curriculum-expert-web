How polar coordinates work

A point is fixed not by (x, y) but by a distance r from the origin (the pole) and an angle θ measured anticlockwise from the positive x-axis (the initial line).

Polar to Cartesian

x = r cosθ  and  y = r sinθ. As θ turns and r changes with it, the point sweeps out a curve.

Reading the family of curves
  • Circle r = a: r is constant, so every point sits a units from the pole.
  • Cardioid r = a(1 + cosθ): a heart shape with a cusp at the pole when θ = π.
  • Limaçon r = a + b cosθ: if b > a an inner loop appears (r goes negative); if a = b it is a cardioid; if a ≥ 2b it is convex.
  • Rose r = a cos(kθ): k petals when k is odd, 2k petals when k is even. Sine roses are the same shape, rotated.
  • Spiral r = aθ: r grows steadily with the angle, winding outward.
The area swept by the radius arm

A thin sector between θ and θ + dθ is almost a triangle of area ½ r·(r dθ). Summing these gives A = ½ ∫ r² dθ. This tool evaluates that integral numerically with the trapezium rule over a fine grid, so any r(θ) you choose is handled.

Watch for negative r: on limaçons with an inner loop, r² is still positive, so the naive integral over a full turn double-counts the overlap. Set α and β to the loop limits to isolate a single region.
