Levels of language analysis

A consistent framework for any spoken or written data. AQA rewards analysis that moves up and down these levels and connects them.

Lexis & semantics

Word choice and meaning: semantic fields, connotation, jargon, slang, formality, taboo and euphemism.

Grammar

Syntax and morphology: sentence function and mood, modality, pronouns, tag questions, non-standard agreement.

Phonology

Sound: accent features, elision, prosody, intonation, sociophonetic variants such as h-dropping or t-glottalling.

Graphology

Visual organisation of written text: layout, typeface, capitalisation, emoji, punctuation for effect.

Pragmatics

Meaning in context: implicature, politeness, positive and negative face, deixis, the cooperative principle.

Discourse

Whole-text patterning: turn-taking, adjacency pairs, topic management, openings and closings, cohesion.

Quick theory glossary

Lakoff (1975)

Deficit model. Claimed women's language shows hedges, tag questions, empty adjectives, super-polite forms and rising intonation, treated as marks of tentativeness.

Zimmerman & West (1975)

Dominance model. Found men produced the large majority of interruptions in mixed-sex talk, framed as conversational dominance.

Tannen (1990)

Difference model. Argued men and women use language for different goals: report talk versus rapport talk, status versus connection.

Cameron (2007)

The myth. Critiqued the idea that men and women communicate differently as a popular myth; differences within each group exceed those between them.

Fairclough

Power in discourse. Distinguished instrumental from influential power, named synthetic personalisation and the unequal encounter.

Labov (1966)

Variationist sociolinguistics. Linked variants such as rhoticity to social class and style; overt and covert prestige.

Descriptivism versus prescriptivism

Prescriptivism

Holds that there is a correct form of English and change is decline. Linked to standardisation, Johnson's 1755 dictionary and complaint-tradition figures. Concepts: the "crumbling castle" and "infectious disease" metaphors (Aitchison).

Descriptivism

Describes language as people actually use it, without judgement. Treats change as natural and rule-governed. Concepts: the "damp spoon", and theories of change such as the wave model, S-curve and random fluctuation.

Standardisation

Selection, codification (dictionaries, grammars), elaboration and acceptance of one variety as Standard English. The standard is a prestige variety, not a linguistically superior one.

Why change happens

Functional theory (new needs, new words), substratum theory (contact), random fluctuation, and the influence of technology, migration and the media.

Structuring a discursive essay on language diversity

AQA Paper 2 rewards a coherent, evaluative argument, not a list of features. Use this scaffold.

1 · Open with a position

State a clear line on the question (for example, that attitudes to accent reveal social prejudice, not linguistic fact). Avoid summarising the question back.

2 · Analyse data first

Lead with features from the data at named language levels. Quote precisely and use terminology accurately.

3 · Bring in theory to explain

Use theorists to interpret the feature, then evaluate the fit. "Zimmerman and West would read this as dominance, though context suggests cooperative overlap."

4 · Weigh competing views

Set deficit against dominance against difference; set prescriptivism against descriptivism. Cameron's critique is useful for complicating gender claims.

5 · Use concepts as lenses

Synthetic personalisation, overt and covert prestige, face and politeness give you analytical purchase beyond naming features.

6 · Conclude with judgement

Return to your line and qualify it. Sophistication comes from acknowledging that one model rarely explains everything.

Theory toolkit

Language and gender: the three models

Deficit (Lakoff)

Women's language framed as lacking authority: hedges, tag questions, empty adjectives, intensifiers, super-polite forms. Criticised for measuring women against a male norm.

Dominance (Zimmerman & West; Fishman)

Differences arise from men's social power: interruptions, minimal responses, women doing the "conversational shitwork" of keeping talk going.

Difference (Tannen; Maltz & Borker)

Men and women socialised into different styles: report versus rapport talk; status versus connection. Treats both as valid, not deficient.

Critique (Cameron)

The "myth of Mars and Venus": claims of difference are exaggerated; within-group variation is large; gender is performed, not fixed (Butler).

Language and power (Fairclough and institutional discourse)

Instrumental power

Power held and enforced through authority: law, the institution, imperatives and obligation modals ("you must"). The unequal encounter.

Influential power

Power that persuades rather than commands: advertising, politics, the media. Often hides its asymmetry.

Synthetic personalisation

Addressing a mass audience as if each reader were an individual ("just for you"), constructing a false equality between text producer and reader.

Power asymmetry in talk

Who controls turn-taking, topic, naming and questioning (courtroom, classroom, interview) reveals institutional power in discourse.

Region, social group, occupation and prestige

Accent and dialect

Accent is pronunciation; dialect is lexis and grammar. Received Pronunciation and Standard English carry overt prestige; regional varieties often carry covert prestige.

Labov

Variationist studies (department stores, Martha's Vineyard) link variants to class and identity; speakers shift style by audience and self-monitoring.

Attitudes

Matched-guise studies (Giles) show accents trigger judgements of status and warmth. Accent prejudice is social, not linguistic.

Occupation and group

Occupational registers and jargon build in-group identity and exclude outsiders; sociolect marks membership of a social group.
