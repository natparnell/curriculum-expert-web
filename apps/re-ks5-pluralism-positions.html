Religious Education • KS5 • A Level / Philosophy & Ethics

Pluralism Positions

Teacher aim: Students place religious responses to other faiths on the exclusivist–inclusivist–pluralist spectrum, then stress-test each model against real interfaith scenarios, evaluating strengths and tensions. Covers study of religion, developments in thought, and religion, society and dialogue.

1Map the positions

Drag each thinker or statement to where it belongs on the spectrum. The scale runs from exclusivism (salvation only through one tradition) through inclusivism (one tradition is normative, but others may share in its truth) to pluralism (many traditions are valid paths). Use a mouse, finger, or the keyboard (Tab to a token, then arrow keys to move it).

2Stress-test the models

Pick an interfaith scenario, then judge how well each model copes with it. There is no single right answer for every cell, but some judgements fit a model far better than others. The app responds to the combination of scenario and model, not just a generic reveal.

3Read the text critically

The same New Testament verses are quoted by exclusivists and inclusivists alike. The position you hold shapes how you read the text. Expand each verse to see how the wording can be pulled in different directions.

John 14:6 – "the way, the truth, the life"
"I am the way and the truth and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me." John 14:6 (NIV)

Exclusivist reading: "Except through me" is read as a closed gate: explicit faith in Christ is the only route to God.

Inclusivist reading: Salvation is through Christ, but Christ's saving work may reach those who never named him. The verse names the means, not the conscious requirement.

Critical question: Is the verse making a metaphysical claim about how salvation works, or a pastoral claim addressed to the disciples in context (John 14 as the farewell discourse)? Genre and audience change the weight of "no one".

Acts 4:12 – "no other name"
"Salvation is found in no one else, for there is no other name under heaven given to mankind by which we must be saved." Acts 4:12 (NIV)

Exclusivist reading: A direct statement that salvation requires the name of Jesus, supporting a narrow gate.

Pluralist reading: Hick would argue this is the confessional language of a first-century community describing its experience of the Real, not a literal census of who is saved.

Critical question: Does "must be saved" describe a universal mechanism, or the conviction of Peter speaking to the Sanhedrin? Reading critically means weighing the speaker, setting and purpose, not only the words.

Acts 17:23 – "to an unknown god"
"For as I walked around and looked carefully at your objects of worship, I even found an altar with this inscription: to an unknown god. So you are ignorant of the very thing you worship, and this is what I am going to proclaim to you." Acts 17:23 (NIV)

Inclusivist reading: Paul affirms that the Athenians already worship the true God, if unknowingly. Other religion contains genuine, if incomplete, awareness of God.

Exclusivist reading: "You are ignorant" stresses that natural religion is insufficient and must be completed by the gospel.

Critical question: Is the Areopagus speech a model for dialogue (start where the other stands), or a model for mission (correct the other's error)? The same passage funds both.
