Harmony and Counterpoint Lab

KS5 Music · Advanced harmony, counterpoint and analytical theory

Teaching aim: Students realise a figured bass and write first-species counterpoint against a cantus firmus, then hear it played back and receive instant, rule-by-rule feedback on voice leading (parallel fifths and octaves, leaps, dissonance treatment and cadence). Built for A-level harmony work and exam-board chorale and counterpoint tasks.

1. Realise the figured bass

A bass line and its figures are given. For each chord, choose the soprano, alto and tenor notes so the realisation spells the figured chord, follows good four-part voice leading, and ends with a strong cadence. Click a note head on the stave (or use the keyboard) to raise or lower it.

How to play and keyboard controls

Tab moves between the editable upper voices (soprano, alto, tenor) chord by chord. With a note focused, press Up / Down to move it by a step in the key, Enter to hear that single note, and P to play the whole exercise. The bass is fixed (it is the given line). Figures appear under each bass note.

2. Write first-species counterpoint

A cantus firmus is fixed in the lower voice. Write one note against each, note against note (first species), in the upper voice. The checker marks each interval as consonant or dissonant and applies the classic species rules: begin and end on a perfect consonance, no parallel or direct (hidden) fifths and octaves, prefer contrary and oblique motion, no large or repeated leaps, and approach the final by step.

First-species rules at a glance

Consonances allowed between the parts: unison, 3rd, 5th, 6th and octave (and a 10th, a compound 3rd). The 4th is treated as a dissonance between two voices in strict counterpoint. Open with a perfect consonance, close with an octave or unison approached by step, and never move into a perfect consonance by parallel or similar motion.

Voice-leading rule reference

The checker tests these rules. Use this table when interpreting the feedback messages.

RuleWhat the checker looks forWhy it matters at A-level
Parallel 5ths / 8vesTwo voices a perfect 5th or octave apart that move in the same direction keeping that interval.The cardinal banned parallel in chorale and species writing; loses independence of the parts.
Direct (hidden) 5ths / 8vesOuter voices reaching a perfect 5th or octave by similar motion with a leap in the top voice.Marked down in chorale harmonisation; exposes a weak approach to a perfect interval.
Spelling the chordEach upper voice belongs to the chord implied by the bass and its figure.A correct realisation must contain the right chord tones (root, 3rd, 5th, 7th as figured).
Voice spacingGaps larger than an octave between adjacent upper voices (S-A, A-T).Standard four-part spacing keeps an even, singable texture.
Voice crossing / overlapAn upper voice dipping below the voice beneath it, or below the bass.Crossing muddies the texture and is penalised in exam mark schemes.
Leading noteThe leading note (degree 7) at a perfect cadence rising to the tonic.The expected resolution at a strong cadence; a frozen or falling leading note is weak.
LeapsMelodic leaps larger than a 6th (other than an octave) in any single voice.Smooth, mostly stepwise lines are rewarded; large leaps need care.
Dissonance (species)Any non-consonant vertical interval in first species; the 4th counts as a dissonance.First species is strictly note-against-note consonance only.
Contrary motionProportion of moves where the two parts move in opposite directions.Contrary and oblique motion give the most independent counterpoint.
Final approachThe counterpoint reaching the final perfect consonance by step.The cadential close of a species line should resolve stepwise.
