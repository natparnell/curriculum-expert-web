Core exercise physiology (OCR H555)

Cardiovascular system

The cardiac cycle is the sequence of diastole (filling) and systole (contraction) of the atria and ventricles. It is coordinated by the conduction system: the sino-atrial node (the pacemaker) fires an impulse across the atria, the atrio-ventricular node delays it, then the bundle of His and Purkinje fibres spread it through the ventricles, producing a coordinated contraction.

Starling's law of the heart states that the greater the venous return, the greater the stretch of the ventricle wall and so the greater the force of contraction and stroke volume. Cardiac output is given by Q = SV × HR (cardiac output equals stroke volume multiplied by heart rate). During exercise, vascular shunting redistributes blood from the gut, kidneys and skin to working muscle. Over prolonged exercise in the heat, cardiovascular drift occurs: heart rate climbs while stroke volume falls, as plasma volume drops through sweating and blood is diverted to the skin for cooling.

Respiratory system

Breathing mechanics rely on pressure gradients. On inspiration the diaphragm and external intercostals contract, the thoracic cavity enlarges and pressure falls, drawing air in. Gaseous exchange at the alveoli and at the muscle is driven by partial pressure gradients: oxygen diffuses from high partial pressure in the alveoli to lower partial pressure in the blood, and carbon dioxide diffuses the other way. Exercise increases tidal volume and breathing frequency, raising minute ventilation, while residual volume stays roughly constant.

Neuromuscular system

A motor unit is a single motor neurone and all the muscle fibres it stimulates. By the all-or-none law, a motor unit either contracts fully or not at all; greater force comes from recruiting more units and from wave summation, not from a partial twitch. Proprioceptors such as the muscle spindle detect changes in muscle length and trigger the stretch reflex, a protective contraction that resists overstretching. This is why a fast eccentric pre-stretch can enhance a subsequent powerful concentric contraction.

OCR-style exam questions (click to reveal model answers)
1. Explain how the Bohr shift helps oxygen delivery to working muscle during exercise. (4 marks)
  • Exercising muscle produces more carbon dioxide, raising PCO₂ and lowering pH (more acidic).
  • Muscle temperature also rises during exercise.
  • These conditions shift the oxyhaemoglobin dissociation curve to the right, reducing haemoglobin's affinity for oxygen.
  • So at any given PO₂ the saturation is lower, meaning more oxygen is released (unloaded) to the working muscle exactly where it is needed.
2. Using Q = SV × HR, describe how cardiac output increases during exercise. (3 marks)
  • Heart rate rises due to sympathetic stimulation from the cardiac control centre.
  • Stroke volume rises through greater venous return and Starling's law (greater stretch gives greater contraction force).
  • Because Q is the product of the two, cardiac output increases from about 5 L/min at rest toward 20 to 30 L/min at maximal exercise.
3. Explain vascular shunting and why it is important during exercise. (3 marks)
  • Vascular shunting is the redistribution of blood flow controlled by the vasomotor centre.
  • Vasoconstriction of arterioles to the gut, kidneys and other inactive organs reduces flow there, while vasodilation to working muscle increases flow.
  • This ensures the muscle receives more oxygen and nutrients and has waste products such as carbon dioxide and lactate removed.
4. A coach has a sprinter and a marathon runner. Identify the dominant muscle fibre type for each and justify your choice. (4 marks)
  • Sprinter: dominant Type IIx (fast glycolytic) fibres. They contract very fast and produce very high force using the ATP-PC and anaerobic systems, ideal for short maximal efforts, though they fatigue quickly.
  • Marathon runner: dominant Type I (slow oxidative) fibres. They are highly fatigue resistant with many mitochondria, capillaries and myoglobin for aerobic energy production over long durations.
5. Explain the role of the muscle spindle in the stretch reflex. (3 marks)
  • Muscle spindles are proprioceptors that detect the rate and degree of change in muscle length.
  • When a muscle is stretched rapidly, the spindle sends a signal that triggers a reflex contraction of that same muscle.
  • This stretch reflex protects the muscle from overstretching and injury, and can be used in PNF stretching and plyometrics.
6. Explain cardiovascular drift during prolonged exercise. (3 marks)
  • During prolonged sub-maximal exercise, especially in the heat, sweating reduces plasma volume.
  • Blood is also diverted to the skin for cooling, reducing venous return and so stroke volume.
  • To maintain cardiac output, heart rate rises (drifts upward) over time even though intensity is unchanged.
