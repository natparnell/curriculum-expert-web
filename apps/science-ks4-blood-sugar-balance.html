Teacher and pupil notes (curriculum links)

Homeostasis is keeping a stable internal environment despite changes outside or inside the body. All control systems share three parts: a receptor (detects change), a coordination centre (processes information), and an effector (carries out the response).

  • Set point: blood glucose is held near 4 to 6 mmol/L. The dashed green line marks roughly 5 mmol/L.
  • Glucose rises (after eating): the pancreas releases insulin. Insulin makes the liver and muscles take in glucose and store it as glycogen, so glucose falls.
  • Glucose falls (during exercise or fasting): the pancreas releases glucagon. Glucagon makes the liver break glycogen back into glucose, so glucose rises.
  • Negative feedback: the response always opposes the change, returning the level to the set point.
  • Type 1 diabetes: the pancreas makes little or no insulin. It is usually treated with insulin injections and by managing diet.
  • Type 2 diabetes: the body cells stop responding well to insulin (insulin resistance), so glucose stays high. It is linked to obesity and is managed by diet, exercise and sometimes medication.

Linked idea (reflex arc): like the glucose loop, a reflex has a receptor, a coordinator and an effector. The difference is speed and signal: reflexes use fast electrical impulses along neurones (stimulus, receptor, sensory neurone, relay neurone, motor neurone, effector), while glucose control uses slower chemical hormones in the blood.
