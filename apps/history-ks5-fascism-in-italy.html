▸ How historians frame this debate

Structuralists stress the long-term weaknesses of Liberal Italy: the narrow elite politics of trasformismo, the unresolved divide between an industrialising north and an agrarian, impoverished south, the alienation of Catholics after 1870, and the discrediting of the Liberal state by the costly 1911–12 Libyan war. On this reading Fascism filled a vacuum that the Liberal order had dug for itself.

The First World War is treated by many as the decisive accelerant. The "mutilated victory" (vittoria mutilata), the dislocation of demobilisation, and the biennio rosso (the "two red years" of 1919–20 with factory occupations and land seizures) frightened property-owners and the middle classes, while D'Annunzio's seizure of Fiume in 1919 supplied a theatrical template of nationalist direct action.

Intentionalists foreground Mussolini's own agency: his shift from socialism to ultra-nationalism, his fusing of the squadristi's violence with respectable nationalism, the founding of the PNF in 1921, and the calculated bluff of the March on Rome in October 1922.

Elite collusion is the hinge that many recent accounts emphasise: Fascism could only seize power because King Victor Emmanuel III refused to sign Prime Minister Facta's decree of martial law and instead invited Mussolini to form a government. Industrialists, landowners and the army had already chosen to tolerate squadrist violence against the left.

▸ The limits of "legality"

Mussolini liked to call his a "legal revolution". Each step was given a constitutional veneer, but the architecture rested on violence the regime tolerated or directed. The pivot was the Matteotti crisis: when the murder of the socialist deputy in 1924 nearly toppled him, the opposition's withdrawal into the Aventine Secession left the field clear, and Mussolini chose escalation over retreat in his speech of 3 January 1925.

Historians debate whether 1925–26 (the Leggi Fascistissime) or 1929 (the Lateran Pacts, which won Catholic acquiescence) marks the true completion of the dictatorship. Either way, the regime never achieved the total reach of Nazi Germany: the monarchy, the Church and the conservative elites survived as independent power-centres, which is exactly why the King could still dismiss Mussolini in 1943.

▸ Applying provenance, content and tone

When you analyse a primary source, work the NOP triangle: Nature (what kind of document is it: a speech, a private letter, a propaganda poster?), Origin (who, when, with what purpose?), and Purpose (what was it trying to achieve, and for whom?). A Mussolini speech is highly valuable as evidence of how the regime presented itself, but limited as evidence of how power actually operated.

For interpretations, ask what an historian is arguing, what evidence underpins it, and how the wider debate qualifies it. Do not simply label a view "biased": explain how its emphasis (long-term weakness vs individual agency vs elite choice) shapes the claim, then reach a supported judgement.
