Reading the half-life off the graph. The half-life t½ is the time for the count to fall from N₀ to N₀/2 (and from there to N₀/4, and so on, in equal steps: that constancy is the signature of exponential decay). The simulator finds the first crossing of the dashed N₀/2 line and reports it as "from curve". Because decay is random, a finite population scatters slightly around the smooth curve, so the measured value wobbles about the theoretical t½ = ln2 / λ. Increase λ and the nuclei decay sooner: a larger decay constant means a shorter half-life. Activity A = λN is the rate of decay in becquerel (1 Bq = 1 decay per second); it falls in proportion to N, so it decays exponentially too.

Binding energy per nucleon

Interactive curve

A nucleus weighs less than its separate protons and neutrons. That missing mass, the mass defect Δm, was released as energy when the nucleus formed: E = Δmc². Divide the total binding energy by the number of nucleons and you get the binding energy per nucleon, a measure of how tightly each nucleon is held. Click any nucleus on the curve to see where its energy would go.

No nucleus selected.

Click a nucleus on the curve above (or focus the curve and press 1 to 7) to see its binding energy per nucleon and whether it gains energy by fusing or splitting.

Why both fusion and fission release energy. Nuclear reactions move nucleons towards the peak of the curve, near ⁷₆Fe at about 8.8 MeV per nucleon, the most tightly bound region. Light nuclei (left of the peak) climb the curve when they fuse: the product is more tightly bound per nucleon, so energy is released. Heavy nuclei (right of the peak) climb the curve when they split by fission: the fragments sit higher on the curve, so energy is released. Either way the products are more tightly bound, mass is lost, and that mass appears as kinetic energy of the products via E = Δmc².

Fission

A heavy nucleus such as uranium-235 absorbs a neutron and splits into two medium-mass fragments plus 2 to 3 neutrons. Those neutrons can trigger further fissions: a chain reaction. About 200 MeV is released per fission.

²³⁵U + n → ⁹²Kr + ¹⁴¹Ba + 3n

Fusion

Light nuclei such as deuterium and tritium join to form helium plus a neutron. Fusion needs enormous temperature and pressure to overcome electrostatic repulsion, but releases more energy per nucleon than fission. It powers the Sun.

²H + ³H → ⁴He + n

Ideal gas: pV = nRT

Kinetic theory model

An ideal gas is modelled as point particles in random motion, colliding elastically with the walls. Their bombardment is what we measure as pressure. The equation of state pV = nRT links pressure, volume, amount and temperature. Change the temperature or volume and watch the particles, the wall collisions, and the pressure respond.

Kinetic theory and temperature. The pressure on the walls comes from the rate of change of momentum as particles bounce off. Kinetic theory derives pV = ⅓Nm<c²>, and comparing with pV = NkT gives the key link ½m<c²> = ₃⁄₂ kT: the mean translational kinetic energy of a molecule is directly proportional to absolute temperature, with the Boltzmann constant k = 1.38 × 10⁻²³ J K⁻¹. Heat the gas and the particles speed up, hit the walls harder and more often, and the pressure rises (Gay-Lussac: p ∝ T at constant V). Squeeze the piston and the same collisions happen in less space, so pressure rises (Boyle: p ∝ 1/V at constant T).

Thermal physics essentials

Reference

Temperature measures the average kinetic energy of particles; internal energy is the total of all the random kinetic and potential energies inside a body. Heating, and changes of state, both change internal energy in ways the following relationships quantify.

Internal energy

The sum of the randomly distributed kinetic and potential energies of all the particles in a system. Raising the temperature raises the kinetic part; changing state changes the potential part (breaking bonds), which is why temperature stays constant during melting or boiling.

Absolute zero & the Kelvin scale

Absolute zero (0 K, −273 °C) is the temperature at which particles have minimum internal energy. The Kelvin scale starts here, so T/K = θ/°C + 273. A change of 1 K equals a change of 1 °C.

Specific heat capacity

The energy to raise the temperature of 1 kg by 1 K without a change of state.

E = mcΔθ

Water has a high c (4180 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹), so it stores a lot of energy per degree.

Specific latent heat

The energy to change the state of 1 kg at constant temperature, breaking or forming bonds.

E = mL

Lf for fusion (melting), Lv for vaporisation (boiling). Temperature is constant throughout.

The ideal gas laws

Boyle (const T): pV = const. Charles (const p): V/T = const. Gay-Lussac (const V): p/T = const. Combined into the equation of state:

pV = nRT

R = 8.31 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹; n is the number of moles. Equivalently pV = NkT with N molecules.

Mean kinetic energy

Kinetic theory links the microscopic and macroscopic worlds:

½m<c²> = ₃⁄₂ kT

The mean translational KE per molecule depends only on absolute temperature, not on the gas. k = 1.38 × 10⁻²³ J K⁻¹ (the Boltzmann constant = R / NA).

Worked check. How much energy turns 0.20 kg of ice at 0 °C into water at 20 °C? Melt it first: E = mLf = 0.20 × 3.34×10⁵ = 6.68×10⁴ J. Then warm the water: E = mcΔθ = 0.20 × 4180 × 20 = 1.67×10⁴ J. Total ≈ 8.4×10⁴ J. The latent step costs more than the 20 K warming, because breaking the bonds of the solid takes a large amount of energy at constant temperature.

Exam-style questions

Question 1 · Radioactivity

A radioactive source has a decay constant λ = 0.025 s⁻¹. Calculate (a) its half-life, and (b) the fraction of nuclei remaining after 60 s. [4]

(a) t½ = ln2 / λ = 0.693 / 0.025 = 27.7 s (3 s.f.).
(b) N/N₀ = e−λt = e−0.025×60 = e−1.5 = 0.223, so about 22% of the nuclei remain. (60 s is just over two half-lives, and ¼ = 25%, so 22% is sensible.)
Question 2 · Mass-energy equivalence

In a fission event the total mass of the products is 0.20 u less than the reactants. Calculate the energy released, in MeV and in joules. [3]

Using 1 u ≡ 931.5 MeV: E = 0.20 × 931.5 = 186 MeV.
In joules: 186 MeV × 1.60×10⁻¹³ J/MeV = 2.98×10⁻¹¹ J. (Equivalently E = Δmc² with Δm = 0.20 × 1.66×10⁻²⁷ kg gives the same answer.)
Question 3 · Binding energy

Explain why both the fusion of light nuclei and the fission of heavy nuclei can release energy, with reference to the binding-energy-per-nucleon curve. [4]

Binding energy per nucleon peaks near iron-56 (the most stable nuclei). Any reaction that moves nucleons towards the peak increases binding energy per nucleon, so the products are more tightly bound. Light nuclei (left of the peak) move towards it by fusing; heavy nuclei (right of the peak) move towards it by fission. In both cases the products have a greater total binding energy than the reactants, so mass is lost (Δm) and energy is released via E = Δmc².
Question 4 · Ideal gas

A fixed mass of ideal gas at 300 K and pressure 1.0 × 10⁵ Pa is heated at constant volume to 450 K. Find the new pressure, and state what happens to the mean kinetic energy of the molecules. [3]

At constant V, p/T = const, so p₂ = p₁ × T₂/T₁ = 1.0×10⁵ × 450/300 = 1.5×10⁵ Pa.
Mean KE ½m<c²> = ₃⁄₂kT is proportional to T, so it rises by the factor 450/300 = 1.5 (a 50% increase).
Question 5 · Thermal energy

A 2.0 kW kettle heats 0.50 kg of water from 20 °C to 100 °C. Taking c = 4180 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹ and ignoring losses, find the time taken. [3]

Energy needed: E = mcΔθ = 0.50 × 4180 × 80 = 1.67×10⁵ J.
Time: t = E / P = 1.67×10⁵ / 2000 = 84 s (about 1 minute 24 seconds). Real kettles take longer because of heat lost to the surroundings and the kettle body.
