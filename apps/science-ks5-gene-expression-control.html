Control of Gene Expression

Teaching aim: to understand how gene expression is regulated at transcriptional, post-transcriptional and post-translational levels, using the lac operon as a model of inducible control, and how mutations alter the polypeptide produced.

Three levels of regulation

Transcription to protein

Gene expression can be controlled at several points between the gene and the working protein. OCR A groups these into transcriptional, post-transcriptional and post-translational control.

1. Transcriptional

  • Transcription factors bind to promoter regions to switch transcription on or off.
  • Activators increase the rate; repressors (as in the lac operon) decrease it.
  • Oestrogen is a steroid hormone that enters the cell and acts as a transcription factor by binding a receptor.
  • Controls whether mRNA is made at all.

2. Post-transcriptional

  • Pre-mRNA is edited: introns spliced out, exons joined.
  • A 5' cap and 3' poly-A tail are added to stabilise mRNA.
  • Alternative splicing lets one gene yield several proteins.
  • Controls the mature mRNA available for translation.

3. Post-translational

  • Modification of the polypeptide after it is made.
  • Adding non-protein groups, folding, or cutting (e.g. proinsulin to insulin).
  • Activation by cyclic AMP changing protein shape (e.g. enzyme activation).
  • Controls the final active protein.
Transcription factors are proteins that move from the cytoplasm into the nucleus and bind to specific DNA sequences near a gene. By stabilising or blocking RNA polymerase at the promoter, they determine whether that gene is transcribed. The lac repressor is a bacterial example of a transcription factor that works by repression.

Epigenetics, homeobox genes & apoptosis

Development & control

Epigenetics

Heritable changes in gene function without a change in the DNA base sequence. The two main mechanisms are increased methylation of DNA (adding methyl groups to cytosine, which prevents transcription factors binding and switches the gene off) and decreased acetylation of histones (which makes chromatin condense, so genes cannot be transcribed). These marks can respond to the environment and may be passed to daughter cells.

Homeobox / Hox genes

A group of genes containing a conserved homeobox sequence (180 base pairs) coding for a homeodomain that binds DNA. The proteins act as transcription factors regulating the body plan, controlling the development of the correct anatomical features in the correct positions along the head-to-tail axis. They are highly conserved across animals, plants and fungi.

Apoptosis

Programmed cell death. Enzymes break down the cell cytoskeleton; the cell shrinks and the membrane blebs; chromatin condenses and DNA breaks into fragments; vesicles are engulfed by phagocytes with no release of hydrolytic enzymes. It removes unwanted cells during development (e.g. between fingers) and is controlled by genes responding to internal and external signals.

Linking it together

Mitosis, apoptosis and the regulated expression of homeobox genes together shape the developing organism. Errors in this control, including blocked apoptosis or epigenetic silencing of tumour-suppressor genes, can contribute to tumour formation, showing why precise control of gene expression matters.

Exam-style questions

Reveal answers

A mutation changes a single base in a structural gene, but the polypeptide produced is unchanged. Name this type of mutation and explain why the polypeptide is unchanged. [2]

Silent (synonymous) substitution.
  • The genetic code is degenerate: more than one codon codes for the same amino acid.
  • The new codon still codes for the same amino acid, so the primary structure (sequence of amino acids) is unchanged.

Explain why an insertion or deletion of one base usually has a much greater effect on the polypeptide than a substitution. [3]

  • An insertion or deletion causes a frameshift: all codons downstream of the mutation are read in a different reading frame.
  • Many amino acids are therefore changed, so a very different (usually non-functional) polypeptide is produced.
  • A substitution changes at most one codon, so usually only one amino acid (or none, if silent) is affected.

Describe the role of the repressor and the operator in switching the lac operon off when no lactose is present. [3]

  • The regulatory gene (lacI) is transcribed and translated to produce the repressor protein.
  • In the absence of lactose, the repressor binds to the operator region.
  • This blocks RNA polymerase from binding/moving from the promoter, so the structural genes are not transcribed and the enzymes are not made.

When lactose is added to E. coli, the structural genes are expressed. Explain how lactose acts as an inducer. [3]

  • Some lactose is converted to allolactose, which binds to the repressor protein.
  • This changes the shape (tertiary structure) of the repressor so it can no longer bind the operator.
  • The operator is free, so RNA polymerase can transcribe the structural genes (lacZ, lacY, lacA), producing the enzymes to metabolise lactose.

Even when lactose is present, E. coli transcribes the lac structural genes only slowly if glucose is also abundant. Explain why. [2]

  • Glucose is the preferred respiratory substrate, so it is energetically wasteful to make lactose-metabolising enzymes when glucose is available.
  • High glucose keeps cyclic AMP (cAMP) levels low; without cAMP the activator (CAP/CRP) cannot promote efficient binding of RNA polymerase, so transcription is slow. This is described as the operon being only weakly expressed.

Define epigenetics and name one mechanism by which a gene can be epigenetically switched off. [2]

  • Epigenetics: heritable changes in gene function (expression) without a change in the DNA base sequence.
  • One mechanism: increased methylation of DNA (prevents transcription factors binding) or decreased acetylation of histones (chromatin condenses, blocking transcription).
