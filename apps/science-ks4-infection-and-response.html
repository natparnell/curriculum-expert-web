Herd immunity: when a large enough fraction of a population is immune (through vaccination or recovery), an infected person can rarely find a susceptible contact, so each case produces fewer than one new case and the outbreak fizzles out. This protects people who cannot be vaccinated, such as newborns or those who are immunocompromised. The threshold rises with the pathogen's infectiousness (R₀): measles, with R₀ around 15, needs roughly 95% coverage.

Primary vs secondary immune response

Phagocytosis & antibody lock-and-key

White blood cells defend the body three ways: phagocytes engulf and digest pathogens (phagocytosis); lymphocytes produce antibodies with a shape complementary to a specific antigen (a lock-and-key fit) and produce antitoxins that neutralise toxins.

Communicable diseases and their pathogens

Pathogens are microorganisms that cause communicable (infectious) disease. They spread by direct contact, water, air or vectors, and may produce toxins or damage tissues directly.

Non-specific human defences (first line): the skin acts as a physical barrier and produces antimicrobial secretions; the nose has hairs and mucus to trap particles; the trachea and bronchi secrete mucus and have cilia that waft trapped pathogens away; the stomach produces hydrochloric acid that kills swallowed pathogens.

Vaccination & antibiotics

A vaccine introduces small quantities of dead or inactive forms of a pathogen (carrying its antigens) into the body. Lymphocytes respond by producing antibodies. If the live pathogen later enters, the body produces the correct antibodies rapidly and in large numbers (the secondary response), so the person is unlikely to fall ill.

Antibiotics (such as penicillin) kill bacteria inside the body and have greatly reduced deaths from bacterial disease. They do not kill viruses, which live inside cells, so painkillers only treat viral symptoms. Overuse and failure to complete courses select for antibiotic-resistant strains such as MRSA. Resistance spreads because resistant bacteria survive, reproduce and pass on the resistance gene; development of new antibiotics is slow and costly.

Developing new drugs

New medical drugs are tested and trialled before use to check three things:

  • Toxicity (is it harmful?)
  • Efficacy (does it work?)
  • Dose (the right amount to give)

Preclinical testing uses cells, tissues and live animals. Clinical trials use healthy volunteers (very low doses to check safety) then patients to find the optimum dose. In a double-blind trial some patients receive a placebo, and neither the patient nor the doctor knows who has the real drug until the results are analysed, removing bias. Results are peer reviewed before publication.

Higher / triple-science extras

Monoclonal antibodies

Monoclonal antibodies are produced from a single clone of cells and are specific to one binding site on one protein antigen. A mouse lymphocyte is fused with a tumour cell to make a hybridoma, which divides rapidly and secretes the antibody. Uses include pregnancy tests (binding to the hormone HCG), measuring hormone levels, identifying pathogens, research, and delivering drugs or radioactive markers to specific cells such as cancer cells. They caused more side effects than first hoped, so they are not yet as widely used as predicted.

Detecting plant disease

Plant diseases can be detected by visible symptoms (stunted growth, spots on leaves, areas of decay, discolouration, malformed stems or leaves, pests) and by lab tests. Identification can use a gardening manual or website, take infected plant to a laboratory to identify the pathogen, or use a testing kit containing monoclonal antibodies. Plants need mineral ions: a lack of nitrate causes stunted growth (protein synthesis) and a lack of magnesium causes chlorosis (no chlorophyll). Plants defend themselves with physical barriers (cellulose cell walls, waxy cuticle, bark), chemical defences (antibacterial chemicals, poisons) and mechanical adaptations (thorns, hairs, mimicry).

Exam-style questions

Attempt each question, then reveal a model answer with the marking points an examiner would expect.

1. Explain how vaccinating a large proportion of a population can protect individuals who have not been vaccinated. [3 marks]
  • Vaccinated people are immune, so they do not catch or carry the pathogen.
  • The pathogen cannot spread easily from person to person because few susceptible contacts remain.
  • An infected person passes it to fewer than one new person, so the disease does not spread / the outbreak dies out (herd immunity), protecting the unvaccinated.
2. The secondary immune response is faster and larger than the primary response. Explain why this means a vaccinated person rarely becomes ill on later exposure. [3 marks]
  • The first exposure (vaccine) causes lymphocytes to produce memory cells specific to the antigen.
  • On re-exposure these memory cells produce the correct antibodies more quickly and in much greater quantity.
  • The pathogen is destroyed before it can multiply enough to cause symptoms, so the person does not become ill.
3. Antibiotics have no effect on diseases caused by viruses, such as measles. Suggest why. [2 marks]
  • Antibiotics kill bacteria but viruses are not bacteria / have a different structure.
  • Viruses live and reproduce inside the body's cells, so a drug that killed the virus would be likely to damage the cells too.
4. Describe how the overuse of antibiotics can lead to the development of antibiotic-resistant strains of bacteria. [4 marks]
  • Random mutations occur in bacteria; some make a bacterium resistant to the antibiotic.
  • When the antibiotic is used, non-resistant bacteria are killed but resistant ones survive (natural selection).
  • The resistant bacteria reproduce and pass on the resistance allele/gene to offspring.
  • Over time the resistant strain becomes more common; overuse and not completing courses speed this up.
5. In a clinical trial a new drug is tested using a double-blind design with a placebo. Explain why a placebo and the double-blind method are used. [3 marks]
  • The placebo (a substance with no active drug) acts as a control / comparison, so any effect of the real drug can be shown.
  • Neither the patient nor the doctor knows who receives the real drug or placebo.
  • This prevents bias in how patients report symptoms and how doctors assess them, making the results valid/reliable.
6. Monoclonal antibodies can be used to detect a specific molecule. Explain why a monoclonal antibody binds to only one type of antigen. [2 marks]
  • It is produced from a single clone of cells, so all the antibodies are identical with the same shaped binding site.
  • The binding site is complementary in shape to one specific antigen only (lock-and-key), so it binds to that antigen alone.
