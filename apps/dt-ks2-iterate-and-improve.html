Iterate and Improve

Build a marble-run delivery cart, test it against the design spec, then improve it run by run.

Teacher aim: Pupils evaluate a prototype against a design specification, identify what failed and why, then make one purposeful change at a time and re-test, building a visible iterative-improvement timeline.
Design & Technology KS2 Evaluate strand Iterative design

The brief

Toy designer "Brookmoor Workshop" needs a small cart to carry one marble safely down a sloped track and stop gently at the bottom. Your job is to make a prototype that passes every line of the spec.

Design specification

CriterionIt passes when…
Delivers the marbleThe cart reaches the end of the track.
Stops gentlyArrival speed is calm, not a crash.
Stays on the trackThe cart does not tip over on the bends.
Light enough to liftTotal weight is no more than 7 units.
AffordableMaterial cost is no more than 9 coins.

    Designers who changed things

    Real designers rarely got it right first time. They tested, failed, learned and improved, just like you.

    James Dyson
    Built 5,127 prototype vacuum cleaners before one worked the way he wanted.
    Iteration: each "failure" told him what to change next.
    The Wright Brothers
    Tested over 200 wing shapes in a home-made wind tunnel before flying in 1903.
    Evaluate: they measured results, then improved the design.
    Mary Anderson
    Designed the windscreen wiper after seeing drivers struggle in the rain.
    Brief: a real problem led to a clear specification.
    Teacher notes and curriculum links
    • NC strand (KS2 Evaluate): investigate and analyse a range of existing products; evaluate their ideas and products against their own design criteria; understand how key events and individuals in design and technology have helped shape the world.
    • Core idea: testing a prototype against the spec, logging what failed, then changing one variable and re-testing. The timeline makes the iterative loop visible.
    • Talk prompts: "Which criterion failed and why?" "What single change will you make next, and what do you predict will happen?" "Did your change fix one thing but break another? (a trade-off)"
    • Trade-offs to spot: bigger wheels mean speed but tipping; ballast adds stability but weight; the brake helps stopping but costs coins and weight.
    • Stretch: ask pupils to reach a full pass in the fewest versions, then explain their reasoning. Compare to Dyson's 5,127 prototypes.
