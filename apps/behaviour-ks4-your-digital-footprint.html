Stage 1: You are the recruiter

Imagine you run admissions at a sixth-form college, or you hire for a Saturday job. You search a candidate's public profile. For each post, decide how it lands: a green flag (helps them), an amber flag (think twice) or a red flag (a real problem). You get specific feedback every time.

Stage 2: Now you advise Jordan

Jordan has heard the feedback and wants to fix things before applying to college. For each problem post, choose the smartest move. Deleting is not always the best answer, and some "fixes" make things worse.

Stage 3: Lock down the account

A footprint is not only old posts. It is also what you allow now. Drag each setting to the right level. Watch the exposure meter respond. Aim for a profile that is open enough to be useful, private enough to be safe.

Five things to take away

  • Public means permanent. Screenshots outlive deletions. Post as if a future employer is already reading.
  • Context collapses online. A joke for your mates can read very differently to a stranger judging you.
  • Images carry data. Uniforms, house numbers, geotags and reflections can reveal where you live or study.
  • Scams target reach, not just money. "Free" giveaways, "is this you in this video?" links and DMs from strangers are bait.
  • Privacy is a habit, not a one-off. Review who can see, tag and message you every few months.
