    Where Hot Deserts Are, and Why

    Why here? Most hot deserts sit in two bands at about 30° north and 30° south. Warm air rises at the Equator, loses its moisture as rain, then sinks back down at these latitudes. This dry, sinking air (high pressure) stops clouds forming, so almost no rain falls. Tap a desert to locate it.

    Hot Desert vs Tropical Rainforest

    FeatureHot desertTropical rainforest
    LocationAround 30° N and S (high pressure)Around the Equator (0°, low pressure)
    RainfallVery low and unreliable (under 250mm/yr)Very high (over 2,000mm/yr)
    TemperatureHot days, cold nights, big daily rangeHot and steady all year, small range
    PlantsSparse, drought-resistant (cacti, thorns)Dense, layered, lush evergreen
    BiodiversityLow number of speciesExtremely high biodiversity
    SoilThin, sandy or stony, little organic matterThin but recycled fast by warm, wet conditions

    How People Use and Live in Hot Deserts

    🐔Pastoral farming
    Nomadic herders such as the Tuareg and Bedouin move livestock (goats, sheep, camels) between scarce water sources.
    💧Irrigated farming
    Where groundwater or rivers reach the desert, water is pumped to grow crops, for example dates at oases or wheat under pivot irrigation.
    ⚡Energy and minerals
    Clear skies make deserts ideal for solar power, and they hold oil, gas and minerals (the Sahara, the Arabian desert).
    🏖Tourism and cities
    Spectacular landscapes draw visitors, and large cities (such as Las Vegas and Dubai) grow where water can be supplied.
    Challenge: living in a hot desert means coping with extreme heat, water shortages and the risk of soil being exhausted or land turning to desert (desertification).
