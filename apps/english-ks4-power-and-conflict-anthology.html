Power and Conflict: building a comparative response

Teaching aim: students learn to compare two poems from the AQA Power and Conflict cluster, weighing the poets' methods, structure, language and viewpoints, and how context shapes meaning, then build a defensible thesis and a comparative paragraph.

Assessment objectives. The Section B comparison question rewards AO1 (a clear, well-supported argument with textual references), AO2 (analysis of the poets' methods and their effects, using subject terminology) and AO3 (the relationship between the poems and the contexts in which they were written). The skill is to compare the methods, not to write about each poem in turn.

How the comparison question is marked ▶

In the AQA paper one poem is named and printed; you choose a second from the cluster. The examiner wants a comparison run through the whole answer, not two separate descriptions joined at the end. Aim to make every paragraph do all three jobs:

  • AO1Argue a clear point about how each poem treats the theme, and support it with apt, embedded references.
  • AO2Name and analyse the methods (form, structure, language, imagery) and explain their effect on the reader.
  • AO3Link the poems to the context that shaped them, but only where it deepens the comparison.

Use comparative connectives (similarly, whereas, in contrast, equally) so the comparison is explicit. This builder assembles a grid, a thesis and a model paragraph to get you started; the analysis must then become your own.
