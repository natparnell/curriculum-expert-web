Religious Education • Key Stage 5 • Philosophy of Religion

Is This Experience Veridical?

Apply Swinburne's principles of credulity and testimony to case studies of numinous, mystical and conversion experiences, then weigh the naturalistic counter-explanations that might defeat them.

Teacher aim: Pupils evaluate whether reported religious experiences provide evidence for God, balancing prima facie support (credulity, testimony) against defeaters (drugs, deception, prior unreliability, conflicting claims). Strand: religious experience and religious language.

1 The two principles you will use

Richard Swinburne argued that, in the absence of special considerations, experiences should be trusted. These are rational starting points, not guarantees.

Principle of Credulity

If it seems to a person that X is present, then probably X is present, unless there is good reason to doubt it. How things seem is prima facie evidence for how they are.

Principle of Testimony

In the absence of special considerations, the experiences of others are probably as they report them. We should normally believe what people sincerely tell us.

Swinburne lists four defeaters (special considerations) that weaken a report: (a) the subject is unreliable, (b) the experience occurred under conditions known to mislead, (c) the thing claimed is unlikely on other evidence, (d) the experience can be fully explained without it being veridical. Your job: judge how strongly each case survives its defeaters.
