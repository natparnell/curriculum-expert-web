Particle Model of Matter

Teaching aim: heat a substance from ice to steam at constant power and watch the heating curve build live, so you can link the two temperature plateaus to changes of state, and compute the energy at each stage with E = m c Δθ and E = m L. Then measure density and test yourself.

Heating experiment

Selecting a substance changes the melting point, boiling point, specific heat capacities and latent heats used in the simulation.

What you are watching

The heater adds E = P × t joules every second at constant power. While the temperature rises the energy goes to particle kinetic energy: E = m c Δθ.

During melting and boiling the temperature holds flat (a plateau). The energy now breaks bonds between particles, raising potential energy not kinetic energy: E = m L. Mass is conserved throughout, so the change of state is a physical change.

Specific heat capacity vs specific latent heat

Specific heat capacity c is the energy to raise 1 kg by 1 °C with no change of state: E = m c Δθ. It governs the sloped sections.

Specific latent heat L is the energy to change the state of 1 kg with no change in temperature: E = m L. Fusion (L_f) is melting or freezing, vaporisation (L_v) is boiling or condensing. It governs the flat plateaus.

Particle arrangement in the three states

Solid

Regular fixed lattice. Particles touch and vibrate. Fixed shape and volume. Most dense.

Liquid

Close together but irregular. Particles slide past each other. Fixed volume, takes the shape of its container.

Gas

Far apart, random rapid motion. Fills the container. Least dense. Particles hitting walls cause pressure.

Gas temperature and pressure

Raising the temperature of a gas increases the average kinetic energy of its particles, so they move faster. In a sealed container they hit the walls harder and more often, so the pressure rises.

For a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature (Higher tier), squeezing it into a smaller volume packs the collisions closer together, so pressure rises: p V = constant.
