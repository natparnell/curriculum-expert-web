◆ Shakespeare's methods (AO2)

Form, structure and language are deliberate. Name the method, then explain the effect: never label and move on.

Iambic pentameter

Ten-syllable lines of unstressed-stressed feet are the verse of noble, ordered speech. When Macbeth's lines fracture or share across speakers, the disorder mirrors his unravelling mind.

Listen for the steady five-beat pulse of the kingly characters.

Trochaic chant

The witches break the pattern: stressed-unstressed feet in short seven-syllable lines set them apart as unnatural and incantatory, their rhyme working like a spell.

“Double, double toil and trouble”

Blood imagery

Blood tracks guilt that cannot be washed away. It moves from the soldier's honour to a stain that spreads across both Macbeth and his wife, turning seas red.

“Will all great Neptune's ocean wash this blood”

Darkness imagery

Night and the blotting-out of light accompany every evil deed. Characters summon darkness to hide their intentions from heaven and from themselves.

“Come, thick night”

Dramatic irony & equivocation

The audience knows what characters do not. The witches' prophecies are true yet misleading, and the play warns against trusting fair-seeming words.

“Fair is foul, and foul is fair”

Soliloquy

Soliloquies open Macbeth's interior conflict directly to the audience, so we judge the gap between his private terror and his public actions.

The dagger speech stages a mind already turning to murder.

◆ Jacobean context (AO3)

Context earns marks only when it is woven into analysis of the text, never bolted on as a fact.

1603 onward

James I and patronage

Written for a king who claimed descent from Banquo and who feared assassination. Banquo's noble line and Macbeth's ruin flatter and reassure the new monarch.

Belief

Divine right of kings

Kings were thought to be appointed by God. Killing an anointed king, Duncan, is not just murder but a sin against the cosmic order, which is why nature itself rebels.

1605

The Gunpowder Plot

A recent attempt to blow up King and Parliament made regicide and equivocation urgent topics. The Porter's talk of an equivocator would have landed sharply with the first audiences.

Belief

Witchcraft

James I wrote Daemonologie and witch trials were real. The witches would read as a genuine, feared threat that could corrupt a soul, not as mere fantasy.

Belief

The Great Chain of Being

Everything had its ordained place, from God to king to subject. Macbeth's overreaching ambition violates this hierarchy, unleashing chaos until rightful order is restored.

Society

Gender and masculinity

Manhood was tied to courage and dominance. Lady Macbeth weaponises this, equating murder with manliness and asking to be “unsexed” to act.

◆ From the printed extract to the whole play

AQA prints a short extract and asks you to write about it and the play as a whole. Use this five-move plan.

1

Decode the focus

Underline the focus word in the question, for example ambition or the supernatural. Everything you write must serve it. Make a one-line thesis before you start.

2

Mine the extract closely

Find two or three short quotations in the printed extract. Zoom in on method and effect: word choice, verse, imagery. This is your AO2-rich opening.

3

Track the idea across the play

Show how the same theme develops elsewhere, earlier or later. Move forwards and backwards in the plot to prove a whole-play argument. Use the theme tracker to plan this.

4

Weave context purposefully

Bring in one or two contextual links that sharpen the point, such as divine right or witchcraft beliefs. Embed it in a sentence about meaning, never as a standalone fact.

5

Conclude on Shakespeare's intent

End on what Shakespeare wants the audience to think or feel, and why it mattered then. A confident final judgement lifts AO1.
