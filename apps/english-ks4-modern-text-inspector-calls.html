An Inspector Calls: The Modern Text Essay Workshop

Teaching aim: plan a thesis-led whole-text essay on An Inspector Calls by J.B. Priestley, weaving Priestley's dramatic methods (AO2) and historical context (AO3) into a coherent argument (AO1), as required by the AQA modern-text question.

How the modern-text question works, and how to use this planner

In AQA GCSE English Literature Paper 2, Section A, you answer one essay from a choice of two questions on your modern text. There is no extract: you write about the whole play from memory.

The three assessment objectives

  • AO1: a clear, well-argued response that uses the text to support ideas. This is your thesis and your selection of relevant points and quotations.
  • AO2: analysis of the methods Priestley uses, with subject terminology. For a play this means dramatic methods: structure, dramatic irony, stage directions, lighting, dialogue, the Inspector as a device.
  • AO3: the relationship between the text and the contexts in which it was written and set: 1912 versus 1945, Priestley's socialism, the World Wars, the post-war welfare-state mood.

AO4 (vocabulary, spelling, punctuation and grammar) is also assessed on this question, but is not modelled here.

Using the planner

Pick a question, write a thesis that answers it directly, then build a plan by adding points from the bank into the introduction, three developed paragraphs and a conclusion. The coverage readout checks you have woven AO1, AO2 and AO3 together rather than splitting them.

1.Choose your exam question

Questions are written in the AQA style. Some target a theme, some a character. Choosing changes which points are most relevant.

2.Write a thesis

A strong answer opens with a line of argument that responds to the whole question, not a definition. Try: "Priestley uses [character/method] to argue that [idea], so that a 1945 audience would [response]."

3.Point and quotation bank

Each card is one idea with a short key quotation, the dramatic method behind it (AO2) and the relevant context (AO3). Filter by relevance to your question, then add cards to your plan.

4.Build your plan

Add points into each slot. Aim for one clear idea per paragraph, with method and context woven in, all driving your thesis.

5.Assessment-objective coverage

Live feedback on whether your plan balances argument (AO1), methods (AO2) and context (AO3).

Character-change tracker

A key marker of a sophisticated answer is judging who changes and who does not. The young Birlings learn; the older generation refuses to. Select a character to trace their arc across the three acts.

Dramatic-irony explorer

Priestley sets the play in 1912 but wrote it in 1945. Mr Birling makes confident predictions the audience knows were catastrophically wrong. This gap is dramatic irony: it undercuts Birling's authority and primes us to distrust capitalist self-confidence. Step through his claims.
