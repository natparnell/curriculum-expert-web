Three-Dimensional Work: The Construction Studio

Teaching aim: develop and refine your handling of materials in three dimensions by assembling a sculptural maquette from primitive forms. Rotate it to read it from every angle, adjust scale and proportion, test its balance and structure, and analyse how the formal elements (form, mass, space, surface, scale, balance and structure) shape the work.

Reading the elements

  • FormEach primitive is a closed three-dimensional shape: solid, with real volume.
How depth and shading are drawn

This stage has no photographs and no 3D engine. Every form is drawn from scratch with simple geometry. A flat plane gives ground; forms are placed in space using an isometric-style projection so the same shape reads differently as you rotate the stage. Each face is shaded by how much it faces the light: faces turned towards the top-left read lighter, faces turned away read darker. That tonal change is what gives a flat screen the illusion of mass and surface.

The app estimates balance by comparing where the combined mass sits against the footprint on the base. If the centre of mass drifts beyond the supporting footprint, or sits high on a narrow base, the maquette is flagged as unstable, exactly the judgement a sculptor makes before committing to a material.

Your reflection

Analyse the sculptors Carving · Modelling · Construction · Casting

Sculptors arrive at form by different routes. Carving takes material away; modelling builds it up; construction joins separate parts; casting reproduces a form in a new material. Study how each one uses materials and form, then borrow a method for your own maquette.

KS3 sketchbook & analysis prompts

  • Record: from at least three angles, draw your maquette in your sketchbook. Note where the silhouette is most interesting and where the space around the form (the negative space) does the work.
  • Refine: change one decision. Swap a form, alter its scale, or move the mass. Annotate why the new version reads better or worse against your theme.
  • Mass & space: describe in words where the mass is concentrated and how light would fall across the surface in your chosen material.
  • Balance & structure: if a real sculptor built this, what would hold it up? Identify the weakest join and suggest how to strengthen it.
  • Analyse a sculptor: pick one artist from the gallery. Explain how their method (carving, modelling, construction or casting) suits the forms they make, and what you can take from it.
