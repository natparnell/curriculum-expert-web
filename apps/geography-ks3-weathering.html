Rocks & Weathering

Teaching aim: understand how the three rock types differ, how rock is broken down in place by mechanical, chemical and biological weathering, and how this differs from erosion, by running a freeze-thaw simulator and sorting real examples.

1The three rock types

All rock falls into three groups, formed in different ways. Knowing how a rock formed helps explain how easily it weathers.

Formed from heat

Igneous

Made when molten magma or lava cools and hardens into crystals. Hard and resistant.

Examples: granite, basalt.

Formed in layers

Sedimentary

Made when sediments settle, are buried and cemented into layers over millions of years. Often softer.

Examples: limestone, sandstone, chalk.

Formed by heat & pressure

Metamorphic

Made when existing rock is changed by intense heat and pressure deep underground. Usually hard.

Examples: marble (from limestone), slate (from clay).

3The three types of weathering

Weathering is the breakdown of rock in situ (where it sits), without it being moved. There are three kinds.

Mechanical (physical)

The rock is broken into smaller pieces without changing its chemistry. The main process in cold climates is freeze-thaw, where water gets into cracks, freezes, expands and breaks the rock. Exfoliation peels off outer layers as the rock heats and cools.

Modelled in the simulator above.

Chemical

A chemical reaction changes and dissolves the rock. Rainwater is slightly acidic, so it slowly dissolves rocks such as limestone. This reaction is called carbonation.

Example: limestone pavements and caves.

Biological

Living things break the rock down. Plant roots grow into cracks and prise them apart, and burrowing animals loosen and weaken rock and soil.

Example: tree roots splitting a paving slab.

5Weathering vs erosion

These two words are often muddled. The difference is whether the broken rock moves.

Stays put

Weathering

The breakdown of rock in place. The rock is broken or dissolved but the pieces are not carried away.

  • Happens in situ (where the rock is)
  • No transport involved
  • Freeze-thaw widening a crack is weathering
Wears away & moves

Erosion

The wearing away and transport of material by a moving force: water (rivers, sea), wind or ice (glaciers).

  • Material is removed and carried elsewhere
  • Needs a moving agent: water, wind or ice
  • A river carrying away loosened rock is erosion
The key test: if the rock is broken or dissolved but stays where it is, that is weathering. The moment the pieces are picked up and transported away, that is erosion. Weathering often happens first and prepares loose material that erosion can then move.

6From rock to soil and landscape

Weathering does more than break rock: it builds the ground we stand on.

Step one

Rock breaks down

Weathering breaks solid rock into smaller and smaller fragments and grains over very long timescales.

Step two

Soil forms

These mineral fragments mix with decayed plant and animal matter (organic material) to form soil, the thin layer plants grow in.

Step three

Landscapes are shaped

Over time, weathering and erosion together carve distinctive landscapes, from scree slopes and tors to limestone pavements.
