How the case router decides

The first question is always: is this criminal or civil?

  • Criminal law deals with offences against society, prosecuted by the state (usually the Crown Prosecution Service). It is heard first in a magistrates' court (summary and either-way offences) or the Crown Court (indictable, serious offences, with a jury). The standard of proof is beyond reasonable doubt.
  • Civil law settles disputes between people or organisations (money owed, contracts, negligence, property, family). It starts in the County Court (most everyday claims) or the High Court (large or complex claims). The standard of proof is the lower balance of probabilities.
  • Tribunals handle specialist disputes, for example an Employment Tribunal for unfair dismissal or discrimination at work.

Appeals move up the hierarchy: magistrates' decisions can go to the Crown Court; serious matters and points of law reach the Court of Appeal and finally the Supreme Court, the highest court in the United Kingdom.

3 The structure of the courts

Cases start lower down and appeals travel upwards. The criminal route is on the left, the civil route on the right; the two top courts are shared.

Supreme Court The highest court in the UK. Hears appeals on the most important points of law, criminal and civil.
↑ appeal on a point of law ↑
Court of Appeal Criminal Division and Civil Division. Hears appeals from the Crown Court and the High Court.
↑ serious appeals ↑
Crown Court Serious (indictable) crimes. Judge plus jury. Also hears appeals from magistrates.
High Court Large or complex civil claims. Judge only.
↑ cases begin here ↑
Magistrates' court Around 90 percent of criminal cases start and finish here. Less serious offences.
County Court Most everyday civil disputes: debts, contracts, personal injury, small claims.
Tribunals (specialist) Outside the main court ladder. For example the Employment Tribunal hears unfair dismissal and workplace discrimination claims.

The five aims, in plain English
  • Retribution: the offender deserves to be punished in proportion to the crime ("just deserts").
  • Deterrence: putting the offender, and others, off committing crime.
  • Rehabilitation: changing behaviour so the person does not reoffend.
  • Protection of the public: keeping a dangerous person away from society (incapacitation).
  • Reparation: the offender makes amends to the victim or community.

Sentence types range from discharges and fines, through community orders (unpaid work, curfews, treatment requirements), to custody. A youth caution diverts young people away from court.

6 The youth justice system

Children and young people (aged 10 to 17 in England and Wales) are dealt with differently.

A separate system, with a different focus

The main statutory aim of the youth justice system is to prevent offending by children and young people. Most cases are heard in the youth court (a type of magistrates' court), which is less formal and closed to the public. Only the most serious cases go to the Crown Court.

Out-of-court outcomes

Many young people receive a community resolution, youth caution or youth conditional caution instead of being charged, to avoid a criminal record where possible.

Youth sentences

These include referral orders, youth rehabilitation orders, and, as a last resort, a detention and training order. The minimum age of criminal responsibility is 10.
