RE · GCSE · AQA 8062 · Theme E: Crime & Punishment

Crime & Punishment

Teaching aim: to understand religious and non-religious teachings on crime, the causes of wrongdoing, and the aims of punishment, and to evaluate the arguments for and against capital punishment using sources of authority.

Key terms The vocabulary of the topic

AQA Religious Studies expects precise use of subject terminology. Learn these before you begin.

Glossary

Crime
An act against the law that is punishable by the state.
Sin
An act against the will of God or against a moral law; a religious idea, wider than crime.
Deterrence
Punishment intended to put people off committing crime.
Retribution
Punishment as a deserved response so the offender "pays back" for the wrong done.
Reformation
Punishment intended to change an offender's behaviour for the better.
Protection
Punishment that keeps society safe from the offender.
Forgiveness
Showing grace and ceasing to feel resentment towards a wrongdoer.
Capital punishment
The death penalty: punishing a crime by execution.

Beliefs Why people commit crime, and attitudes to law-breakers

Reasons for crime studied at GCSE include poverty, upbringing, mental illness, addiction, greed, hate, and opposition to an unjust law. Religions distinguish good and evil intentions and actions, and consider the effect of crime such as theft, murder and hate crimes on victims and society.

✚ Christianity
  • Humans are made in the image of God but are tempted to sin; intention matters as well as action."You shall not steal" / "You shall not murder" (Exodus 20).
  • Christians should hate the sin but love the sinner, and recognise causes such as poverty."Love your neighbour as yourself" (Mark 12:31).
  • Jesus showed compassion to wrongdoers and was executed alongside criminals.Parable of the lost sheep; the criminal on the cross (Luke 23).
👑 Hinduism
  • Actions have consequences through karma; wrong actions affect this life and future rebirths.The law of karma; the goal of moksha.
  • Following one's dharma (duty) and practising ahimsa (non-violence) guard against wrongdoing.Teachings of the Bhagavad Gita on right action.
  • Greed and ignorance are seen as roots of harmful action; self-control is valued.The Laws of Manu on duty and justice.
⚖ Non-religious views
  • Humanists judge actions by their consequences for human wellbeing, not by religious law.
  • Many point to social causes of crime: poverty, poor upbringing, mental illness and addiction.
  • Some accept that opposing an unjust law may be morally justified (civil disobedience).
👪 Effect on victims and society
  • Theft can cause loss, fear and a breakdown of trust within a community.
  • Murder causes irreparable harm to families and undermines the sanctity of life.
  • Hate crimes target identity and damage social cohesion; both religious and non-religious people condemn them.

Beliefs The treatment of criminals and forgiveness

GCSE study covers prison, corporal punishment and community service as forms of treatment, alongside the religious value placed on forgiveness.

✚ Christian attitudes
  • Most Christians accept prison and community service, which allow for reformation.
  • Many reject corporal punishment as failing to respect human dignity; some quote the call to "turn the other cheek".Matthew 5:39.
  • Forgiveness is central; Christians are taught to forgive "seventy times seven".Matthew 18:21-22; the Lord's Prayer.
👑 Hindu attitudes
  • Punishment that helps an offender restore good karma and reform is valued.
  • Ahimsa leads many Hindus to oppose harsh corporal punishment.
  • Forgiveness (kshama) is a virtue that frees both victim and offender from continuing harm.

Exam AQA-style practice questions

Reveal model points after attempting each question yourself. The 12-mark question requires balanced argument, reference to religion, and a justified conclusion (AO2).

1 mark

Which one of the following means "punishment as a deserved payback for wrongdoing"?

A: deterrence   B: retribution   C: reformation   D: protection

Answer

B: retribution.

2 marks

Give two reasons why people may commit crime.

Command word: "give" means state two reasons.

Indicative content

Any two of: poverty; poor upbringing; mental illness; addiction; greed; hate; opposition to an unjust law.

4 marks

Explain two religious beliefs about the aims of punishment.

Command word: "explain" means develop two points; refer to sacred writings or another source of authority.

Indicative content

1. Many Christians support reformation, believing every person can change and be forgiven, reflecting Jesus's compassion to wrongdoers (the criminal on the cross, Luke 23).

2. Hindus may emphasise that punishment helps restore good karma and right behaviour, linked to dharma and the long-term goal of moksha.

5 marks

Explain two contrasting beliefs in contemporary British society about corporal punishment. Refer to the main religious tradition in Britain and one other religious tradition.

Command word: "explain ... contrasting" means give two different views and develop them.

Indicative content

Christianity (main tradition): many Christians reject corporal punishment as failing to respect human dignity and the teaching to "turn the other cheek" (Matthew 5:39); they prefer reformative treatment.

Hinduism (one other): many Hindus oppose corporal punishment because of ahimsa (non-violence), preferring approaches that restore good karma.

A developed contrast and clear reference to a source of authority is required for full marks.

12 marks + 3 SPaG

"The death penalty can never be justified." Evaluate this statement.

In your answer you should: refer to religious arguments; refer to non-religious arguments; reach a justified conclusion. (AO2)

Model framework

Arguments agreeing (death penalty never justified): the sanctity of life means only God should take life ("You shall not murder", Exodus 20); Jesus taught forgiveness and showed mercy, which the death penalty removes; it ends any chance of reformation; ahimsa in Hinduism opposes the taking of life; non-religious arguments note that innocent people have been wrongly executed and that there is weak evidence it deters.

Arguments disagreeing (it can be justified): some appeal to retribution and "a life for a life" (Exodus 21:24); it offers strong protection of society and may deter the most serious crimes; some Hindus historically accepted state punishment under dharma to maintain order; some non-religious people argue it gives justice to victims' families.

Justified conclusion: a strong answer weighs both sides and reaches a clear, reasoned judgement, for example that the weight of Christian teaching on forgiveness and the sanctity of life, together with the risk of executing the innocent, makes the death penalty difficult to justify; while acknowledging the genuine force of the retribution and protection arguments. Examiners reward a conclusion that follows from the argument, not a list.
