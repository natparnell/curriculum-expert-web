What is the inverted-U theory?

As arousal rises from low to moderate, performance improves. Beyond an optimal point, further arousal causes performance to drop, giving a curve shaped like an upside-down U. The location of that optimal point is not fixed: it shifts with the type of skill and the performer.

Gross, simple or power skills (such as a rugby tackle or a sprint start) tolerate and often need high arousal, so their peak sits to the right. Fine, complex or precision skills (such as a golf putt or archery) need low to moderate arousal, so their peak sits to the left. Beginners and cognitive-stage performers also peak lower than experts.

3. Key terms for your answers

Arousal: a state of physical and mental readiness or alertness, on a scale from deep sleep to intense excitement.
Optimal arousal: the arousal level at which a performer produces their best performance for a given skill.
Gross skill: uses large muscle groups, little precision (for example a shot put). Peaks high.
Fine skill: uses small, precise movements (for example a snooker shot). Peaks low.
Over-arousal: arousal past the optimal point, causing rushed decisions, muscle tension and dropped performance.
Stage of learning: beginners (cognitive stage) reach their optimal point at a lower arousal than experts (autonomous stage).
