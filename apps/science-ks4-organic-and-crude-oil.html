    Crude oil: a finite mixture

    Crude oil formed over millions of years from the buried remains of ancient sea organisms (mainly plankton). It is a finite resource: it is being used far faster than it forms, so it will run out. It is a mixture of a very large number of compounds, most of which are hydrocarbons (molecules made of hydrogen and carbon only). Most are alkanes.

    Intermolecular forces grow stronger as chains get longer, so more energy is needed to separate the molecules. That is why boiling point and viscosity both rise down the column.

    Combustion of hydrocarbon fuels

    Complete combustion (plenty of oxygen)

    The carbon and hydrogen are fully oxidised. Both products are released, and energy is transferred to the surroundings (combustion is exothermic).

    hydrocarbon + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water
    CH4 + 2O2 → CO2 + 2H2O

    Incomplete combustion (limited oxygen)

    With too little oxygen, fuels also produce carbon monoxide (a toxic gas) and soot (carbon particulates), and release less energy.

    Carbon monoxide (CO) is a colourless, odourless toxic gas: it binds to haemoglobin and stops the blood carrying oxygen. Soot causes respiratory problems and global dimming.

    Alkenes and the bromine water test

    Cracking also produces alkenes. Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons: they contain a carbon to carbon double bond (C=C), general formula CnH2n (for example ethene C2H4).

    Test for an alkene: shake the gas or liquid with orange bromine water. An alkene decolourises the bromine water (orange to colourless) because the C=C double bond reacts by addition. A saturated alkane leaves it orange.

    Because of the reactive double bond, alkenes are useful starting materials, especially for making polymers (plastics) by addition polymerisation.

    Exam-style questions

    AQA GCSE Chemistry style. Attempt each question, then reveal the mark scheme.

    1. Crude oil is described as a finite mixture of hydrocarbons. Explain what is meant by (a) finite and (b) hydrocarbon. (2 marks)
    (a) Finite: it is being used up faster than it forms, so it will eventually run out / is non-renewable. (1)
    (b) Hydrocarbon: a compound made of hydrogen and carbon atoms only. (1)
    2. State the general formula of the alkanes and give the molecular formula of the alkane with 5 carbon atoms (pentane). (2 marks)
    General formula: CnH2n+2. (1)
    Pentane: H = (2 × 5) + 2 = 12, so the formula is C5H12. (1)
    3. As you move down a fractionating column, describe how the boiling point and viscosity of the fractions change, and explain why. (3 marks)
    Both boiling point and viscosity increase down the column. (1) This is because the hydrocarbon chains get longer. (1) Longer chains have stronger intermolecular forces, so more energy is needed to separate the molecules (higher boiling point) and they flow less easily (higher viscosity). (1)
    4. Decane (C₁₀H₂₂) is cracked to produce octane and one other product. Write a balanced equation and name the type of compound produced alongside the octane. (3 marks)
    C10H22 → C8H18 + C2H4 (2: 1 for formulae, 1 for balancing: C 10 = 8 + 2, H 22 = 18 + 4). The other product (C2H4, ethene) is an alkene. (1)
    5. A student burns methane in a limited supply of oxygen. Name two harmful products that may form and state one problem caused by each. (4 marks)
    Carbon monoxide (1): toxic, binds to haemoglobin and reduces oxygen carried by the blood. (1)
    Carbon (soot / particulates) (1): causes respiratory problems and global dimming / makes surfaces dirty. (1)
    6. Describe a chemical test, including the result, that distinguishes ethene from ethane. (3 marks)
    Add / shake each gas with bromine water. (1) Ethene (the alkene) turns the bromine water from orange to colourless (decolourises it). (1) Ethane (the alkane) leaves the bromine water orange (no change). (1)
