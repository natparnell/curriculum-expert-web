Urban Issues & Challenges

Teaching aim: understand the causes and consequences of rapid urbanisation worldwide, contrast the urban opportunities and challenges of a city in a newly emerging economy (NEE) with those of a major UK city, and evaluate strategies for sustainable urban living and urban transport.

All named-city detail (Rio de Janeiro and Bristol) is illustrative and simplified for teaching. Figures are approximate, the maps are hand-drawn diagrams, and they are not to scale or for navigation.
1 · Global urbanisation & megacities

Why are cities growing?

Urbanisation is the increase in the proportion of people living in towns and cities. In 2007 the world passed the point where over half of all people were urban; the rate is now fastest in LICs and NEEs.

Two processes drive it: rural to urban migration (people moving for work, services and safety, pulled by perceived opportunity and pushed by rural poverty, drought or conflict) and natural increase (a young migrant population having children, with birth rate exceeding death rate).

A megacity is an urban area with over 10 million people. Most new megacities are in Asia, Africa and South America.

5 · How this works & key terms

About this app

The City explorer uses two hand-drawn zoned maps. Each coloured zone stores a short profile plus its main opportunities and challenges, mapped to AQA content (location and importance, causes of growth, social and economic opportunities, and managing squatter settlements, water, sanitation, energy and the environment). The Sustainable city builder scores your five strategy choices. Each option carries weighted effects on a sustainability score and four liveability indicators (air quality, water security, congestion and community), so trade-offs become visible. None of the figures are real measurements; they are teaching estimates.

Urbanisation
The growing share of a population living in towns and cities.
NEE
Newly emerging economy: a country with rapid economic growth, such as Brazil.
Megacity
An urban area with a population of over 10 million.
Rural to urban migration
People moving from the countryside to cities, driven by push and pull factors.
Natural increase
Population growth where the birth rate is higher than the death rate.
Squatter settlement (favela)
An area of informal, often self-built housing on land the residents do not own.
Urban regeneration
Reviving a run-down urban area, often former industrial or derelict land.
Urban sprawl
The unplanned outward spread of a city into the surrounding countryside.
Sustainable urban living
Meeting present needs (housing, transport, resources) without harming future generations.
Deprivation
A lack of access to income, jobs, health, education or decent housing.
