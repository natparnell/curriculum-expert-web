The exposure triangle

Correct exposure is a balancing act between three settings. Open the aperture by one stop and you must shorten the shutter or drop the ISO by one stop to hold the same brightness. Each control also brings a creative side effect, which is why the same exposure can be achieved many ways with very different looks.

Aperture → depth of fieldWide apertures isolate a subject with a soft background; narrow apertures keep everything sharp for landscapes.
Shutter → motionFast shutters freeze a sprinter; slow shutters streak movement into deliberate blur.
ISO → noiseLow ISO is clean for studio work; high ISO rescues dim scenes at the cost of grain.

How the workflow meets the assessment objectives

AO1

Assessment objective 1

Develop ideas through investigation, demonstrating critical understanding of sources.

  • Research photographers across the genres above (portrait, landscape, documentary, still life, abstract).
  • Make written and visual notes that show how their lighting and composition inform your plan.
AO2

Assessment objective 2

Refine work by exploring ideas, selecting and experimenting with techniques and processes.

  • Shoot the same subject at different apertures, shutter speeds and viewpoints.
  • Compare exposures and editing choices; explain why one version is stronger.
AO3

Assessment objective 3

Record ideas, observations and insights relevant to intentions as work progresses.

  • Annotate your contact sheet: what worked, what to reshoot, which frames to take forward.
  • Capture lighting conditions and settings so the process is evidenced, not assumed.
AO4

Assessment objective 4

Present a personal and meaningful response that realises intentions and makes connections.

  • Choose one resolved image that answers your original question or theme.
  • Present it with intent (scale, sequence, edit) so it reads as a deliberate outcome.

Exam and coursework style prompts in AQA assessment-objective language. Think first, then reveal a model response. These suit both the Portfolio and the Externally Set Assignment.

AO1  Explain how you would investigate sources for a photography project on the theme "Edges".
Model: Gather a mood board of photographers whose work touches edges literally and conceptually (for example coastlines, thresholds, society's margins). Annotate two or three in depth: identify their lighting, viewpoint and composition, then state precisely how each will shape my own shoot plan. The aim is critical understanding, not description.
AO2  Describe an experiment that refines your control of depth of field.
Model: Photograph one still-life subject at f/1.8, f/5.6 and f/16 with the focus fixed, holding exposure constant by adjusting shutter and ISO. Lay the three side by side, label the depth of field in each, and justify which serves my intention best. This shows selecting and experimenting with techniques and processes.
AO2  How does the exposure triangle force a creative trade-off? Give a worked example.
Model: To freeze a skateboarder I need 1/1000 s, which darkens the frame by three stops versus 1/125 s. I recover light either by opening the aperture (losing background sharpness) or raising ISO (adding noise). I would open the aperture to keep the image clean and accept a softer background, recording the reasoning as evidence.
AO3  What should a strong contact-sheet annotation contain?
Model: For each shortlisted frame, note the settings, the lighting, what works compositionally, and one specific improvement or reshoot. Observations should connect back to my intentions so the marker can see thinking, not just selection.
AO4  What makes a final image a "personal and meaningful response"?
Model: It resolves my original theme with a clear point of view, draws on my AO1 sources without copying them, and is presented with deliberate choices about edit, scale and sequence. It should feel like a conclusion the journey was building towards.
All AOs  Outline a documentary shoot that evidences all four objectives.
Model: AO1: research social documentary photographers and a local subject. AO2: trial available light versus fill flash and high versus low viewpoints. AO3: build a contact sheet with settings and reflections after each session. AO4: present a tight edited sequence that tells the story I set out to tell, connected back to my sources.
Glossary of lens-based terms
Aperture (f-stop)
The lens opening. Small f-numbers (f/1.4) are wide; large numbers (f/16) are narrow.
Shutter speed
How long the sensor is exposed. Fast freezes motion; slow blurs it.
ISO
Sensor sensitivity to light. Higher ISO brightens but adds grain.
Exposure value (EV)
A single number for overall brightness. 0 EV is correctly exposed here; negative is dark, positive is blown out.
Depth of field
How much of the scene front to back is acceptably sharp.
Bokeh
The quality of out-of-focus background blur produced by a wide aperture.
Contact sheet
A grid of every frame, used to select and annotate the strongest images.
Power point
A rule-of-thirds intersection where the eye naturally rests.
