Long-term training adaptations

With regular aerobic training the heart shows cardiac hypertrophy: the muscular wall thickens, so stroke volume rises and resting heart rate falls (bradycardia). The lungs gain a larger vital capacity and more efficient gaseous exchange, and capillarisation increases at the alveoli and the muscles.

Key knowledge: the two systems (AQA 8582)

The musculo-skeletal system

Functions of the skeleton: support, protection of vital organs, movement (as a system of levers), structural shape and the point of muscle attachment, plus the production of red blood cells in the bone marrow and mineral storage.

Major bones you should know: cranium, vertebrae, ribs, sternum, scapula, clavicle, humerus, radius, ulna, pelvis, femur, patella, tibia, fibula, talus.

Synovial joints allow a wide range of movement. The freely moving types include the hinge joint (elbow, knee, ankle), allowing flexion and extension only, and the ball and socket joint (shoulder, hip), allowing flexion, extension, abduction, adduction and rotation.

Antagonistic muscle pairs: muscles can only pull, so they work in pairs across a joint. The agonist (prime mover) contracts while the antagonist relaxes. Examples: biceps and triceps at the elbow, and quadriceps and hamstrings at the knee.

The cardio-respiratory system

The heart and cardiac cycle: the four chambers are the right atrium, right ventricle, left atrium and left ventricle, separated by the septum and controlled by valves. In diastole the chambers relax and fill; in systole they contract and force blood out.

Double circulation: deoxygenated blood returns to the right side of the heart, is pumped to the lungs (pulmonary circuit), returns oxygenated to the left side, and is then pumped to the body (systemic circuit). Blood passes through the heart twice per full circuit.

Blood vessels: arteries carry blood away from the heart under high pressure with thick muscular walls; veins return blood at low pressure and contain valves to prevent backflow; capillaries are one cell thick to allow gaseous exchange with the tissues.

Mechanics of breathing: during inspiration the diaphragm flattens and the intercostal muscles raise the ribs, increasing thoracic volume and drawing air in; during expiration these muscles relax and air is pushed out. Gaseous exchange happens at the alveoli: oxygen diffuses into the blood and carbon dioxide diffuses out, across a large, moist, thin and well-supplied surface.

Cardiac output = heart rate × stroke volume. It rises sharply during exercise to deliver more oxygen to the working muscles.
