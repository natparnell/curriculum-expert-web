Composition Studio

Build a diatonic melody on the piano-roll grid, set a chord progression underneath, then hear them together. Use the development tools to turn a short motif into a finished phrase.

Teaching aim: to apply the core techniques of AQA A-level composing — melodic shape and phrasing, motif development through sequence, inversion and augmentation, diatonic harmony and cadence, texture and structure — while composing to a brief and freely.

The composition sandbox

Click a cell to place or remove a note. Only diatonic pitches for the chosen key and scale are shown, so everything you write stays in key. Set a chord per bar in the track beneath the grid, then press Play to hear melody and harmony together.

How to use the grid (keyboard and mouse)

Each row is a diatonic pitch; the lowest tonic and an octave above are shaded. Each column is one beat, and four beats make a bar (4/4). Click any cell to place a note; click again to remove it. A column can hold one melody note at a time, so placing a new note clears any other note in that beat.

Keyboard: Tab into the grid, move with the arrow keys, and toggle a note with Enter or Space. Press P anywhere to Play or Stop.

Development tools

Composers rarely invent new material for every bar. They take a short motif and develop it. Select a source bar range, then apply a technique. The result is written into the target bars, overwriting whatever is there.

What each technique does
  • Sequence: the same melodic shape restated one step higher or lower in the scale (a diatonic sequence). A staple of melody writing.
  • Inversion: the contour is turned upside down. A rising third becomes a falling third, measured in scale steps about the first note.
  • Retrograde: the phrase played backwards.
  • Augmentation: note values doubled, so the phrase moves at half speed and fills twice as many bars. Diminution halves the values.

Composing guidance

Melody: shape and phrasing

Aim for a clear contour with a single high point (climax). Balance steps and leaps; follow a leap with stepwise movement in the opposite direction. Write in phrases (often 2 or 4 bars) that answer one another, like question and answer (antecedent and consequent).

Harmony: diatonic chords I to VII

DegreeChordFunction

Primary chords I, IV, V carry the key. Secondary chords ii, iii, vi add colour. End phrases with cadences: V–I (perfect), IV–I (plagal), ?–V (imperfect), V–vi (interrupted).

Texture and structure

The grid plus chord track gives you melody and accompaniment. Add a countermelody by writing a second line that moves when the tune is held. Use the structure selector to lay out sections (binary, ternary, rondo, verse-chorus) so repetition and contrast are deliberate.

Compose to a brief

Set brief: a reflective opening

Write an 8-bar melody in a major or minor key for a solo instrument. It should:

  • open with a 2-bar motif and develop it by sequence in bars 3 to 4;
  • reach a clear climax in bars 5 to 6;
  • close with a perfect cadence (V–I) in bars 7 to 8;
  • use mostly stepwise motion with no leap wider than an octave.

Mark-band reminder (AQA composing)

The highest bands reward a coherent and stylistically convincing piece: a memorable melodic idea that is genuinely developed (not merely repeated), a secure harmonic framework with varied cadences, textural interest beyond block chords, and a clear structure. Use this studio to draft those decisions before notating in your scorewriter.
