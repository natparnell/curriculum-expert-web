History • Key Stage 5 • Component 2 Depth Study

Contingency and Counterfactual Lab

Teacher aim: students test causal complexity across a 25 to 50 year depth study by removing or altering one factor in a crisis, then reasoning out how the chain of events might plausibly have changed. The lab makes visible the difference between underlying conditions, trigger causes and genuinely contingent moments.

1 The depth-study crisis

Depth study: The road to the First World War, Europe 1890–1919. By 1914 a web of long-run conditions had built up across the period: a hardened alliance system, an Anglo-German naval and arms race, inflexible mobilisation timetables (Germany's Schlieffen Plan), nationalist pressure in the Balkans, and a culture among elites that expected a short, winnable war. Then, in a matter of weeks, a single assassination tipped the continent into general war.

28 June 1914: Archduke Franz Ferdinand is assassinated in Sarajevo. Over the following July Crisis, decisions cascade: Austria-Hungary issues an ultimatum to Serbia, Russia mobilises, Germany activates the Schlieffen Plan and invades Belgium, and Britain enters the war.

Your task: pick one factor below, decide how to change it, then run the counterfactual. A historian never claims certainty about "what would have happened"; you are reasoning about plausibility, not prophecy.
