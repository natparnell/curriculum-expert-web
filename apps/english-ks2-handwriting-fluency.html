    What fluent handwriting looks like

    Fluent handwriting is legible, even and quick. Letters sit on the line, are a consistent size, and join smoothly so the hand keeps moving without stopping to print each letter separately. The KS2 National Curriculum expects pupils to increase the legibility, consistency and quality of their handwriting.

    A good rule of thumb: a reader should not have to slow down, and the writer should not have to lift the pen for every letter.

    The four joins

    • Diagonal join, no ascender: a stroke climbs from the baseline to the next letter, for example in, ai, un.
    • Diagonal join to an ascender: the join climbs higher to meet a tall letter, for example th, ch, il.
    • Horizontal join: a flat stroke from the top of a letter, for example oo, ow, we.
    • Joins from letters like o and r: these start from the top, so the join curves down then across, for example on, re, oa.

    Which letters are best left unjoined

    Some letters are usually left unjoined because joining them slows the hand or harms legibility. Capital letters never join to the letter after them, and letters such as g, j, q, x, y and z are commonly left to break before the next letter rather than forcing an awkward join.

    The four things to check

    • Consistent size: ascenders (b, d, h, k, l, t) reach the same height; descenders (g, j, p, q, y) drop the same depth; small letters fill the same x-height.
    • Even spacing: equal gaps between letters, and a finger-width gap between words.
    • Parallel, equidistant down-strokes: the straight down-strokes of letters lean at the same angle and sit the same distance apart, which is what makes a line of writing look even.
    • Correct joins: the right join for each pair, and sensible breaks where joining would not help.

    How to use this app

    Use Join & Trace to watch each join animate, then trace it on the guideline surface and read the slant and coverage feedback. Use Self-assess to mark your own writing against the criteria, and Spot to Improve to train your eye on samples that contain deliberate faults.
