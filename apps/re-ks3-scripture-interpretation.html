RE · KS3 · Agreed Syllabus · Sources of Authority & Interpretation

How do believers read sacred texts, and why do they read them differently?

Teaching aim: To understand what believers treat as sources of authority, to explore how the same sacred passage can be read literally, symbolically or contextually, and to see how interpretation creates diversity of belief and practice within a single religion.

1Where do believers find authority?

When believers decide what is true or how to live, they draw on different sources of authority. Most use several together, weighing them in different ways.

📖

Sacred texts

Writings believed to carry teaching from God or the tradition, such as the Bible, the Qur'an, the Torah, the Vedas or the Guru Granth Sahib.

👤

Religious leaders & tradition

The guidance of scholars, clergy and long-standing community practice that has been handed down and trusted over time.

🧠

Reason & conscience

Thinking carefully about what is right, and listening to the inner sense of conscience that helps a person judge an action.

✨

Personal experience

A believer's own sense of the sacred, in prayer, worship, community or a moment that feels deeply meaningful.

4Decision scenario: one religion, different answers

A community is asked to welcome and care for refugees arriving in their town. Members of the same religion read the same teaching, “love your neighbour as yourself”, yet they respond in different ways.

Reveal how believers with different interpretations might apply their sources of authority to this issue.

More literal Reads the command directly

“The text says love your neighbour, so we open our hall, organise meals and welcome people personally. The instruction is plain and we follow it as written.”

More liberal Reads the spirit of the teaching

“We see neighbour as a symbol for all humanity, so we campaign for fairer national policy and long-term support, not only immediate help. The deeper principle matters more than the literal wording.”

Contextual Reads the original setting

“The teaching was first given to a small community caring for one another. We ask what loving a neighbour means today, and conclude it includes the stranger, so we do both practical welcome and advocacy.”

Drawing on other sources Reason and conscience

“Alongside the text, our conscience and our leaders' guidance tell us this is right, even where resources are stretched, so we act within what we can sustain.”

The key idea: all four believers share the same religion and the same text, yet their interpretation shapes a different response. This is how a single religion comes to hold a diversity of belief and practice.

5Reflect

Think quietly, or discuss with a partner:
  • Which source of authority do you think people rely on most when they make important decisions, and why?
  • Can a text be true even if it is not meant to be read literally? What might “true” mean here?
  • Is diversity within a religion a strength or a problem? Give a reason for both views.
