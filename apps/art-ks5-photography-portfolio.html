Photography: the Personal Investigation

Teaching aim: to develop a sustained, personal and meaningful photographic enquiry. You will master advanced camera technique, locate your work within the practice of established photographers and movements, and build a coherent body of work and a related written element that evidences the four AQA assessment objectives at A-level depth.

Take full manual control of the exposure triangle and apply advanced photographic techniques to a procedural scene. Watch how aperture, shutter speed and ISO interact, and how a chosen technique transforms the image.       Guidance

      4How it is assessed: what each part rewards

      Component 1 (the Personal Investigation) is worth 96 marks at A-level, marked across the four assessment objectives, with the related written element embedded within them. Use these explainers when you plan and when you review.

      The four assessment objectives, in plain terms
      AO1: Develop ideasInvestigate sources and show critical understanding of contextual material. Reward comes from analysis and connection, not from copying biographies of photographers.
      AO2: Explore and developRefine your ideas through experimenting with techniques, processes and media, reviewing as you go. Show a journey, including experiments that did not work.
      AO3: RecordRecord ideas, observations and insights relevant to your intentions. In photography this is your contact sheets, shoot logs, edits and reflective annotation.
      AO4: PresentPresent a personal and meaningful response that realises your intentions and connects the visual and written elements, demonstrating understanding of formal elements.
      The written element: what makes it strong

      The related written element is 1000 to 3000 words of continuous prose that is integral to your practical work. It is not a separate essay sitting alongside the portfolio: it should explain, justify and deepen the practical enquiry.

      Line of reasoningOpen with a clear question or argument tied to your theme, then sustain it through the piece.
      Critical use of sourcesReference photographers and texts accurately, with a bibliography. Quote and analyse, do not simply describe.
      Subject terminologyUse correct terms for technique (aperture, depth of field, exposure) and for analysis (composition, tone, mood, formal elements).
      IntegrationReference your own images and experiments so the writing and the practical work clearly belong together.
      Advanced techniques worth showing in your portfolio

      Examiners reward purposeful, controlled use of technique that serves an idea. Aim to evidence several of the following in your AO2 experiments:

      Full manual exposureDeliberate control of aperture, shutter and ISO together, chosen for effect rather than left on automatic.
      Depth of fieldWide apertures to isolate a subject; narrow apertures for front-to-back sharpness in landscape.
      Long exposureSlow shutter on a tripod to blur water, cloud or light trails and suggest the passing of time.
      Studio and natural lightingHigh-key and low-key set-ups, and reading available light to control mood.
      Multiple exposure and photomontageLayering images in camera or in editing to build surreal or symbolic meaning.
