Teacher aim: Pupils run a campaign in a knife-edge marginal seat. They spend limited time and money on real campaign tactics (manifesto pledges, leafleting, canvassing, social media, working with a pressure group) and watch live polling shift, learning how parties, manifestos and citizen participation compete for votes.

How polling moves (for pupils)
  • Manifesto pledges set your message. A popular, costed pledge wins trust; an unpopular or uncosted one can backfire.
  • Air war vs ground war: social media reaches many cheaply but persuades weakly; canvassing and leafleting are slower but shift firm votes.
  • Pressure groups can mobilise supporters and add credibility, but tying yourself to a divisive cause risks alienating moderates.
  • Targeting matters: repeating the same tactic gives less each time (voters tire of it). Mix your tactics.
  • Resources are finite: this is the real dilemma of democratic participation: time and money always run out.
Teacher notes & curriculum links KS4 Citizenship
  • Strand: Elections, political parties and the role of citizens. Pupils experience how parties use manifestos and campaigning to compete for votes, and how citizens participate (voting, pressure groups, volunteering, campaigning).
  • Key terms surfaced: marginal seat, manifesto, pledge, canvassing, leafleting, pressure group, swing voter, first-past-the-post, campaign finance.
  • Discussion prompts: Should there be limits on campaign spending? Is the "air war" of social media good for democracy? When does a pressure group strengthen, and when does it distort, the public debate?
