The Earth & Atmosphere

Teaching aim: to understand the layered structure of the Earth, follow a single rock around the rock cycle, learn what the atmosphere is made of, and see why the Earth's resources are limited and must be used efficiently.

What is the atmosphere made of?

The air around us is a mixture of gases. By volume it is roughly 78 percent nitrogen and 21 percent oxygen, with a small amount of carbon dioxide and other gases such as argon and water vapour. Hover or use the buttons to highlight a gas.

The carbon cycle in outline

Carbon is constantly moved between the air, living things, the oceans and rocks:

  • Photosynthesis: plants and algae take carbon dioxide out of the air and lock the carbon into sugars and tissue.
  • Respiration: plants, animals and microbes release carbon dioxide back into the air.
  • Feeding and decay: carbon passes along food chains; when things die, decomposers return carbon to the air and soil.
  • Oceans: seawater dissolves carbon dioxide; shelled creatures build it into limestone over millions of years.
  • Combustion: burning fossil fuels (coal, oil and gas) returns ancient carbon to the air as carbon dioxide, faster than the cycle removes it.

The Earth: a source of limited resources

The Earth provides everything humans use: metals, fossil fuels, minerals and water. Many of these are finite: they take millions of years to form, so once used they cannot be quickly replaced.

  • Use less: design products that need fewer raw materials.
  • Reuse and recycle: metals such as aluminium can be melted and used again, saving energy and ore.
  • Renewables: using sunlight, wind and water for energy reduces reliance on finite fossil fuels.
