KS3 Design and Technology • Design

From Context to Spec

Read a design context, find what real users need, turn those needs into a measurable specification, then sketch and annotate concepts that prove each spec point.

Teacher aim: Pupils practise user-centred design: interrogating a context, writing measurable specification points and communicating design intent through annotated sketches and CAD-ready ideas.

📋 The design context

Client: Brookfield Community Allotments, a fictional gardening group used for this lesson.

Brookfield runs raised-bed plots used by older gardeners and by a Year 7 club. Members say small hand tools (trowels, forks, dibbers) get muddy, lost in the soil and mixed up between plots. The group has asked Year 8 to design a portable hand-tool tidy that one person can carry to a bed and keep beside them while they work. Budget is low and it must cope with rain.

Stage 1: Interrogate the context

User-centred design starts with the users, not the product. Tap the phrases in the brief below that reveal a real user need or constraint. You need five.

Click or tap a phrase to select it. Selecting clues that are not user needs will be flagged. You can change your mind.

The tidy is used by older gardeners and by a Year 7 club, so it must suit a wide range of hand sizes and strength. Members report that tools get muddy and lost in the soil. Different tools get mixed up between plots, causing arguments. The allotment is by the coast where it rains often, so the product must cope with rain. The committee likes the colour green and meets on Tuesdays. Because plots are spread out, the tidy must be easy for one person to carry to a bed. The shed was painted last year.

User needs you have identified

    Tip: a user need says what the user must be able to do, or a real constraint the design must respect. It is not a personal preference about style.

    Stage 2: Write a measurable specification

    A good specification point is measurable or testable: it uses a number, a unit, a standard or a yes/no test, so you can prove the product meets it. Turn each user need into one spec point. Tap a starter chip if you are stuck.

    Measurable words to reach for: a quantity with a unit (mm, kg, litres), a number (holds 6 tools), a recognised test (waterproof rating, drains within 30 seconds), or a clear yes/no check.

    Stage 3: Sketch and annotate your concept

    Communicate your design intent. Sketch a quick concept, then drop an annotation pin onto the feature that meets each spec point. Pin every spec point to show how your idea answers the brief.
