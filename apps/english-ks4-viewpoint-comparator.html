Text A · 19th century

On the Crowding of the Streets

Mary Sefton, letter to a city newspaper, 1843 (fictional source for demonstration)

I walked this morning from Bishop's Gate to the wharf, and was near pressed insensible by the multitude. The pavement is a river of bodies; a lady may not pass without her sleeve being torn.

It is, I confess, a marvel of trade. Every face is bent upon some errand, every cart heaped with the produce of our manufactories. The very air hums with industry.

Yet I fear for the children. They dart between the wheels like sparrows, and I have seen one struck down for want of a moment's care. We have built a great machine and forgotten who must live inside it.

Text B · 21st century

My Daily Commute Is Slowly Crushing Me

Dev Okafor, online opinion column, 2019 (fictional source for demonstration)

I do not travel to work so much as I am processed by it. Six forty-five, platform two, and a thousand of us are funnelled into a carriage built, generously, for half that number.

And yes, I know what the economists say. This is the engine of the city, the productivity, the growth. I have read the dashboards. I have seen the graphs go up.

But nobody asks how it feels. My phone counts my steps and my heart rate; it does not count the small daily wearing-down of being a number in someone else's spreadsheet.
