Social and historical context

Context explorer

Priestley sets the play in 1912 but wrote it for a 1945 audience. That gap is itself a directorial weapon: the audience knows what the Birlings cannot. Each card links the context to a choice a theatre maker can make.

1912

When the play is set

A confident Edwardian spring. Class divisions are rigid; Birling calls the Titanic "absolutely unsinkable" and dismisses talk of war.

Theatre maker: use dramatic irony. Costume and set should look secure and grand so the audience's hindsight does the undermining.
1945

When it was first performed

The Second World War is ending; Britain is about to elect a Labour government and build the welfare state. The audience has lived the consequences Birling denies.

Theatre maker: direct the final phone call to land as a warning to the audience now, not just to the family on stage.
1945

Priestley's socialist message

Priestley was a committed socialist. The Inspector's final speech ("We are members of one body") argues for collective responsibility against Birling's "every man for himself".

Theatre maker: let the Inspector address the auditorium directly in the final speech to break the fourth wall and implicate the audience.
?

The dramatic device of the Inspector

Goole arrives, controls the interrogation, then vanishes; the phone call suggests a real inspector is coming. Is he a policeman, a ghost, a conscience, or Priestley's own voice?

Theatre maker: your casting, lighting and sound design decide which reading the audience takes. The play never settles it for you.
Eva

Eva Smith / Daisy Renton

The working-class woman whose death links every character. She never appears on stage; she is built entirely from what others say about her.

Theatre maker: decide whether to keep her absent (a symbol of every exploited worker) or evoke her through a photograph, a projection or an empty chair.
Gen

The generational split

Sheila and Eric accept responsibility and change; their parents and Gerald retreat to the status quo. Priestley pins his hope on the young.

Theatre maker: use proxemics and blocking to separate the younger generation from the older as the play ends, so the divide is visible.

How to use this

This tool treats An Inspector Calls as Eduqas GCSE Drama requires: as live theatre to be realised, not a novel to be analysed. Everything here is illustrative teaching material to practise the choices and vocabulary you need for the set-text questions.

1. Director's concept board

  • Pick one of three pivotal moments.
  • Adjust set, lighting state, sound, focus and proxemics. The canvas stage redraws and the readout explains the meaning your combination creates.
  • There is no single correct answer. The skill is justifying your choice against Priestley's intentions.

2. Character interpretation

  • Choose a character and a key line, then a vocal and a physical choice.
  • See how delivery changes meaning. This is how an actor builds a role.

3. Context explorer

  • Six cards link the 1912 setting and 1945 first performance to concrete theatre-maker decisions.

4. Page to stage

  • Eduqas-style questions with reveal-able model paragraphs. Notice the structure: choice plus reason plus intended effect on the audience, with accurate terminology.

Key terminology to use

  • Proxemics: the meaning created by distance between actors.
  • Blocking: where and how actors move on stage.
  • Levels: standing, sitting, raised positions to show status.
  • Lighting state, sound cue, stage configuration (proscenium, thrust, in the round), dramatic irony, tension, symbolism.
