      Fibres, fabrics and finishing explained

      The technical knowledge behind the choices above.

      Fibres: natural and synthetic

      Natural fibres come from plants or animals. Cotton (plant) is cool, soft, absorbent and cheap, but creases and is not very warm. Wool (sheep) is warm and absorbs moisture while still feeling dry, but is costlier and can shrink. Silk (silkworm) is strong, smooth and luxurious, but expensive and delicate to wash.

      Synthetic fibres are made from chemicals, usually from oil. Polyester is strong, durable, quick-drying and cheap, but not very absorbent. Nylon is very strong, light and abrasion-resistant, used for bags and outdoor gear. Synthetics resist creasing but can feel less breathable and shed microplastics when washed.

      From fibre to fabric: weaving and knitting

      Weaving interlaces two sets of yarns at right angles (the warp running the length and the weft running across). Woven fabric is stable and hard-wearing with little stretch, good for structured products such as bags and jeans.

      Knitting loops a single yarn through itself. Knitted fabric stretches and recovers, making it comfortable for jumpers, T-shirts and sportswear, but it can ladder and lose shape. Fibres can also be bonded into non-woven fabrics such as felt.

      Properties: strength, warmth, absorbency, durability, stretch, cost

      A designer matches properties to the job. Strength and durability matter for products that carry weight or are used hard. Warmth and absorbency matter for clothing worn next to the skin. Stretch gives comfort and fit. Cost is always a constraint: the best fabric is the one that meets the brief at a sensible price, not simply the most expensive.

      Construction and joining: seams, hems and fastenings

      A seam joins fabric pieces. A plain seam is quick and neat for most products; a French seam encloses raw edges for a strong, tidy finish on lightweight fabric; an overlocked edge stops fraying on stretch fabrics. A hem finishes a raw edge by folding and stitching it.

      Fastenings open and close a product: buttons, a zip, press studs or hook-and-loop (Velcro). The choice depends on how often it opens, how strong it must be and how it should look.

      Surface decoration in outline

      Printing applies colour or pattern to the surface (for example block, screen or transfer printing). Dyeing colours the whole fabric or yarn, including tie-dye and batch dyeing. Applique stitches shaped pieces of fabric on top for texture and pattern. Embroidery decorates with stitched thread, by hand or machine.

      Modern and smart textiles, and sustainability

      Modern textiles are engineered for performance: microfibres are extremely fine for soft, dense fabrics; breathable membranes (such as the technology behind waterproof-breathable jackets) keep rain out while letting sweat vapour escape. Smart textiles respond to their environment, for example thermochromic fabrics that change colour with temperature.

      Sustainability asks where fibres come from, how much water and energy production uses, how long a product lasts and whether it can be reused or recycled. Natural fibres are renewable but can be water-intensive; synthetics last well but rely on oil and shed microplastics. Designing for durability and repair reduces waste.
