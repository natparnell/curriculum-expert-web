Complex Numbers & the Argand Diagram

Teaching aim: Build a geometric feel for complex arithmetic. Drag z₁ and z₂ on the Argand plane and watch how addition, subtraction, multiplication, division and conjugation behave as moves in the plane: vectors that add tip-to-tail, and a multiplication that rotates and scales. Then explore De Moivre powers and the standard complex loci.

Drag the points to move z₁ and z₂. Keyboard: focus the diagram, press 1 or 2 to choose a point, then use the arrow keys (hold Shift for finer steps).

What you are seeing

Modulus and argument: the formulae

For z = a + bi:

  • Modulus: |z| = √(a² + b²) — the distance from the origin.
  • Argument: arg(z) = atan2(b, a), the angle from the positive real axis, measured in the principal range −π < arg(z) ≤ π.
  • Modulus-argument form: z = r(cosθ + i·sinθ), written r·cisθ.

Multiplication multiplies the moduli and adds the arguments: |z₁z₂| = |z₁||z₂|, arg(z₁z₂) = arg z₁ + arg z₂. That is why multiplying by a number of modulus 1 is a pure rotation.

De Moivre's theorem follows by repetition: [r(cosθ + i sinθ)]ⁿ = rⁿ(cos nθ + i sin nθ).

The complex loci, in words
  • | z − a | = r is every point a fixed distance r from the point a, so it draws a circle of radius r centred on a.
  • | z − a | = | z − b | is every point equidistant from a and b, which is the perpendicular bisector of the segment ab.
  • arg( z − a ) = θ is every point such that the line from a to z makes a fixed angle θ with the positive real direction: a half-line starting at (but not including) a.
