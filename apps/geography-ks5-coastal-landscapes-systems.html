The coast as an open system: sediment cells & the sediment budget

A sediment cell (or littoral cell) is a largely self-contained stretch of coast within which the movement of sediment is broadly enclosed. England and Wales are divided into 11 major cells, bounded by headlands and deep water that act as barriers. Each cell is an open system: it exchanges energy and matter with its surroundings.

The sediment budget balances inputs against outputs. A positive budget produces a wider beach (accretion); a negative budget produces a narrower beach and recession. When defences are built, they can shift the budget of the next section into deficit: an example of how change in one part of the system feeds through the whole.

03

Marine and sub-aerial processes

Marine erosion Wave energy

  • Hydraulic action: the sheer force of breaking waves, plus air compressed into joints and bedding planes, fracturing the rock (cavitation).
  • Abrasion (corrasion): waves hurl sediment against the cliff face, wearing it away like sandpaper. The dominant erosional process in most settings.
  • Attrition: sediment particles collide and grind each other down, becoming smaller and rounder. This does not erode the coast directly but reduces grain size.
  • Solution (corrosion): chemical dissolving of soluble rock such as chalk and limestone by mildly acidic seawater.

Marine transport & deposition Movement

  • Traction: large boulders rolled along the sea bed.
  • Saltation: smaller stones bounced along the bed.
  • Suspension: fine sediment carried within the water column.
  • Solution: dissolved load carried chemically in the water.
  • Longshore drift: swash moves sediment up the beach at the angle of wave approach; backwash returns it straight down under gravity, producing net along-shore transport.
  • Deposition: occurs where energy falls below the threshold to carry the load, in sheltered bays, estuaries and behind structures.

Sub-aerial weathering Land-side

  • Mechanical: freeze-thaw and salt crystallisation prise rock apart along joints without chemical change.
  • Chemical: carbonation, hydrolysis and oxidation alter the mineral structure and weaken the rock.
  • Biological: root action and burrowing organisms break the rock down.
  • Weathering prepares material that marine processes then remove, accelerating cliff retreat.

Mass movement Land-side

  • Rockfall: sudden detachment of blocks from a steep, well-jointed cliff.
  • Slumping (rotational slip): typical of weak clay cliffs that become saturated; the cliff rotates along a curved (concave) failure plane. Classic on the Holderness and Christchurch Bay coasts.
  • Soil creep and solifluction: slow downslope movement of regolith.
  • Mass movement supplies sediment to the foot of the cliff, feeding the budget below.

Sea-level change and coastal recession

Eustatic vs isostatic change

Eustatic change is a global change in the volume of water in the oceans, driven by ice melt (rise) or ice growth (fall) and by thermal expansion of warming water. It affects all coasts.

Isostatic change is local vertical movement of the land. After the last glaciation, ice unloading allows the crust to rebound upwards (isostatic recovery), so relative sea level there falls even as the world ocean rises. Northern Britain is rising; the south-east is sinking.

Emergent and submergent coasts

Submergent coasts form where relative sea level rises and drowns the land. A ria is a drowned river valley (the Fal and Fowey in Cornwall); a fjord is a drowned glacial trough with a shallow seaward threshold.

Emergent coasts form where relative sea level falls, exposing former marine features. A raised beach sits above the modern shoreline, often backed by a relict (fossil) cliff. The west coast of Scotland shows both.
