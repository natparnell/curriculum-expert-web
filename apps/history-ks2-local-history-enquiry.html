The Local History Enquiry Workshop

Teaching aim: to learn how historians investigate how their local area has changed over time, by asking a good question, examining a range of sources, judging what each source can and cannot tell us, and drawing an evidence-based conclusion about change and continuity.

Step 1 · Ask a historical question

Every enquiry begins with a question worth answering.

A historian looking at Millbrook wants to find out about the past. Which of these makes the best enquiry question for a local history study? Pick one.

A strong enquiry question is open (it cannot be answered with a single fact), it is about change over time, and it can be answered with evidence. "Is it nicer?" is just an opinion. "What is the population today?" is a one-fact question with no sense of the past. "How has it changed?" invites us to gather and weigh several sources.

Step 3 · Judge what each source can and cannot tell us

No single source gives the whole picture. Historians cross-check.

You have looked at four sources. Now weigh them. Move the sliders to show how reliable and useful you think each one is for our question, then read the historian's note. There is no single right answer here, but the notes explain the strengths and limits of each source.

A source can be reliable (carefully recorded, close to the events) yet only useful for one small thing. A newspaper headline may be vivid and useful for showing how people felt, but it can exaggerate to sell papers. Maps and the census are usually steadier records, but they still miss things, such as how people felt or why the change happened. Good historians cross-check several sources and notice where they agree.

🏁

Step 4 · Draw an evidence-based conclusion

Answer the question using the evidence you gathered.

Our enquiry question was: "How has Millbrook changed from a farming village into a town?" Here is the evidence you collected, source by source.

    A good conclusion notices what changed (the village grew into a town, with new roads, more people and new jobs) but also what stayed the same, the continuity (the old church and some street names survived). Real places almost always show both. Always answer using the evidence, and say honestly where the sources are uncertain.

    How a local history enquiry works (a framework you can reuse)

    Historians do not just collect old things. They ask a question, then look for sources that might answer it, work out what each source can and cannot tell them, and only then reach a conclusion. The same four steps work for any place. Swap Millbrook for your own town or village and use the real records below.

    The four steps

    • Ask a clear, open question about change over time.
    • Examine a range of sources, old and new.
    • Judge what each source can and cannot tell you, and how reliable it is.
    • Conclude using the evidence, noticing both change and continuity.

    Sources a local historian can use

    The more kinds of source you use, the more trustworthy your answer. Try to cross-check at least three.

    Old and new maps The census Old photographs Local newspapers Parish records Buildings and street names Oral history from older residents

    Change and continuity

    Change is what is different now. Continuity is what has stayed the same. A careful conclusion mentions both, because most places keep some of their past while the rest is transformed.
