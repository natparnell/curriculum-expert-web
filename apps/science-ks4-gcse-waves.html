2The key ideas

A wave transfers energy and information without transferring matter. The particles or fields oscillate about a fixed rest position.

Transverse waves

Oscillations are perpendicular (at right angles) to the direction of energy transfer. Examples: all electromagnetic waves, ripples on water, and S-waves (seismic). They have crests and troughs.

Longitudinal waves

Oscillations are parallel to the direction of energy transfer, creating compressions and rarefactions. Examples: sound waves in air and P-waves (seismic). They need a medium to travel through.

Defining a wave

  • Amplitude: maximum displacement from rest. Linked to energy carried.
  • Wavelength λ: distance for one full cycle (metres).
  • Frequency f: waves per second (hertz, Hz).
  • Period T: time for one wave, T = 1/f (seconds).

The wave equation

v = f × λ where v is wave speed (m/s), f is frequency (Hz) and λ is wavelength (m). Rearrange to f = v/λ or λ = v/f. Wave speed depends on the medium, not on amplitude.

Reflection

When a wave meets a boundary it can bounce back. The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection, both measured from the normal (the line at 90 degrees to the surface).

Refraction

When a wave crosses a boundary into a different medium its speed changes, so it bends. Slowing down bends the ray towards the normal; speeding up bends it away. Frequency stays the same; wavelength changes.

5Higher tier & triple extras

Extension content for AQA Triple (separate) Physics.

Sound and the ear

Sound is a longitudinal wave. Vibrations enter the ear canal and make the eardrum vibrate. Tiny bones pass these vibrations to the cochlea, which converts them to electrical signals. Human hearing covers about 20 Hz to 20 kHz; this range limits which sound waves we can convert.

Ultrasound

Sound above 20 kHz. It partly reflects at a boundary between media. Timing the echo gives distance, so ultrasound is used for prenatal scanning and detecting flaws in materials. It is non-ionising, so it is safer than X-rays for imaging.

Lenses

Convex (converging) lenses bring parallel rays to a real focal point and can form real or magnified virtual images. Concave (diverging) lenses spread rays so they appear to come from a virtual focal point, always forming a diminished virtual image. Magnification = image height ÷ object height.

Black-body radiation

All bodies emit and absorb infrared radiation. A perfect black body absorbs all radiation that falls on it. The hotter an object, the more energy it radiates per second and the shorter the peak wavelength. A stable temperature means radiation emitted equals radiation absorbed, which is key to Earth's energy balance.

6Exam-style questions

Try each question, then reveal the mark scheme. Show your working for calculation questions.

[3 marks]

1. A water wave has a frequency of 5 Hz and a wavelength of 0.4 m. Calculate its speed and state the equation used.

v = f × λ (1) = 5 × 0.4 (1) = 2 m/s (1).
[2 marks]

2. State one difference between a transverse and a longitudinal wave, and give an example of each.

In a transverse wave the oscillations are perpendicular to the direction of energy transfer, whereas in a longitudinal wave they are parallel (1). Example transverse: light / water ripples / S-waves. Example longitudinal: sound / P-waves (1).
[2 marks]

3. A radio station broadcasts at 100 MHz (1 × 108 Hz). EM waves travel at 3 × 108 m/s. Calculate the wavelength.

Rearrange v = fλ to λ = v / f (1) = (3 × 108) ÷ (1 × 108) = 3 m (1).
[3 marks]

4. Name the EM region used in TV remote controls, give one other use, and state one danger of overexposure.

Infrared (1). Other use: thermal imaging / cooking / optical fibre communication / heaters (1). Danger: skin burns from excessive heating (1).
[3 marks]

5. Explain why a ray of light bends as it passes from air into glass.

The light slows down as it enters the denser glass (1). Because the speed changes at the boundary, the wavelength changes while frequency stays constant, so the wave changes direction (1). It bends towards the normal on entering the denser medium (1).
[2 marks]

6. A wave has a period of 0.02 s. Calculate its frequency.

f = 1 / T (1) = 1 ÷ 0.02 = 50 Hz (1).
