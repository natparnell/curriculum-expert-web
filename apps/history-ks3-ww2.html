The Second World War

Teaching aim: to understand why the Second World War broke out, to trace its key turning points from the German advance to the defeat of the Axis, and to explore the Home Front in Britain and the place of the Holocaust within the war, building a clear overview of how the war unfolded and was won.

1Turning-points timeline: how the war was won

Step through the war event by event. For each turning point you will see what happened, why it shifted the momentum of the war, and how the balance moved between the Axis powers and the Allies. Use the buttons, the rail on the left, or your keyboard arrow keys.

    3The Home Front in Britain

    The war was not only fought by soldiers. On the Home Front, civilians faced the Blitz, children were evacuated, food was rationed, women took on war work, and the government used propaganda to keep up morale. Choose a theme to explore.

    Remembering the Holocaust

    An age-appropriate and respectful account of the Holocaust and its place in the war.

    The Holocaust was the deliberate, state-organised persecution and murder of six million Jewish men, women and children by Nazi Germany and its collaborators between 1941 and 1945. The Nazis also persecuted and murdered Roma and Sinti people, disabled people, Poles, Soviet prisoners of war, political opponents, gay people and others.

    Antisemitism, the hatred of Jewish people, was central to Nazi ideology from the start. After 1933 Jewish people in Germany were stripped of their rights, their businesses were boycotted, and their citizenship was taken away. During the war the persecution became mass murder, carried out in ghettos, by mobile killing squads, and in camps including Auschwitz-Birkenau.

    It was not a side-issue of the warThe Holocaust was bound up with the war itself. Nazi expansion across Europe brought millions of Jewish people under Nazi control, and the murders were concealed behind the fighting.
    Ordinary people were involvedThe Holocaust was not the work of a few individuals. It relied on officials, railways, soldiers and bystanders. This is why historians study how such crimes become possible.
    There was resistance and rescueJewish people resisted, for example in the Warsaw Ghetto Uprising of 1943, and some non-Jews risked their lives to hide or rescue others.
    We remember the people, not only the numberSix million is almost too large to picture. Behind the figure are individual lives, families and communities. Holocaust Memorial Day is marked on 27 January, the date Auschwitz was liberated in 1945.

    We study the Holocaust carefully and respectfully so that we understand how prejudice, when it goes unchallenged, can lead to terrible harm, and so that the victims are remembered.

    How to use this app and what to look for
    • Timeline (Section 1): click an event on the rail, or use Next and Previous, to move through the war. Watch the momentum bar under the map. Early on the needle sits well to the Axis side; from El Alamein and Stalingrad onwards it swings towards the Allies. This shows how a series of turning points, not one single battle, decided the war.
    • Causes explorer (Section 2): the sliders are deliberately open. Historians disagree about how much weight to give the Treaty of Versailles, the Great Depression, Hitler's aggression, and appeasement. Make your own judgement and be ready to justify it with evidence.
    • Home Front (Section 3): the Blitz, evacuation, rationing, women's war work and propaganda all changed daily life for civilians, including children your age.
    • Keyboard: use Tab to move between controls, Enter or Space to activate, and the left and right arrow keys when the timeline is focused to move between events.
    • A note on dates: the war in Europe ended on 8 May 1945 (VE Day) and the war against Japan on 15 August 1945 (VJ Day).
