Key teachings at a glance

The many deities are understood as aspects of the one Brahman. Use the tabs to revise the Tri-murti and tri-guna, the avatars of Vishnu, the four aims of life and the four stages of life.

Brahmathe creator

The aspect of Brahman that brings the universe into being. Not to be confused with Brahman (ultimate reality) or a brahmin (the priestly varna).

Vishnuthe preserver

Sustains and protects the order of the universe (dharma). Descends as avatars when dharma is in danger.

Shivathe destroyer

Destroys so that renewal can follow; associated with transformation and release, often shown in the cosmic dance.

Tri-gunathree qualities

All matter contains three gunas in differing balance: sattva (purity, harmony), rajas (passion, activity) and tamas (inertia, darkness).

Many Hindus are monotheistic in seeing one Brahman behind all deities; others focus devotion on a chosen deity (for example Vishnu, Shiva or the Goddess) as the supreme expression of that one reality.

Avatardescent

A form Vishnu takes on earth to restore dharma when evil grows. The avatars teach how to live a righteous life.

Rama

The ideal king and devoted husband of the Ramayana. His return from exile is celebrated at Diwali; he models duty, loyalty and self-control.

Krishna

Teacher of Arjuna in the Bhagavad Gita and a focus of loving devotion (bhakti). He explains duty, the paths to moksha and the nature of the soul.

Why avatars matter

They show Brahman as personal and active in the world, give Hindus a model of dharma to follow, and are central objects of bhakti worship.

Dharmaduty

Religious and moral duty: living rightly according to one's role, age and circumstances. The foundation of a good life.

Arthaprosperity

Earning a living and gaining wealth and security by honest, dharmic means, so as to support family and society.

Kamapleasure

Enjoyment of legitimate pleasures, love and beauty within the bounds of dharma.

Mokshaliberation

The highest aim: release of the atman from samsara and union with Brahman. The ultimate goal of human life.

Brahmacharyastudent

The stage of study, discipline and learning duty under a teacher.

Grihasthahouseholder

Marriage, raising a family, earning a living and supporting society and the other ashramas.

Vanaprastharetirement

Gradually stepping back from work and worldly duties to focus more on spiritual life.

Sannyasarenunciation

Renouncing possessions and ties to seek moksha. Together with varna, this forms varnashramadharma: duty according to one's class and stage of life.

Festivals and pilgrimage

Beliefs are also lived out through shared festivals and journeys to holy places. Use the tabs to revise two major festivals and key Hindu pilgrimages, and how each one expresses the beliefs above.

What it isfestival of lights

A five-day autumn festival marked by rows of lamps (divas), fireworks, sweets and new clothes. Homes are cleaned and lit to welcome Lakshmi, goddess of wealth and good fortune.

The story

Many Hindus recall the return of Rama and Sita from exile, the people lighting lamps to guide them home, as told in the Ramayana.

What it expresses

The victory of good over evil and light over darkness, and the importance of dharma modelled by Rama. Light symbolises knowledge overcoming ignorance.

How it is observed

Lighting divas, puja to Lakshmi, sharing food and gifts, and visiting family, mandir and community. It builds belonging within the wider Hindu community.

What it isfestival of colours

A joyful spring festival when people throw coloured powders and water, sing and dance together, and welcome the new season.

The story

It recalls the tale of Prahlad and the demoness Holika, in which devotion to Vishnu protects the faithful boy and evil is destroyed in fire; a bonfire is lit on the eve.

What it expresses

The triumph of good over evil and of devotion (bhakti), and the renewal and equality of all people, since social barriers are set aside in the play.

How it is observed

Bonfires, throwing colour, music, sweets and forgiveness of past quarrels. Stories of the playful Krishna are also linked to the celebrations.

Why pilgrimagetirtha

A tirtha is a holy crossing place between the human and the divine. Pilgrimage shows devotion, can build good karma and is believed by many to help toward moksha.

The Ganges & Varanasi

The river Ganges is sacred, often personified as the goddess Ganga. Bathing in it, especially at Varanasi, is believed to purify and to help free the atman; many wish to die there.

The Kumbh Mela

A vast gathering held by rotation at four river sites, where millions bathe at sacred confluences on auspicious dates. It is among the largest religious gatherings in the world.

What it expresses

Devotion, the longing for purification and liberation, and the unity of the Hindu community drawn together across regions and varnas.
