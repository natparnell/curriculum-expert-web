🌿 Quadrat Sampler

Teacher aim: pupils use random quadrat sampling to estimate a whole-field population from a small sample, then judge how sample size, randomness and distribution affect the reliability of their estimate.

The Meadow drag or tap to place

This 100 m by 100 m meadow is divided into a 10 by 10 grid (so 100 squares of 1 m by 1 m sampling area each, representing a 100 m² section). Drop your quadrat to count the plants inside it. A real ecologist samples at random, so use the random button, not your eyes.

Keyboard: click the meadow, then use ← → ↑ ↓ to move the quadrat, R to throw it randomly, and Enter or Space to sample the square it sits on.

How the maths works

Why we estimate: counting every plant in a whole field is slow and often impossible, so ecologists count a fair sample and scale it up.

The formula: mean number per quadrat × total number of quadrats that would fit in the whole area = population estimate. Here one quadrat covers 5 m by 5 m, the meadow is 100 m by 100 m, so 400 quadrats fit in the field.

population estimate = (total counted ÷ quadrats taken) × 400

Link: where do these plants fit in the carbon cycle?

Every green plant in your quadrat is a producer. Through photosynthesis it removes carbon dioxide from the air and locks carbon into glucose and plant tissue. When plants are eaten, respire, die or decay, that carbon is released again through respiration, decomposition and combustion. A healthy, biodiverse meadow stores more carbon, which is why protecting habitats supports the carbon cycle and slows climate change.

Photosynthesis → carbon in Respiration → carbon out Decomposers recycle nutrients More biodiversity → more stored carbon
