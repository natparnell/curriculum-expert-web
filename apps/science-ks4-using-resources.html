Using the Earth’s Resources Sustainably

Teaching aim: explore how chemistry helps us obtain potable water, manage waste, judge the true environmental cost of products through life cycle assessment, and conserve finite resources by reducing, reusing and recycling.

Life Cycle Assessment Comparator

A life cycle assessment (LCA) judges a product’s total environmental impact across four stages. Pick two products, adjust the impact factors, and read the side-by-side profile and reasoned comparison.

The four LCA stages: raw material extraction and processing; manufacture and packaging; use and operation over the product’s life; and disposal (landfill, incineration or recycling). Each stage uses energy, water and resources and produces waste and pollution. Some impacts (such as the energy used) can be measured precisely; others (such as the effect of a pollutant) require value judgements, so LCAs are not fully objective and can be biased to support a product.

Potable Water Treatment Simulator

Potable water is water that is safe to drink. It is not pure water: it still contains dissolved salts and microbes within safe limits. Choose a water source, then build the correct treatment route by clicking the stages in order.

Fresh water (from reservoirs, lakes, rivers and aquifers) is treated by passing it through filter beds to remove solids, then sterilising it to kill microbes using chlorine, ozone or ultraviolet light. Salty water (sea water) has very high levels of dissolved salts, so it must be desalinated by distillation or by reverse osmosis. These need large amounts of energy, so they are only used where there is little fresh water.

Treating waste water (sewage)

Sewage and agricultural waste water must be treated before being returned to the environment. The main stages are:

  • Screening and grit removal: large solids and grit are removed.
  • Sedimentation: the sewage settles; heavier sludge sinks to the bottom while lighter effluent floats on top.
  • Anaerobic digestion of sludge: the sludge is broken down by microorganisms without oxygen, releasing methane (a useful biogas) and producing fertiliser.
  • Aerobic biological treatment of effluent: air is bubbled through the effluent so aerobic bacteria digest organic matter and harmful microbes.

Treating waste water needs more processes than treating fresh ground water, but far less energy than the desalination of sea water.

Conserving Resources: Extract vs Recycle

Natural resources are limited. We can reduce our use of finite resources by reducing the quantity we use, reusing items, and recycling materials. Compare the cost of obtaining a metal from its ore against recycling it from scrap.

Recycling metals saves energy and limited metal ores. Scrap is sorted (often by melting) and made into new objects. The amount of separation needed depends on the metal and what the recycled material will be used for. Recycling almost always uses far less energy than extracting metal from its ore, which needs mining, transport and processes such as electrolysis or reduction.

Finite vs Renewable Resources

The Earth’s resources, together with farming, provide humans with warmth, shelter, food and transport. Chemistry plays an important role in improving agricultural and industrial processes to give us new products, and in achieving sustainable development.

Finite (non-renewable)Renewable
Fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas)Wood and other crops
Metal oresWind, solar and hydro energy
Limited and being used up faster than they formReplaced at the rate they are used, or faster
Sustainable development meets the needs of people today without harming the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. Chemists use the Earth’s resources to make useful products, and the chemical industry uses these resources to make new and improved materials such as fuels, fertilisers and alloys.

Triple Science Extras

The Haber process (making ammonia)

Ammonia (NH₃) is used to make nitrogen-based fertilisers. The raw materials are nitrogen (from the air) and hydrogen (from natural gas). They are passed over an iron catalyst:

N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g)  (reversible, exothermic forward)

  • Conditions: about 450°C, a pressure of around 200 atmospheres, and an iron catalyst.
  • Compromise temperature: a lower temperature gives a higher yield but is too slow; 450°C balances yield against rate.
  • High pressure: favours the side with fewer gas molecules (the ammonia side), so increases yield, but very high pressures are expensive and unsafe.
  • Unreacted nitrogen and hydrogen are recycled; the ammonia is cooled and removed as a liquid.

NPK fertilisers

NPK fertilisers contain compounds of the three elements nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P) and potassium (K). Ammonia can be used to make ammonium salts and nitric acid. Industrial production reacts concentrated acids with raw materials, while in the school lab the salts are made by titration and crystallisation in smaller, more careful quantities.

Corrosion and rusting

Corrosion is the destruction of a material by reaction with substances in the environment. Rusting is the corrosion of iron, which needs both water and oxygen.

  • Iron + water + oxygen → hydrated iron(III) oxide (rust).
  • Barrier methods: painting, oiling, greasing or coating with plastic keep out air and water.
  • Sacrificial protection: a more reactive metal (such as zinc or magnesium) corrodes instead of the iron. Galvanising coats iron with zinc, which acts as both a barrier and a sacrificial metal.
  • Aluminium does not corrode away because its oxide layer is strongly held and protects the metal beneath.

Alloys

Most metals in everyday use are alloys: mixtures of a metal with one or more other elements. The different-sized atoms distort the regular layers, making it harder for them to slide, so alloys are usually harder than the pure metal.

  • Bronze = copper + tin. Brass = copper + zinc.
  • Gold alloys for jewellery are measured in carats (24 carat is pure gold) or fineness.
  • Steels are alloys of iron with carbon and other metals; high-carbon steel is hard and brittle, low-carbon steel is soft and easily shaped, and stainless steel (with chromium and nickel) resists corrosion.
