Find the grid reference

Type the reference for the feature named below. Four figures locate a whole square; six figures locate a point inside it (imagine each square split into tenths).

Loading challenge.

Skill: four and six figure grid references. Highlighted target flashes on the map when you press Hint. Six figure answers are marked correct within one tenth of a square either way.

Read the relief

Brown lines are contours, each one 10 metres of height. Lines close together mean a steep slope; spread out means gentle. Answer the question, then watch the map respond.

Loading question.

Skill: interpreting contour patterns, gradient and landform shape. The relevant slope is shaded on the map when you answer.

Run a GIS enquiry

Geographers ask a question, gather data, present it, then reach a conclusion. Work through the enquiry on where to site a new visitor car park near Brackrigg Tor.

Step 1: the question. Which enquiry question is best for choosing a car park site?
Step 2: the data layers. The GIS holds four map layers. Which single layer matters most for keeping visitors safe from flooding?
Step 3: read the data. Visitor counts per square (people per day): use the figures to find the busiest square.
Step 4: the decision. Combine your layers. Which square is the best car park site: close to demand, not on the flood plain, gentle ground, near the road?
Enquiry complete.

Skill: the geographical enquiry cycle and layered GIS thinking: question, data, analysis, conclusion.
