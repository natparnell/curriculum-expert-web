Signs that a chemical reaction has happened

  • A colour change
  • A gas given off (bubbles, fizzing)
  • A temperature change
  • A precipitate (a solid forms in a liquid)
  • A new smell
  • Light or a flame produced

Key ideas explained

Physical change vs chemical change

A physical change alters how a substance looks or its state, but makes no new substance. Melting ice, dissolving sugar and boiling water are physical changes, and they can usually be reversed. A chemical change (a reaction) forms one or more new substances with new properties. Burning wood, rusting iron and mixing an acid with a metal are chemical changes, and they are usually difficult to reverse.

Signs of a chemical reaction

Look for a colour change, a gas given off, a temperature change, or a precipitate forming. A new smell, or light and heat from a flame, are also good clues. One sign on its own is not always proof, so scientists look for several.

Exothermic and endothermic reactions

An exothermic reaction transfers energy to the surroundings, so the temperature rises. Combustion (burning) and neutralisation are exothermic. An endothermic reaction takes energy in from the surroundings, so the temperature falls. Dissolving some salts, and the reaction of citric acid with sodium hydrogencarbonate, are endothermic. The energy moves between the chemicals (the system) and everything around them (the surroundings).

Conservation of mass

During a reaction the atoms are only rearranged into new combinations. No atoms are created and none are destroyed, so the total mass of the products equals the total mass of the reactants. If a reaction seems to gain or lose mass, it is usually because a gas has entered or escaped. In a sealed container the mass never changes.
