Action Potential Lab

Teacher aim: set a stimulus, fire the neurone, and watch voltage-gated Na⁺ and K⁺ channels open and close as the membrane potential is plotted live. Pupils learn why threshold is all-or-nothing, what each phase of the trace means, and how the refractory period limits firing frequency. Strand: response, homeostasis and control (nervous coordination).

Teacher notes and exam links
  • All-or-nothing: the slider lets pupils prove that stimulus size does not change the amplitude of the action potential. Once threshold (−55 mV) is reached the spike is the same size every time; below threshold nothing happens. A stronger stimulus codes for intensity by raising firing frequency, not spike height.
  • Depolarisation: voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open, Na⁺ floods in down its electrochemical gradient, membrane rushes toward about +40 mV (positive feedback).
  • Repolarisation: Na⁺ channels inactivate and voltage-gated K⁺ channels open; K⁺ leaves the axon, restoring negativity.
  • Hyperpolarisation: slow K⁺ channels overshoot below −70 mV before the sodium-potassium pump and leak channels re-establish the resting potential.
  • Refractory period: fire twice quickly to see the second spike suppressed while Na⁺ channels are still inactivated. This sets the maximum firing frequency and ensures one-way (unidirectional) propagation.
  • Propagation: the membrane diagram shows local currents depolarising the next region, moving the impulse along. (Myelination and saltatory conduction extend this idea.)
