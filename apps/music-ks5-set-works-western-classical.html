How to use this, and what the exam expects

AQA Area of Study 1 asks you to recognise the style of an era from its sound and to write about it using accurate terminology. Examiners reward precise listening: not just "it sounds old" but "terraced dynamics and a basso continuo, so this is Baroque".

Start in the Style explorer: pick an era, read its features, and play the synthesised gestures so the words attach to a sound. Then test yourself in Name that era, which plays a feature and asks you to identify both the era and the device. The Sonata form map shows the key relationships that drive a Classical first movement, and Appraising gives exam-style questions with model answers.

These are stylised illustrations of single devices, not recordings of real set works. Use them to train your ear, then apply the vocabulary to the real repertoire you study in class.

Sonata form: the structure of a Classical first movement

Expand each section to see its function and key plan. Play the tonic-then-dominant tonal journey to hear why sonata form is a story of keys.

Tonic (I) → Dominant (V) → unstable / modulating → Tonic (I)
In the exposition the music leaves the tonic for a related key (the dominant in a major-key movement). The recapitulation brings the second subject back in the tonic, resolving the tonal tension.
