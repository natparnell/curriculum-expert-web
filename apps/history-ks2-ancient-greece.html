Ancient Greece: Life, Power and Lasting Ideas

Teaching aim: to understand Greek life and achievements and their influence on the western world, by comparing the city-states of Athens and Sparta, exploring Greek gods and myths, and discovering the ideas the Ancient Greeks gave us that we still use today.

i When and where

Ancient Greece was not one country but hundreds of separate city-states (each called a polis) spread across mainland Greece and the islands of the Aegean Sea. Its golden age was roughly 2,500 years ago. The Greeks shared a language, the same gods and the Olympic Games, but each city-state ran itself and they often went to war with one another.

2 How Greek democracy worked

The word democracy comes from the Greek words demos (the people) and kratos (power), so it means "power to the people". In Athens, citizens did not vote for politicians to decide for them. They went to a great open-air meeting called the Assembly on the Pnyx hill and voted on the laws themselves. This is called direct democracy.

But it was not fair like ours. Only free adult men born in Athens counted as citizens and could vote. Women, enslaved people and foreigners could not, even though they were the majority. Around 1 in 5 Athenians could actually vote. Today in the United Kingdom almost every adult can vote, whatever their sex or background, and we choose Members of Parliament to represent us.

4 Gods, myths and legends

The Greeks believed in many gods and goddesses who lived on Mount Olympus. They told myths (stories about the gods) and legends (stories about heroes) to explain the world and to teach lessons. Choose a god to meet them.

5 Great achievements and famous wars

In just a few hundred years, the Greeks made discoveries and built things that changed the world. They also fought two famous struggles that we still study today.

🏛
Architecture: tall stone columns and temples like the Parthenon. We copy them on banks, museums and law courts.
🎭
Theatre: the first plays, both tragedies and comedies, performed in huge open-air theatres.
📐
Maths and science: Pythagoras and his triangles, Archimedes and Euclid's geometry.
💭
Philosophy: thinkers like Socrates, Plato and Aristotle asked how we should live.
⚔
Hoplites: citizen-soldiers who fought shoulder to shoulder in a wall of shields called a phalanx.
👑
Alexander the Great: by 323 BC he had spread Greek ideas across an empire reaching India.
The Persian Wars (490 to 479 BC): the huge Persian Empire twice tried to conquer Greece. At Marathon and Salamis, and at Thermopylae where 300 Spartans held a narrow pass, the Greek city-states joined together and won. These victories let Greek culture flourish into its golden age.

? Enquiry questions: think like a historian

Have a go at answering each question in your own words first, then click to reveal a historian's response.

  • Why did Athens and Sparta turn out so differently?
    Each city-state decided for itself what mattered most. Athens valued debate, trade, art and learning, so it built schools, theatres and a democracy. Sparta feared rebellion from the enslaved people it ruled, so it valued strength and obedience above all and turned itself into an army. Different choices led to two very different ways of living.
  • Was Athenian democracy really fair?
    It was a huge step forward, because for the first time ordinary citizens voted on the laws themselves. But it was not fair to everyone: women, enslaved people and foreigners had no vote, so only about one in five people could take part. We can admire the idea while also seeing how much fairer voting is today.
  • Why did the Greeks tell so many myths?
    The Greeks did not have modern science to explain thunder, the seasons or the sea, so myths gave them answers, for example Zeus throwing thunderbolts. Myths also taught lessons about pride, courage and respecting the gods, and they were exciting stories that brought communities together.
  • How do we know about Ancient Greece if it was so long ago?
    Historians use evidence. The Greeks left behind ruined temples and theatres, painted pottery, statues, coins and writing on stone and papyrus. Writers such as Herodotus recorded events. By studying these sources carefully, and noticing what is missing, historians piece the story together.
  • Why does Ancient Greece still matter to us today?
    Greek ideas are all around us: democracy and voting, the Olympic Games, the theatre, geometry in maths, the columns on important buildings, and even words like "telephone" and "alphabet". Studying Greece helps us see where many of our own ideas began.
