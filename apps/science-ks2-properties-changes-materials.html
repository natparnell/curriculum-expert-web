Why the method matters

  • Filtering separates an insoluble solid from a liquid. The filter paper has tiny holes: water passes through, but the solid grains are too big and stay behind. It works for sand and water, but not for salt water (salt dissolves and slips through).
  • Sieving separates solids of different sizes, such as large pebbles from fine sand. The big pieces cannot fit through the mesh.
  • Evaporating recovers a dissolved solid from a solution. Heating turns the water into a gas (water vapour) and leaves the solid, such as salt, behind as crystals.
  • Using a magnet separates a magnetic material, such as iron filings, from a non-magnetic one, such as sand. Only iron, steel, nickel and cobalt are attracted to the magnet.

Reversible or irreversible?

A reversible change can be undone and you get the same materials back. An irreversible change makes a new material and cannot easily be undone. Drag each change into the right box, or use the keyboard: focus a tile, press Enter to pick it up, then press R for reversible or I for irreversible.

The properties we compare at KS2

  • Hardness: how well a material resists being scratched or dented.
  • Solubility: whether it dissolves in water to form a solution.
  • Transparency: whether you can see clearly through it.
  • Electrical conductivity: whether electricity flows through it.
  • Thermal conductivity: how quickly heat passes through it.
  • Magnetism: whether a magnet attracts it.
  • Response to heat: whether heating changes it, and whether that change can be undone.
