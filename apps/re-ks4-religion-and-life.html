Religion & Life

Teaching aim: to understand and compare Christian, Hindu and non-religious (Humanist) responses to the big questions about the origins of the universe and of human life, the value of life, our responsibility for the world, and what happens after death, using accurate sources of authority and recognising diversity of belief.

Explainer

Key teachings and terms

Core ideas for Theme B, with the AQA terminology you should use in answers. Christian and Hindu teachings sit beside non-religious (Humanist) thinking so you can show diversity of belief.

The value of life and our responsibility for the world
Sanctity of life
The belief that life is holy and God-given, so it has a special value. In Christianity it rests on humans being made "in the image of God" (Genesis 1:27).
Quality of life
The idea that the value of a life depends on its wellbeing and dignity. Often weighed against sanctity of life in debates on abortion and euthanasia.
Dominion
The God-given duty to rule over creation responsibly (Genesis 1:28). It means careful authority, not the right to exploit.
Stewardship
The duty to look after the world for God and for future generations. Christians and Hindus both stress caring for creation.
Ahimsa
The Hindu (and Jain/Buddhist) principle of non-violence and not harming living things, which shapes Hindu views on animals, diet and the environment.
Sustainability
Using resources so that they are not used up and the planet is protected for the future. Linked to reducing pollution and waste.
Origins, evolution and life after death
Creation
The act of God bringing the universe into being. The Genesis narrative describes God creating in six "days"; Hindu accounts describe creation as a repeating cycle.
The Big Bang theory
The scientific explanation that the universe expanded from an extremely hot, dense state about 13.8 billion years ago.
Evolution
Charles Darwin's theory that species change over many generations through natural selection. Many believers accept it as the method God used.
Awe and wonder
The sense of amazement at the universe that, for many believers, points to a creator and sits comfortably with scientific discovery.
Resurrection
The Christian belief that, as Jesus rose from the dead, believers will be raised to eternal life and judged by God.
Reincarnation (samsara)
The Hindu belief that the atman (soul) is reborn into new bodies, with karma shaping the next life, until moksha (release) is reached.
How beliefs about origins and the afterlife shape living

Religion and science: many Christians and Hindus treat the Big Bang and evolution as describing how the universe and life developed, while their scriptures explain why and who. On this view the two are not rivals but answer different questions, so they are compatible. A minority read Genesis literally and reject parts of science (sometimes called creationism). Humanists accept the scientific account alone and see no need for a creator.

Effect on behaviour: belief in stewardship and sanctity of life encourages many believers to protect the environment, oppose waste, value the vulnerable, and act morally now because their actions matter for judgement (Christianity) or for karma and rebirth (Hinduism). Humanists ground the same care in reason, empathy and the one life we have, rather than in any afterlife.
