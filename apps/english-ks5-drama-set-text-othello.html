4. The play as a tragedy AO1 / AO3

The Aristotelian model and its limits

Aristotle's Poetics frames tragedy as the fall of a great figure through hamartia (a fatal error or flaw), producing a reversal of fortune (peripeteia), a moment of recognition (anagnorisis) and, in the audience, a purging of pity and fear (catharsis). Othello fits the shape: a noble general undone by jealousy, recognising too late that he has "loved not wisely but too well", killing himself in a final act of judgement.

But the model strains. Critics ask whether Othello's flaw is internal at all, or whether Iago manufactures it; whether a hero so manipulated retains the tragic dignity Aristotle requires; and whether reading race and gender as mere backdrop flattens what the play is actually doing. A strong AO5 answer treats the Aristotelian reading as one interpretation among several, not the truth of the text.

Iago and the audience's complicity

Iago is the play's engine. His soliloquies take the audience into his confidence so that we know more than Othello does: this is dramatic irony weaponised. We watch the trap close, knowing the handkerchief is innocent, knowing Cassio's "confession" is about Bianca. Some critics argue this makes the audience complicit, even seduced, by Iago's wit, a discomfort the play deliberately stages.

His method is language: insinuation, the half-finished sentence, the imagery of the monstrous and the bestial ("an old black ram is tupping your white ewe") that he plants in Brabantio's and then Othello's mind, until Othello's own verse decays from the music of Act 1 into the broken, animal prose of his collapse.
