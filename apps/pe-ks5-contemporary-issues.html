Theory: commercialisation and the golden triangle

Commercialisation is the use of sport to make money. It is driven by the three interdependent commercial forces known as the golden triangle: sport, sponsorship and the media. Each force relies on the other two, and a change in one ripples through the others.

Sport
Provides the spectacle and the audience. It sells broadcasting rights to the media and offers sponsors a platform and a fan base to reach.
Sponsorship and business
Provides money in return for advertising, brand association and audience reach. Sponsors follow the media coverage, so heavily televised sports attract the largest deals.
The media
Provides coverage and revenue through broadcasting rights, and delivers the audience that sponsors pay for. The media can also dictate kick-off times, rules and formats to suit broadcasting.

Positive consequences

More money for facilities, wages and grassroots; greater global exposure; improved spectator experience; professionalisation of athletes.

Negative consequences

Loss of control to broadcasters and sponsors; fixture changes that disadvantage loyal fans; minority sports squeezed out; pressure on players to perform and to behave as brand ambassadors; widening inequality between rich and poor clubs.

Theory: ethics, deviance and doping
Sportsmanship
Conforming to the unwritten rules and spirit of the game: fair play, respect for opponents and officials, and conventions such as kicking the ball out when a player is injured.
Gamesmanship
Bending the rules and using questionable but not strictly illegal tactics to gain an advantage, for example time-wasting, sledging or feigning injury. It stretches the spirit of the game without breaking the written rules.
Deviance
Behaviour that breaks the written rules and the accepted norms of sport. Positive deviance is over-conformity (training while injured); negative deviance is under-conformity or rule-breaking (doping, match-fixing, violence).

Why athletes dope

Pressure to win and to earn; the rewards of success; the belief that others are doping; physiological gains (strength, endurance, recovery, masking pain); coercion from coaches or regimes; and the perceived low chance of being caught.

Drug testing: for and against

For: protects athlete health, upholds fair competition, deters cheating, protects sport's image and role models. Against: high cost, intrusion into privacy (whereabouts rules), testers always behind new substances and masking agents, risk of false positives and inadvertent doping.

Violence in sport

By players: caused by the importance of winning, pressure from coaches and crowds, frustration, retaliation and the nature of contact sports. By spectators: caused by tribalism, hooliganism, alcohol, frustration, poor stewarding and the emotional intensity of rivalries.

Theory: the impact of technology

Categories of sport technology

Officiating aids (Hawk-Eye, VAR, goal-line technology, the television match official) improve the accuracy of decisions. Performance analysis (GPS vests, motion capture, video analysis, data analytics) informs coaching and tactics. Equipment and clothing advances (carbon fibre, aerodynamic suits, lighter footwear) raise performance. Fitness and recovery technology (cryotherapy, hypoxic chambers, wearables, hydration monitoring) supports training and rehabilitation.

Evaluation

Technology can improve fairness, safety, performance and the spectator experience, but it can also slow the game, undermine the authority of officials, raise costs that widen inequality between rich and poor, and erode the human, unpredictable character of sport.
