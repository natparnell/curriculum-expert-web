The hot desert ecosystem

Climate

Hot deserts lie around 30° north and south of the Equator. They are very hot by day (often above 40°C) and cold at night because clear skies allow heat to escape. Rainfall is below 250 mm a year and very unreliable.

Drives the whole system: little water limits the soils, plants and animals that can survive.

Soils

Desert soils are thin, sandy and low in nutrients and organic matter because there are few plants to add humus. They are often salty (saline) where water evaporates at the surface, leaving salts behind.

Poor soils support only sparse plants, which in turn keeps the soil thin and easily eroded.

Plants and animals

Biodiversity is low because few species can cope with the heat and water shortage. The organisms present are highly specialised, with adaptations such as deep roots, water storage and being active only at night.

Each part depends on the others: this interdependence means damage to one part affects the whole ecosystem.

Interdependence

Climate, water, soils, plants and animals form a delicate, finely balanced web. Plants stabilise soils and provide food; animals spread seeds and recycle nutrients; water controls everything.

Because the balance is fragile, small changes such as overgrazing can quickly tip land towards desertification.

Case study: the Thar Desert, India

Location: NW India & Pakistan Area: ~200,000 km² Rainfall: 120–240 mm/yr One of the most densely populated deserts on Earth

▲ Development opportunities

Mineral extraction

Gypsum, limestone, phosphate and feldspar are mined; the area also has reserves of marble used in construction.

Energy

Over 320 sunny days a year power solar farms (such as Bhaleri); strong winds drive the Jaisalmer wind park; coal and oil reserves are also exploited.

Farming

The Indira Gandhi Canal brings water for irrigation, allowing crops such as wheat and cotton to be grown on previously barren land.

Tourism

Desert safaris on camels from Jaisalmer and the annual Desert Festival bring income and create jobs.

▼ Development challenges

Extreme temperatures

Summer highs reach 50°C, making outdoor work, farming and tourism difficult and increasing water demand.

Water supply

Rainfall is low and unreliable; the canal loses water through evaporation and causes salinisation of soils through over-irrigation.

Inaccessibility

Sparse roads, shifting sand and great distances make it hard and costly to move people, goods and machinery.

Desertification on the desert fringe

What is happening

Desertification is the spread of desert-like conditions into semi-arid land on the fringe of hot deserts. Fertile land degrades until it can no longer support vegetation, and soils blow or wash away.

Around the world about one billion people are at risk from desertification, mainly in semi-arid regions such as the Sahel.

Why it matters

Once vegetation is lost there are no roots to hold the soil, so soil erosion accelerates and the land becomes even less able to recover. The strategies in the model above tackle the causes rather than the symptoms.
