Evolution & Inheritance

Teaching aim: to understand that living things have changed over time, that offspring vary from their parents, and that animals and plants adapted to their environment can change across many generations through natural selection.

What is happening? On pale bark, light moths are camouflaged and dark moths are easy for birds to spot, so more light moths survive to have young. Over many generations the population shifts. This is natural selection, and over very long times it leads to evolution.

Fossils: clues from the deep past

Layers of rock build up over millions of years, with the oldest at the bottom. Fossils trapped in the rock tell us about living things from long ago and show that life has changed over time. Tap a layer to explore.

Tap a rock layer above to find out what kind of fossils were left behind, and how scientists read the rocks like a storybook of life.
How fossils form: when an animal or plant dies, it can be buried in mud or sand. Over millions of years the soft parts rot away and the hard parts, like bones or shells, slowly turn to rock. Because deeper layers are older, fossils show that the kinds of living things on Earth have changed over a very long time.

Check what you know

1. On sooty, dark tree bark, which moths are most likely to survive and have young?

2. A puppy looks similar to its parents but is not identical. What do we call these small differences?

3. In a stack of rock layers, where are the oldest fossils usually found?

The big idea: living things produce offspring of the same kind, but those offspring vary. Some variations help an animal or plant suit its environment better (this is adaptation). Those individuals tend to survive and pass their helpful features on. Over very many generations the whole population changes. That slow change is evolution, and fossils give us the evidence that it has happened over millions of years.
