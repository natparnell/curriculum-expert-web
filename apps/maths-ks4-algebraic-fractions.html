    How algebraic fractions work (and the traps to avoid)

    An algebraic fraction is just a fraction whose numerator and denominator contain algebra. The same rules you know for ordinary fractions apply, but you must factorise first so that you can see the common factors.

    Simplifying

    Factorise the numerator and the denominator fully, then cancel any factor that appears in both. For example (x²-9)/(x²+7x+12) factorises to (x-3)(x+3) / (x+3)(x+4). The factor (x+3) appears top and bottom, so it cancels, leaving (x-3)/(x+4).

    Adding and subtracting

    Put both fractions over a common denominator (usually the product of the two denominators, or their lowest common multiple if they share a factor), combine the numerators, then simplify. Expand carefully when subtracting: the minus sign applies to every term in the second numerator.

    Multiplying and dividing

    To multiply, factorise everything and cancel any factor common to a numerator and a denominator, then multiply across. To divide, flip the second fraction (multiply by its reciprocal), then proceed as for multiplication. This is "keep, change, flip".

    Solving equations with fractions

    Multiply every term by a common denominator to clear the fractions, then solve the resulting linear or quadratic equation. Always state any value of x that would make a denominator zero: those values are not allowed.

    The biggest trap

    You can only cancel factors, never individual terms. In (x+2)/(x+3) nothing cancels, because the x's are terms added to other things, not factors multiplying the whole numerator and denominator. Cancelling them would be like turning (2+5)/(2+7) into 5/7, which is plainly false.
