How to read it: the difference between reactants and products is the overall energy change (ΔH). If the products sit lower the reaction is exothermic (ΔH negative, surroundings warm up); if the products sit higher it is endothermic (ΔH positive, surroundings cool down). The height from reactants up to the peak is the activation energy: the minimum energy needed to start the reaction. A catalyst provides an alternative route with a lower activation energy, so more particles have enough energy to react. It is unchanged at the end and does not alter ΔH.

2 Exothermic and endothermic reactions

Energy is transferred to or from the surroundings, which we usually detect as a temperature change.

Exothermic

Energy released → surroundings warm up

The temperature of the surroundings rises. ΔH is negative. Everyday examples: combustion (burning fuels), neutralisation, oxidation, many displacement reactions, and self-heating hand warmers.

Endothermic

Energy taken in → surroundings cool down

The temperature of the surroundings falls. ΔH is positive. Examples: thermal decomposition (e.g. heating metal carbonates), the reaction of citric acid with sodium hydrogencarbonate, and sports injury cold packs.

Required practical

Measuring temperature change

Mix known volumes of reactants in an insulated polystyrene cup, record the temperature with a thermometer and find the maximum change. A rise indicates exothermic; a fall indicates endothermic.

3 Bond energy calculator

4 Cells, batteries and fuel cells

A chemical reaction can be made to transfer energy as electricity. This is the chemistry of cells (triple science).

Cells & batteries

Voltage from chemistry

A simple cell produces a potential difference when two different metals are connected by an electrolyte. The bigger the difference in reactivity of the two metals, the larger the voltage. A battery is two or more cells connected in series, adding their voltages.

Rechargeable vs not

Can the reaction reverse?

In non-rechargeable cells the reactants are used up and the reaction cannot be reversed. In rechargeable cells the chemical reactions are reversed when an external electrical current is supplied.

Hydrogen fuel cell

Continuous supply of energy

Hydrogen and oxygen are supplied continuously and react to produce a voltage with only water as the product: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O. Unlike a battery it does not run down or need recharging while fuel is supplied.

Evaluation

Fuel cells vs rechargeable cells

Fuel cells produce only water at the point of use and keep working while fuelled, but hydrogen is hard to store and is often made from fossil fuels. Rechargeable batteries are convenient but store less energy per kilogram and degrade over many charge cycles.
