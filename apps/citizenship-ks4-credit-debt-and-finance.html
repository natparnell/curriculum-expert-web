2What is APR, and what does each credit type cost?

APR (Annual Percentage Rate) shows the yearly cost of borrowing, including interest and most fees. The higher the APR, the more you pay. It lets you compare like with like.

Overdraft Borrowing on your current account. Arranged overdrafts now around 35% to 40% APR; unarranged can be far worse.
Credit card Flexible borrowing, typically around 24% APR. Cheap if cleared each month; costly if only the minimum is paid.
Store card A card for one shop, often around 29% APR. Usually dearer than a normal credit card.
Personal loan A fixed sum repaid over a set term, often around 10% APR for good credit. A relatively cheap way to borrow a larger amount.
Hire purchase Pay for goods (often a car) in instalments; you do not own them until the last payment. Often around 13% APR.
Payday loan Short-term, very high cost borrowing. APR can be around 1,000% or more, even with the FCA cap on charges. Avoid where possible.
Mortgage A long-term loan to buy property, secured against the home, often around 5% APR. Low rate but huge total interest over decades.

3Gross vs net pay: where does your money go?

Your gross pay is what you earn before deductions. Your net pay (take-home) is what lands in your account after Income Tax and National Insurance. Move the slider to see a rough split.

Taxes fund shared public services: the NHS, state schools, roads, police, defence, and pensions and benefits. National Insurance helps pay for the State Pension and the NHS.

4Monthly budget balancer: stay in the black

You have a set take-home each month. Share it across needs, wants and savings. Spend more than you have and you slip into the red. A common rule of thumb is roughly 50% needs, 30% wants, 20% savings.

5Saving: simple vs compound interest

Interest is what the bank pays you for saving (or charges you for borrowing). With simple interest you earn only on what you put in. With compound interest you also earn interest on past interest, so savings grow faster over time.

An emergency fund (often three to six months of essential costs) is money set aside for surprises like a broken boiler or losing work, so you do not have to borrow at high cost.

7How debt builds up, and where to get help

Debt can snowball when you only pay the minimum, miss payments (which add fees and interest), or borrow to repay other borrowing. High-APR debt grows fastest.

Ways to manage and avoid problem debt:

  • Always pay at least the minimum and aim for more.
  • Pay off the highest-APR debt first.
  • Build an emergency fund so you do not have to borrow.
  • Tell lenders early if you are struggling: most can help.
  • Never use a new high-cost loan to pay an old one.

Free, independent help in the UK

  • MoneyHelper (government-backed money guidance).
  • Citizens Advice (free debt and consumer advice).
  • StepChange and National Debtline (free debt charities).

Free advice is always better than paying a firm that promises to "fix" your debt.
