🌞Shadow Studio

Light, straight lines and shadows · KS2 Science

Teaching aim: Pupils see that light travels in straight lines, so moving the Sun changes a shadow's length and direction. The sundial challenge links shadow direction to the time of day. Demonstration use only.

Drag the Sun around the sky. Watch the tree's shadow change. The dotted line is a light ray travelling in a straight line.

Slides the Sun from the east side of the sky to the west side.
Raises or lowers the Sun above the horizon.

What the shadow is doing

Shadow direction: west
Shadow length: very long
Sun height: low
Drag the Sun, or click it and use the arrow keys. Notice the shadow always points away from the Sun.

Why this happens

  • Light travels in straight lines from the Sun.
  • The tree blocks some light, so a dark shadow forms behind it.
  • A low Sun makes long shadows; a high Sun makes short shadows.
  • The shadow always falls on the opposite side to the Sun.

🕰 Sundial challenge

A sundial tells the time using a shadow. Move the Sun so the gnomon's shadow points to the target time. In the morning the Sun is in the east, so the shadow points west. At noon the Sun is high, so the shadow is short and points north.

Sundial facts

  • The shadow always points away from the Sun.
  • Morning: Sun in the east, shadow points west.
  • Midday: Sun high in the south, shadow short and points north.
  • Afternoon: Sun in the west, shadow points east.
