How proof works: structure, language and rigorous notation

A mathematical proof is a watertight chain of reasoning. Each line must follow from earlier lines, from a definition, or from an established result. Examiners reward the logical structure and the precise language, not just the final answer.

The four methods

  • Deduction: argue directly from definitions and known facts to the conclusion. A typical move is to write a general object algebraically, for example an even number as 2n or an odd number as 2n + 1.
  • Exhaustion: split the statement into a small, finite set of cases and verify every single one. Valid only when the cases are genuinely exhaustive.
  • Disproof by counterexample: to disprove a universal claim ("for all ..."), you need just one example where it fails.
  • Contradiction: assume the negation of what you want to prove, deduce something impossible, and conclude the assumption was false.

The shape of a proof by contradiction

This is the structure examiners look for, in order:

  • Assume the negation: "Assume, for contradiction, that ... ".
  • Reason validly from that assumption.
  • Reach a contradiction: a statement that cannot be true (for example a = b and a ≠ b).
  • Conclude: "This contradiction shows the assumption was false, therefore ... ".
