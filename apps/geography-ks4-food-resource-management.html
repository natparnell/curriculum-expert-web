The global picture

Surplus, deficit and rising demand

Distribution of surplus and deficit

Food supply is unevenly distributed across the world. Higher income countries (HICs) such as those in North America and Western Europe generally have a food surplus, producing more than they consume. Many lower income countries (LICs), especially across sub-Saharan Africa and parts of South Asia, run a food deficit, consuming more than they can reliably produce.

Why food consumption is rising

  • Economic development: as incomes rise, diets change towards more meat, dairy and processed food, which need more land, water and energy to produce.
  • Rising population: a larger global population needs more calories overall, increasing total demand year on year.

Strategies in detail: pros and cons

Large-scale agricultural development: the Indus Basin Irrigation System, Pakistan

An example of a large-scale agricultural development

The Indus Basin Irrigation System is one of the largest integrated irrigation networks in the world. A series of dams, barrages, link canals and distributary channels diverts water from the River Indus and its tributaries across the arid Punjab and Sindh plains, turning land that would otherwise be too dry to farm into highly productive cropland for wheat, rice, cotton and sugar cane.

Advantages: a large, reliable rise in food supply; jobs in farming and food processing; hydroelectric power from major dams; and export earnings from cash crops that support economic development.

Disadvantages: over-abstraction has reduced flow to the Indus delta; waterlogging and salinisation have damaged soils; water-sharing tensions exist between regions; and the heavy use of irrigation water is unsustainable as the climate warms and rainfall becomes less reliable.

Looking ahead

Moving towards a sustainable resource future

Permaculture

Designing farming systems that copy natural ecosystems: companion planting, crop rotation, composting and managing water carefully so the land stays productive without heavy chemical inputs.

Urban farming initiatives

Growing food in cities using allotments, rooftop gardens, community plots and vertical farms. This cuts food miles, uses unused space and reconnects urban people with food production.

Sustainable sourcing

Buying seasonal and local food, choosing sustainably sourced fish and produce, and reducing food waste and loss along the whole supply chain to lower the environmental footprint of what we eat.
