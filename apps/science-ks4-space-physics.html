Space Physics

Teaching aim: understand how stars are born, live and die; how the elements are forged inside them; why a faster orbit means a smaller radius; and how the red shift of distant galaxies provides evidence for the Big Bang and an expanding universe.

Build a star

M☉ means "solar masses": the mass of our Sun. Below about 8 M☉ a star follows the Sun-like branch; above it, the massive-star branch.

Life cycle of a star

Nebula

A cloud of gas (mostly hydrogen) and dust. Press Evolve to begin.

The key balance. On the main sequence the outward pressure from nuclear fusion balances the inward pull of gravity, so the star is stable. When the fuel needed to sustain fusion runs low, gravity wins and the star changes dramatically. What happens next depends almost entirely on the star's mass.

Orbit simulator

Gravity provides the centripetal force that keeps the planet in orbit. For a stable circular orbit, a smaller radius needs a faster speed; a larger radius means a slower speed.

The physics. Setting gravitational force equal to the centripetal force gives v = √(GM/r). The speed therefore depends only on the central mass and the radius: as r goes up, v goes down (an inverse-square-root relationship). Try the slider, drag it to the smallest radius and watch the planet whip round quickly, just as Mercury orbits faster than Neptune.

Red shift demonstrator

More distant galaxies recede faster (Hubble's law: v ≈ H0 × d). The faster a galaxy moves away, the more its light is stretched towards the red end of the spectrum.

Evidence for the Big Bang. Light from almost every distant galaxy is red-shifted, and the most distant galaxies show the largest red shift, so they are moving away fastest. This is the same in every direction: the whole universe is expanding. Tracing the expansion backwards points to a single hot, dense beginning, the Big Bang.

The solar system

Our solar system formed about 4.6 billion years ago. It contains:

The Sun
A main-sequence star; the only star in our solar system.
Eight planets
Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars (rocky); Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune (gas/ice giants).
Dwarf planets
Such as Pluto and Ceres; orbit the Sun but have not cleared their orbit.
Moons (natural satellites)
Orbit planets, for example our Moon and Jupiter's Galilean moons.
Asteroids
Rocky bodies, mostly between Mars and Jupiter.
Comets
Icy bodies on long, elliptical orbits.

A planet orbits a star; a moon (natural satellite) orbits a planet. Our Sun, and all the other stars, formed from a cloud of dust and gas pulled together by gravity (a nebula).

How the elements form

  • During the main sequence, fusion in a star joins hydrogen nuclei to make helium, releasing energy.
  • In more massive stars, fusion continues, building heavier elements up to and including iron. Fusion cannot release energy by making elements heavier than iron.
  • Elements heavier than iron (such as gold, lead and uranium) are produced only in the enormous energies of a supernova.
  • The supernova explosion also distributes these elements through space. The atoms in your body that are heavier than iron were forged in an exploding star.
Iron is the turning point. Fusing light nuclei up to iron releases energy and supports the star. Beyond iron, fusion would absorb energy, so it cannot sustain the star. That is why iron marks the end of normal stellar fusion and why heavier elements need a supernova.
