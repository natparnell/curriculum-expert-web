Exchange & Transport in Animals and Plants

Teaching aim: explain why larger organisms need specialised exchange surfaces, and model the gas exchange and mass transport systems that supply them, with a focus on the oxygen dissociation curve and the Bohr effect.

At the lungs the high pO₂ loads haemoglobin to roughly 95 to 98% saturation. At respiring tissue the low pO₂ and high pCO₂ mean haemoglobin holds far less oxygen, so it unloads to supply aerobic respiration.

Why sigmoid?

Binding the first O₂ changes haemoglobin's shape (cooperative binding), making the next O₂ easier to bind. This gives the steep middle section.

The Bohr effect

High pCO₂ lowers pH; haemoglobin's affinity for O₂ falls, so the curve shifts right. More O₂ is released exactly where respiration is fastest.

3 marks Explain how the Bohr effect helps to supply oxygen to actively respiring muscle tissue.

Mark points:
  • Active tissue respires faster, producing more carbon dioxide / a higher pCO₂.
  • This lowers the pH, reducing haemoglobin's affinity for oxygen.
  • The dissociation curve shifts to the right, so at a given pO₂ the saturation is lower and more oxygen is released (dissociated) to the tissue for aerobic respiration.

Surface area to volume ratio explorer

Diffusion across the surface supplies the whole volume. As an organism gets larger its volume rises faster than its surface area, so the SA:V ratio falls. Below a certain size, simple diffusion can no longer supply the centre fast enough, so larger organisms need specialised exchange surfaces and a transport system.

A small cube has a high SA:V ratio, so every part of the volume is close to the surface and diffusion alone supplies it.

Features of efficient exchange surfaces

Large surface area, thin (short diffusion distance), permeable, and a steep concentration gradient maintained by a transport medium (blood) or ventilation.

Why size matters

SA scales with length squared but volume scales with length cubed. The metabolic demand rises with volume, so larger organisms need extra surface area for exchange.

2 marks Explain why a single-celled organism such as Amoeba does not need a specialised gas exchange surface.

Mark points:
  • It has a large surface area to volume ratio.
  • The diffusion distance to the centre of the cell is short, so gases can be exchanged fast enough across the cell-surface membrane by diffusion alone to meet its metabolic demand.

During diastole the heart muscle relaxes and blood flows into the atria and ventricles. The atrioventricular (AV) valves are open and the semilunar (SL) valves are closed.

Double circulation

Blood passes through the heart twice per circuit: the right side pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs (pulmonary), the left side pumps oxygenated blood to the body (systemic) at higher pressure.

Why the left wall is thicker

The left ventricle generates the higher pressure needed to push blood around the whole body, so it has a thicker, more muscular wall than the right ventricle.

3 marks Using pressure changes, explain why the semilunar valves close at the end of ventricular systole.

Mark points:
  • The ventricle relaxes, so ventricular pressure falls.
  • Aortic (arterial) pressure becomes higher than ventricular pressure.
  • The higher pressure in the aorta pushes the semilunar valves shut, preventing backflow of blood into the ventricle.

Gas exchange surfaces

Compare organisms

Different organisms solve the same problem in different ways. Select an organism to see its exchange surface and the gradient that drives diffusion.

4 marks Explain the advantage of a counter-current flow in fish gills compared with a concurrent (parallel) flow.

Mark points:
  • Water and blood flow in opposite directions across the gill lamellae.
  • A concentration (diffusion) gradient is maintained along the whole length of the lamella.
  • So blood can become oxygenated even as it nears the oxygen-rich incoming water; with parallel flow, equilibrium is reached and diffusion stops part-way along.
  • Roughly 80% of the oxygen in the water is absorbed, far more than parallel flow allows.

Transport in plants

Xylem & phloem

Plants move water and assimilates over long distances. The transpiration stream pulls water up the xylem; translocation moves sucrose through the phloem from source to sink. Adjust the conditions and see how transpiration rate responds.

Cohesion-tension

Water evaporates from mesophyll cells and leaves through stomata. This creates tension; cohesion between water molecules (hydrogen bonds) pulls a continuous column up the xylem.

Mass flow in phloem

Sucrose is actively loaded at the source, lowering water potential so water enters by osmosis, raising hydrostatic pressure. The pressure gradient drives flow to the sink, where sucrose is unloaded.

3 marks Explain why an increase in air movement (wind) increases the rate of transpiration.

Mark points:
  • Moving air removes water vapour from around the stomata, so it does not build up.
  • This maintains a steep water potential (concentration) gradient between the air spaces in the leaf and the air outside.
  • Water vapour therefore diffuses out faster, increasing the transpiration rate.
