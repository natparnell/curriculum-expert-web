KS5 Computing · Consequences and ethics of computing

Algorithm on Trial

Teacher aim: students interrogate a biased automated hiring system, manipulate its training data and decision rules, observe the effect on fairness metrics, then debate where legal and moral accountability sits. Strand: legal, moral and cultural consequences of computing, AI bias and emerging technologies.

01 The case before the court

A fictional firm, Northgate Logistics, uses an algorithm called SortHire to filter applicants for warehouse team-leader roles. Rejected candidates have complained. Your job: examine SortHire, find the bias, fix what you can, and decide who is answerable.

How SortHire scores an applicant

Each applicant gets a score out of 100, built from weighted factors. Anyone scoring at or above the shortlist threshold is auto-shortlisted; everyone else is auto-rejected with no human review. SortHire was trained on ten years of Northgate's own past hiring decisions.

The catch: Northgate's past managers tended to hire people who lived nearby and who had unbroken work histories. SortHire learned those patterns, including the ones nobody intended it to learn.

02 Interrogate and adjust the rules

Drag the sliders to reweight each factor and use the toggles to change how the data is handled. Every change instantly re-scores all eight applicants below and recalculates the fairness metrics. Aim for a system that is both effective and fair.

Skills assessment score40%

A practical, job-relevant test. Generally the fairest signal of who can do the work.

Distance from depot25%

Rewards living close by. Sounds practical, but postcode strongly tracks ethnicity and income, so it acts as a proxy for protected characteristics.

Unbroken work history25%

Penalises career gaps. This disadvantages carers and people who took maternity or health leave (indirect discrimination).

Relevant experience10%

Years in similar roles. Useful, but can entrench who has already had a foot in the door.

05 Who is accountable?

SortHire has rejected a capable candidate because of a biased rule. Under English law and professional ethics, who carries responsibility? Pick the answer you would defend, then read the verdict. There is a strongest answer, but several are partly right.

Legal and ethical reference (England)

Equality Act 2010: indirect discrimination occurs when a neutral-looking rule (for example, a distance score) puts people sharing a protected characteristic at a particular disadvantage and cannot be objectively justified. An employer remains liable even if a third party built the tool.

UK GDPR / Data Protection Act 2018, Article 22: individuals have the right not to be subject to a solely automated decision with significant effects, and the right to obtain human intervention. A pure auto-reject with no review is legally exposed.

Professional ethics: the BCS Code of Conduct requires members to have due regard to public interest, legitimate rights and avoidance of discrimination. Building or deploying an unaudited biased system breaches that duty regardless of who wrote the data.
