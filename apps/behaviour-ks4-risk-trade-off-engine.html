Know the facts

Open each one. The engine is built on the same ideas.

Risk is reward times harm times likelihood, not just "bad"

A sensible way to weigh a decision is to ask three things: how much do I gain, how likely is something to go wrong, and how serious would it be if it did. A small chance of a serious harm (for example getting into a stranger's car) can outweigh a large, certain reward. Lowering any one of the three factors makes the whole decision safer.

Alcohol and the developing brain

UK Chief Medical Officers advise that an alcohol-free childhood is the healthiest option, and that if young people do drink it should not be before 15. The teenage brain is still developing into the mid-twenties, so the effects of alcohol on judgement, memory and risk-taking are stronger than in adults.

Vaping and nicotine

It is illegal to sell vapes or e-cigarettes to anyone under 18 in the UK. Most vapes contain nicotine, which is addictive and can affect the developing teenage brain, including concentration and mood. "Just trying it" is how most nicotine dependence starts.

Unknown pills and dosage

You can never be sure what is in a pill bought from someone, even a friend. Strength varies enormously between batches, so the same "one pill" can have very different effects. Mixing substances (for example alcohol plus a stimulant) sharply increases the danger. If someone becomes unwell, calling 999 and telling the truth about what was taken saves lives.

Peer pressure and saying no

Most young people overestimate how many of their peers drink, vape or take drugs. Having a planned exit line ("I'm good, thanks", "my coach tests us", "I've got football early") makes refusing easier in the moment. A trusted adult or friend who knows your plan is one of the strongest protective factors there is.

Personal safety at night

Simple plans reduce risk a lot: tell someone your route and expected time, keep your phone charged, stay in well-lit areas, and travel with others where you can. None of these remove your right to be safe; they are just sensible ways to lower the chance and seriousness of harm.
