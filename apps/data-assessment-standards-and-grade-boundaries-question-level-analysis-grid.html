Question-Level Analysis Grid

Teacher aim: read a mock paper as a heatmap of marks by question and by pupil, see how the grade distribution shifts, and let the app rank exactly which topics to reteach first.

1Performance heatmap

Demonstration cohort: Year 11 Foundation Mathematics mock. Each cell shows marks earned out of the marks available for that question. Tap, click, or arrow-key onto a cell for targeted feedback.

Question-level analysis grid. Rows are pupils, columns are questions. Cells show marks scored out of marks available, colour-coded by performance.

Use Tab to enter the grid, then arrow keys to move between cells. Press Enter to read detailed feedback for the highlighted cell.

Mark gained: 0 to 24% 25 to 49% 50 to 69% 70 to 89% 90 to 100%
ReadySelect any cell, question header, or pupil name for instant analysis. The class column totals along the bottom row show which questions caught the cohort out.

2Grade distribution and boundaries

Total marks are mapped onto illustrative Foundation grade boundaries (out of 80). Drag the slider to model what a boundary change would do to the cohort, then watch the distribution and the borderline list update live.

Moving this slider raises or lowers the grade 4 boundary to show how many pupils cross the pass line.

3Reteaching priority list

The app ranks topics by class mark gained, weighted by how many marks they carry. The lowest-scoring, highest-stakes topics rise to the top. This is generated from the grid above, not pre-written.

    How to use this with a class (teacher notes)

    Reading the grid: warm colours (red, orange) mean few marks gained, cool colours (green) mean strong performance. Scan down a column to find a question the whole class struggled with (a teaching gap) versus scan across a row to find a pupil who struggled broadly (a pupil gap).

    Keyboard: press Tab to reach the grid, then ← → ↑ ↓ to move and Enter to read a cell. Every selection is announced for screen readers.

    Caution: boundary modelling here is illustrative only. Real awarding-body boundaries are set after every paper and vary year to year. Use this to discuss the principle, not to predict a real grade. All names and marks are fictional.
