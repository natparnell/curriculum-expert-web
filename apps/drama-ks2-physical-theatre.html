✨ Drama prompts to try

Build these as freeze-frame sequences. Work as an ensemble: every body on the stage adds meaning.

The journey

Show a traveller setting off, facing a storm, growing tired, then arriving safely. Use levels to show effort.

The rescue

One figure is trapped or afraid; another comes to help. How can your bodies show who needs help and who is brave?

The celebration

Start small and still, then build to a joyful, high-energy freeze-frame. Let the levels rise as the feeling grows.

The argument and the making-up

Two figures turn away, then slowly turn back. Show the change of feeling with only posture and face.

📖 For the teacher: what is physical theatre, and why does it matter?

Physical theatre tells stories using the body rather than words. The actor's posture (the shape of the whole body), gesture (what the hands and arms do), facial expression, level (high, medium or low) and use of space all carry meaning for the audience.

A freeze-frame (also called a still image or tableau) is a frozen moment, like pressing pause on a film. Pupils hold a shape that captures a feeling or a key moment. Sequencing several freeze-frames and moving between them creates a short physical-theatre piece.

What the choices communicate:

  • Level: a high level can suggest power, hope, joy or status; a low level can suggest fear, sadness, weakness or hiding. Differences in level between figures show who has importance or control.
  • Space and distance: figures placed close together can show a bond or a confrontation; figures placed far apart can show loneliness or conflict.
  • Body shape and gesture: open, tall shapes read as confident; curled, closed shapes read as frightened or sad.
  • Mime: showing an action clearly with no props builds precise, controlled movement and trains the audience to read the body.

This sits within the English National Curriculum at Key Stage 2: pupils participate in and gain knowledge, skills and understanding associated with the artistic practice of drama, adopting, creating and sustaining roles, and working as an ensemble. Movement and mime give every child a way into drama, including those who find speaking aloud difficult.

Questions to ask pupils: What is this figure feeling? How do you know from the body alone? Whose freeze-frame shows the most important person, and how? What changes between frame one and frame two?
