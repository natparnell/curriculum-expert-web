Projectile and Moments Lab

Teacher aim: students resolve velocity into components, predict and check projectile range, then balance a loaded beam by reasoning about moments and centre of mass. Three live experiments: Projectile launcher, Balance beam and a Stress and strain tester for Young modulus.

Set a speed and angle, then press Launch. Watch how vx stays constant while vy reverses.

Working it through

R = v² sin(2θ) / g   ·   T = 2 v sinθ / g   ·   H = (v sinθ)² / 2g

The horizontal component never changes (no horizontal force). The vertical component is decelerated, stops at the peak, then accelerates back down. Maximum range for a fixed speed is always at 45°, and any two angles that add to 90° give the same range.

Balance the beam

Net moment: 0 N m

Drag a mass left or right along the beam, or change its size. The pivot is fixed at the centre. The beam balances when the total clockwise moment equals the total anticlockwise moment about the pivot. Keyboard: focus a mass and use ← → to move it.

Move a mass and watch the moments update. Aim for a net moment of zero.

Working it through

moment = F × d   (F = m g,   d = distance from pivot)

The beam is in equilibrium when Σ clockwise moments = Σ anticlockwise moments. The centre of mass is the single point where the whole weight could act; if it sits exactly over the pivot, the beam balances.

Working it through

E = σ / ε = (F / A) / (x / L)

In the elastic (straight) region the wire obeys Hooke's law and the gradient is constant: that gradient is the Young modulus. A thinner wire has smaller area A, so the same load gives a larger stress and more strain.
