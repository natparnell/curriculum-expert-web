Constitutional monarchy: what the monarch can and cannot do

In a constitutional monarchy the monarch is head of state but acts within strict limits and on the advice of ministers. This is very different from an absolute monarchy, where the monarch holds personal political power.

The UK monarch (constitutional)

  • Can formally open Parliament at the State Opening, reading a speech written by the government.
  • Can grant Royal Assent, the final step that turns a bill into law (by convention, never refused since 1708).
  • Can appoint the Prime Minister, but by convention this is the leader who can command a majority in the Commons.
  • Cannot make laws, set taxes or choose government policy.
  • Cannot take political sides; the monarch is politically neutral and acts on ministers' advice.

An absolute monarch (for contrast)

  • Can make and change laws personally, without a parliament.
  • Can raise taxes and direct spending by command.
  • Can appoint or dismiss officials at will and decide policy alone.
  • Is not bound by elected representatives or by convention.
  • Is not politically neutral; the monarch is the government.

The Crown in Parliament and parliamentary sovereignty

The Crown in Parliament

UK law is made by the King or Queen acting together with the two Houses (Commons and Lords). A bill becomes an Act only when both Houses agree and the monarch grants Royal Assent.

Parliamentary sovereignty

Parliament is the supreme law-making body. It can make or unmake any law, and no other body (including the courts or the monarch) can override an Act of Parliament.

Head of state vs head of government

The monarch is the ceremonial head of state. The Prime Minister is the political head of government, accountable to elected MPs in the House of Commons.

Devolved vs reserved

Devolved powers (such as health, education and transport) are decided by Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland. Reserved powers (such as defence and foreign affairs) stay with Westminster.

Check your understanding

Think about your answer, then reveal the model response.

1. Why is the UK called a constitutional monarchy?

Because it has a monarch as head of state, but the monarch's powers are limited by law, by Parliament and by long-standing conventions. Real political power lies with the elected government and Parliament, not the Crown.

2. What is the difference between the head of state and the head of government in the UK?

The monarch is the head of state, a largely ceremonial and symbolic role representing the nation. The Prime Minister is the head of government, the political leader who runs the country and is accountable to elected MPs in the House of Commons.

3. Give two things the monarch does that are ceremonial, and one thing the monarch cannot do.

Ceremonial: performing the State Opening of Parliament and granting Royal Assent to bills. The monarch cannot make laws, set policy, raise taxes or take political sides; these belong to the elected government and Parliament.

4. What does "the Crown in Parliament" mean?

It is the idea that laws are made by the monarch acting together with the House of Commons and the House of Lords. A bill becomes an Act of Parliament only when both Houses agree and the monarch grants Royal Assent.

5. What is devolution? Name the three devolved institutions.

Devolution is the transfer of certain decision-making powers from the UK Parliament at Westminster to elected bodies in the nations. The three institutions are the Scottish Parliament (Holyrood), the Senedd or Welsh Parliament (Cardiff), and the Northern Ireland Assembly (Stormont).

6. Sort these into devolved or reserved: school policy in Wales, defence, NHS funding in Scotland, signing a treaty.

Devolved (decided by the nations): school policy in Wales and NHS funding in Scotland. Reserved (kept by Westminster): defence and signing treaties, which are part of UK-wide foreign and defence policy.

7. Why does the UK Parliament keep powers like defence and foreign affairs reserved?

These areas affect the whole United Kingdom and its relationships with other countries, so they are handled once, centrally, at Westminster. Splitting defence or foreign policy between four nations could create confusion and weaken the UK's position internationally.

8. Even though the monarch grants Royal Assent, why do we say Parliament is sovereign?

Because Royal Assent is a convention that has not been refused since 1708; the monarch always agrees on ministers' advice. The real decision sits with Parliament, which can make or unmake any law, so Parliament, not the Crown, holds supreme law-making power.
