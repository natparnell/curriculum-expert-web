Independent Investigation Framework

Teaching aim: to guide you through every assessed stage of the Edexcel A-level Independent Investigation, from defining a focused question through sampling, presentation and statistical analysis to a robust evaluation, while showing exactly what the mark scheme rewards at each step. The Independent Investigation is worth 20% of the A-level and is a written report of 3,000 to 4,000 words.

ⓘ How to use this framework ▾

The Independent Investigation (Component 4, NEA) asks you to define, plan and carry out an enquiry of your own, drawing on the compulsory or optional content and on fieldwork you collected. Examiners mark it across seven assessment areas that mirror the stages below: purpose, planning, data collection, presentation, analysis, conclusion and evaluation.

Work through the tabs in order. Type your own question, choose a sampling method and hear whether it suits your aim, match each data type to a sensible presentation technique, use the stats-test chooser to pick the right statistical test, then complete the evaluation checklist. The completeness tracker at the top fills as you complete each stage, and the final tab builds a copy-to-clipboard plan summary you can paste into your own planning document.

Everything here is a scaffold and the example wording is illustrative. The investigation must be your own work, based on your own data, and verified by your centre.

Defining your question or hypothesis

Anchor the enquiry in the specification and make it measurable.

★ Your enquiry question

A strong title is specific, geographical and answerable with the data you can realistically collect. Tie it to a process or concept from the specification (for example coasts, water and carbon cycles, regenerating places, or your optional topic).

Tip: phrasing it as “To what extent…”, “How does…” or “What is the relationship between…” keeps it answerable.

A directional hypothesis (H1) plus a null hypothesis (H0) sets you up neatly for a significance test later.

⚑ What the mark scheme rewards here
  • A clearly defined, geographically valid aim or question that is rooted in the specification.
  • Evidence of independent thinking: the focus is your own, not a class-wide title copied wholesale.
  • A question whose scope is realistic for the time, location and data available to you.

⚑ What the mark scheme rewards here
  • A range of primary and secondary data, with sources properly attributed.

⚑ What the mark scheme rewards here
  • Use of appropriate statistical techniques, correctly applied and interpreted, with significance referenced where relevant.

Strong conclusions are proportionate: they state how far the data answer the question and acknowledge uncertainty.

⚑ What the mark scheme rewards here
  • A conclusion that directly answers the original question and is fully supported by the data.
  • A proportionate judgement (“to what extent”) rather than an overstated claim.
  • Links back to the wider geographical theory and the specification context.
