The three continua

A skill is rarely purely one type, so AQA places skills on a sliding scale (a continuum) rather than in fixed boxes. The same skill can move along a continuum depending on the situation.

Open to closed describes how much the environment affects the skill. Basic to complex describes how much thinking and decision making is needed. Low to high organisation describes how easily the skill can be broken into parts to practise.

Inverted-U theory of arousal

Arousal is a performer's level of physical and mental readiness. Move the slider to change arousal and watch performance rise to an optimal point, then fall away. Toggle the skill type to see how the optimal arousal point shifts.

Use the arrow keys for fine control. Low arousal is under-aroused; very high arousal causes over-arousal.

Reading the curve

As arousal increases from low, performance improves. At the optimal point performance peaks. Beyond it, over-arousal causes performance to drop.

Controlling arousal

Performers use stress management techniques to keep arousal near the optimal point: deep breathing to lower heart rate and calm the body, mental rehearsal (visualising the skill being performed successfully), and wider relaxation strategies. Under-aroused performers may use a warm-up, music or a team talk to raise arousal.

    What SMART stands for

    Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic, Time-bound. A target that meets all five is far more likely to motivate a performer and let a coach judge whether it has been met.

    Types of guidance and feedback

    Guidance: visual (demonstration or video), verbal (spoken instructions), manual (the coach physically moves the body), mechanical (using equipment such as a harness or float).

    Feedback: intrinsic (felt by the performer) or extrinsic (from outside); concurrent (during) or terminal (after); knowledge of results (the outcome) or knowledge of performance (how it was done).
