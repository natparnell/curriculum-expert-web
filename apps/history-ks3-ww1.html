The First World War, 1914–1918

Teaching aim: to understand why a local assassination in Sarajevo dragged the great powers into a world war, how the Western Front froze into the stalemate of trench warfare, and how the conflict reshaped life on the British Home Front before ending in 1918.

Europe split into two armed camps: the Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy) and the Triple Entente (Britain, France, Russia). A promise to defend an ally could pull everyone in.

Rivalry over empires and colonies, especially in Africa. Germany felt it had arrived too late to win an empire to match Britain's and resented being hemmed in.

Intense pride in one's nation. In the Balkans, Slav peoples wanted independence from Austria-Hungary, which made the region a powder keg.

Explainer: why did the war break out? (the MAIN factors)

Historians use the memory word MAIN for the long-term causes that built up tension across Europe before 1914: Militarism, Alliances, Imperialism and Nationalism. These did not cause the war on their own, but they made Europe dangerously unstable, like a room full of petrol fumes.

The short-term trigger

The spark was the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand, heir to the Austro-Hungarian throne, at Sarajevo on 28 June 1914. Austria-Hungary blamed Serbia and issued harsh demands. When Serbia would not accept them all, Austria-Hungary declared war. The alliance system then turned a local Balkan quarrel into a war between the great powers.

The chain reaction, summer 1914

  • 28 July: Austria-Hungary declares war on Serbia.
  • Russia mobilises its army to protect Serbia, a fellow Slav nation.
  • 1 August: Germany, allied to Austria-Hungary, declares war on Russia.
  • 3 August: Germany declares war on France (Russia's ally) and puts the Schlieffen Plan into action.
  • 4 August: Germany invades neutral Belgium to reach France quickly. Britain, bound by treaty to defend Belgium, declares war on Germany.

So both stories are true: the trigger lit the fuse, but the MAIN factors had laid the explosives. That is why most historians say the deep causes mattered most, with Sarajevo as the spark.

⚙ Why stalemate? New weapons and the deadlock

🔫 The machine gun

A single machine gun could fire hundreds of rounds a minute and cut down advancing soldiers. Defending was far easier than attacking, so neither side could break through.

☣ Poison gas

First used in 1915, chlorine and later mustard gas blinded and choked soldiers. Gas masks helped, but it added terror without breaking the deadlock.

🚒 The tank

Britain first used tanks at the Somme in 1916. Early tanks broke down often, but they could cross trenches and wire, hinting at how the deadlock would finally be broken.

💥 Artillery

Huge bombardments churned the ground into mud and craters. Shells caused most casualties, yet the wire and machine guns often survived, so attacks still failed.

Key battle: the Somme, 1 July to 18 November 1916.
  • On the first day the British army suffered around 57,000 casualties, of whom about 19,000 were killed: the worst day in British military history.
  • A week-long bombardment failed to cut the German wire or destroy the deep dugouts.
  • After months of fighting the front line moved only a few miles. It shows the grinding, attritional nature of trench warfare.
The experience of soldiers.
  • Daily life meant mud, cold, rats, lice and the constant noise of shellfire.
  • Trench foot and disease were as dangerous as the enemy; many suffered shell shock (what we now understand as combat trauma).
  • Soldiers rotated between front line, support and reserve trenches, with long spells of boredom broken by moments of terror.

🏠 The Home Front in Britain

For the first time, a war reached deep into ordinary British life. Everyone, not just soldiers, was expected to play a part. This is sometimes called "total war".

1914–1916

📷 Recruitment and conscription

At first Britain relied on volunteers, encouraged by posters and the "Pals battalions". When volunteer numbers fell after heavy losses, the Military Service Act of 1916 introduced conscription: men aged 18 to 41 could be called up to fight.

1915 onwards

🔨 Women's work

With men away fighting, women took on jobs once done by men: munitions factories ("munitionettes"), transport, farming (the Women's Land Army) and nursing. This challenged old ideas about what women could do and helped the case for the vote, won by some women in 1918.

1917–1918

🍞 Rationing

German U-boats sank merchant ships carrying food, so supplies ran short. To stop hoarding and ensure fairness, the government introduced rationing of sugar, meat, butter and other goods from 1918.

throughout

📢 Propaganda

Posters, newspapers and films were used to keep up morale, encourage enlistment, persuade people to save food and buy war bonds, and present the enemy as cruel. Bad news from the front was often hidden or censored.

1914

📜 DORA

The Defence of the Realm Act (DORA), passed in August 1914, gave the government sweeping powers: to censor news, take over factories and land, control pub opening hours and even put clocks forward (British Summer Time, 1916) to save fuel.

a new front line

🗻 The war comes home

German Zeppelin airships and later aircraft bombed British towns, and the navy shelled east-coast ports. For the first time civilians far from the fighting were under direct attack.

❓ Enquiry questions: think like a historian

Have a go at answering each question in your own words first, then reveal a model response. These are the kinds of second-order questions historians ask: about causation and significance.

Why did a local assassination in Sarajevo lead to a world war? Causation

The assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand was the trigger, not the underlying cause. Long-term tensions (the MAIN factors) had already divided Europe into two armed camps tied together by alliances.

When Austria-Hungary attacked Serbia, the alliance system pulled in Russia, then Germany, then France and finally Britain. So a single event in the Balkans set off a chain reaction across the whole continent. A strong answer weighs the trigger against the deeper causes rather than choosing only one.

Which was more important: the long-term causes or the short-term trigger? Causation

Most historians argue the long-term causes mattered most, because without militarism, the alliances, imperial rivalry and nationalism, the assassination would have stayed a local crisis.

However, the trigger was still necessary: it was the spark that lit the fuse at that particular moment. The best answers explain that causes work together, and may rank them while showing how they connect.

Why did the Western Front turn into a stalemate? Causation

Defensive weapons were far stronger than the means of attack. Machine guns, barbed wire and artillery made it almost impossible to cross no man's land, so both sides dug in.

Once continuous trench lines stretched from the Channel to Switzerland, there were no open flanks to go around. Each side could defend more easily than it could advance, so the front barely moved for years.

How significant was the First World War for women in Britain? Significance

Significant in the short term: women filled vital jobs in munitions, transport and farming, keeping the country running and the army supplied.

Significant in the long term too: their war work strengthened the argument that women deserved the vote, granted to some women in 1918. Historians debate how much was permanent, since many women left these jobs when the men returned, but attitudes had begun to change.

Why was the Battle of the Somme so significant? Significance

It became a symbol of the terrible human cost of trench warfare: around 57,000 British casualties on the first day alone. It showed the limits of the bombardment-then-advance tactic against deep dugouts and uncut wire.

It is also significant because tanks were used in battle for the first time there, pointing towards how the deadlock would eventually be broken in 1918.

🏁 How the war ended: 1918 and the Treaty of Versailles

After four years of stalemate, 1918 brought rapid change and an armistice, followed by a controversial peace.

1917: The USA joins the war, bringing fresh troops and supplies; Russia leaves after its revolution.
Spring 1918: Germany launches a huge final offensive (the Spring Offensive) but cannot break through.
Summer–autumn 1918: The Allies push Germany back in the "Hundred Days Offensive", using tanks, aircraft and combined tactics.
11 November 1918: An armistice (ceasefire) ends the fighting at 11am: the eleventh hour of the eleventh day of the eleventh month.
1919: The Treaty of Versailles forces Germany to accept blame ("war guilt"), pay large reparations, lose land and limit its armed forces. Many Germans saw it as a harsh "dictated peace", a resentment that fed later conflict.
