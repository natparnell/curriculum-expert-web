Uncertainty and Error Lab

Take repeated virtual measurements, work out the mean and its uncertainty, then propagate that uncertainty through a real calculation.

Why take repeats?

Repeats let you average out random error from your reaction time and reveal anomalies (readings that do not fit the pattern). The endorsement expects you to identify and, where justified, exclude anomalous results before processing data.

Take at least five readings here. The app will flag any value that lies more than two standard deviations from the rest so you can decide what to do with it.

Absolute, fractional, percentage

Absolute uncertainty has the same unit as the quantity (here, seconds). For a set of repeats the usual A-level rule is half the range.

Percentage uncertainty = (absolute uncertainty / mean) × 100. You will need this in Step 3 to combine uncertainties.

3 Percentage uncertainty in T

Convert your absolute uncertainty into a percentage. This is the quantity you propagate.

Pass the mean and uncertainty checks in Step 2 to unlock this step.
% uncertainty in T = (±ΔT / T) × 100
%
Enter the percentage uncertainty and press Check.
Worked tip

Divide your ± uncertainty by your mean, then multiply by 100. Example: if ΔT = 0.040 s and T = 1.850 s, then % uncertainty = (0.040 / 1.850) × 100 = 2.16 %.

4 Propagate through the calculation of g

Now combine uncertainties to find the overall uncertainty in g, then judge how reliable your result is.

Pass the percentage check in Step 3 to unlock propagation.
g = 4π² L / T²   ·   L = 0.850 m ± 0.002 m

For a quantity made by multiplying and dividing, you add the percentage uncertainties. Note that T is squared, so its percentage uncertainty is counted twice.

% uncertainty in g = %ΔL + 2 × %ΔT

How the endorsement assessor reads this

A strong evaluation states the result as g = (your value) ± (absolute uncertainty) m/s², compares it with the accepted 9.81 m/s², and comments on whether the difference is within the experimental uncertainty.

If 9.81 sits inside your range, you can say the result is consistent with the accepted value. If not, you should discuss systematic error (for example, measuring length to the bottom of the bob rather than its centre) rather than just blaming random error.
