Study of Religion: Christianity

Teaching aim: to build a connected, evaluative understanding of core Christian doctrine, its scriptural and scholarly grounding, and how the tradition has responded to secularisation, science and pluralism, in line with the Eduqas A-level Religious Studies specification for A Study of Religion (Option D: Christianity).

Christian doctrine is not a list of isolated beliefs but a connected system: the doctrine of God shapes the doctrine of humanity, which frames the problem that salvation answers, which the Church mediates, and which eschatology completes. Click any node to explore its core teaching, the key scholars (sources of wisdom and authority) and the relevant scripture, then see how it links to the rest.

How to use this concept-map (AO1 knowledge tool)

Each node is a doctrinal theme on the Eduqas Christianity specification. Selecting a node loads its teaching, scholars and scripture into the panel, and highlights the doctrines it directly connects to. The aim is to revise relationships between concepts, which underpins strong AO1 (knowledge and understanding) and gives you the synoptic links examiners reward.

Navigate with the mouse, or Tab to a node and press Enter or Space. Watch the readouts update as you explore.

How the debate builder supports AO2 (analysis and evaluation)

AO2 marks reward sustained, balanced argument leading to a justified conclusion, not one-sided assertion. A strong answer engages seriously with the opposing view before deciding.

Scaffold

AO2 essay-structure scaffold

A reliable structure for the evaluative (AO2) part of an Eduqas answer. Each stage maps to the levelled descriptors: identify the issue, present the case, engage the counter-case, weigh, and conclude with a justified judgement.

  1. Frame the issue. Define the key terms and state the central tension precisely (for example, what "salvation" or "secularisation" means in context). sets up AO2
  2. Present the strongest case. Give the most persuasive arguments for one side, supported by named scholars and scripture, not assertion. AO2 with AO1 grounding
  3. Engage the counter-case. Set out the best opposing arguments fairly; do not knock down a weak version. balance
  4. Weigh the arguments. Compare the cases directly: which is more coherent, better evidenced, more faithful to the tradition? critical analysis
  5. Justify a conclusion. Reach a clear, reasoned judgement that follows from the weighing, acknowledging its limits. sustained judgement
