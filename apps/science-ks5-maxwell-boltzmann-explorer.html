Teacher and exam notes (open for the chemistry behind it)

What the curve shows. The Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution plots the number (or fraction) of molecules against their kinetic energy. The area under the whole curve is fixed: it represents the total number of molecules, which does not change with temperature.

  • Raising temperature flattens and broadens the curve, shifting the peak to higher energy. The peak height falls because total area is conserved. Crucially, the proportion of molecules with energy ≥ Ea rises sharply, so rate increases.
  • A catalyst lowers Ea (the orange line moves left). The curve itself is unchanged, but a much larger shaded area now exceeds Ea, so more collisions are successful per unit time.
  • Rate here is modelled with the Boltzmann factor e(-Ea/RT), the same exponential term in the Arrhenius equation k = Ae(-Ea/RT). A small drop in Ea or a rise in T produces a large rise in rate.

Common misconception to challenge: heating does not give every molecule more energy equally, and it does not change Ea. It changes the spread of energies. Only conditions that change the fraction past Ea change the rate.

Folds in: the same kinetics ideas underpin how temperature affects equilibrium position and the rate of approach to equilibrium in the pH and Buffer Lab work.
