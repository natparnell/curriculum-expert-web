Theory briefing: Weiner's attribution model (A-level)

Bernard Weiner proposed that the reasons performers give for outcomes can be classified on dimensions, two of which you model here:

  • Locus of causality: is the cause inside the performer (internal, e.g. ability, effort) or outside them (external, e.g. task difficulty, luck, officials)? Internal attributions drive emotional responses such as pride or shame.
  • Stability: is the cause fixed over time (stable, e.g. ability, task difficulty) or changeable (unstable, e.g. effort, luck)? Stable attributions drive expectancy, the belief that the same result will happen again.
  • Controllability (Weiner's third dimension): can the performer change the cause? Effort is controllable; luck is not. Coaches aim attributions at controllable causes.

Self-serving bias: performers tend to attribute success internally (ability, effort) to protect self-esteem, and failure externally (luck, the ref) to avoid shame. Learned helplessness develops when repeated failure is attributed to stable internal causes ("I am just no good"), so the performer stops trying. Attributional retraining moves failure attributions towards unstable, controllable causes ("I did not train the finish enough") so motivation and effort survive.
