The Atmosphere & Climate Change

How the model works (the science)

Sunlight (mostly visible and ultraviolet) passes easily through the atmosphere. Some is reflected straight back to space by clouds, ice and pale surfaces: this fraction is the albedo. The rest is absorbed by the surface, which warms and re-emits the energy as longer-wavelength infrared radiation.

  • Greenhouse gases (carbon dioxide, methane and water vapour) absorb this outgoing infrared and re-emit it in all directions, including back down. This extra downward energy is the greenhouse effect, and it warms the surface above the temperature it would otherwise reach.
  • The planet keeps warming until the infrared escaping to space again equals the solar energy absorbed. That balance point is the equilibrium temperature.
  • Without any greenhouse gases the surface would average roughly −18°C and freeze. The natural greenhouse effect lifts that to about +15°C.
  • Burning fossil fuels adds carbon dioxide, so more infrared is trapped and the balance settles at a higher temperature. This is the link to global climate change.

The story of the atmosphere

The early atmosphere came from intense volcanic activity, releasing mainly carbon dioxide with some water vapour, nitrogen and traces of methane and ammonia. There was almost no oxygen.

  • As the planet cooled, water vapour condensed to form the oceans. Large amounts of carbon dioxide dissolved into the oceans and later formed carbonate sediments, lowering atmospheric CO&sub2;.
  • Around 2.7 to 2.4 billion years ago, algae and then plants began photosynthesising, removing carbon dioxide and releasing oxygen. Oxygen levels rose gradually over hundreds of millions of years.
  • Carbon was locked away in sedimentary rocks (such as limestone) and in the buried remains of organisms that became fossil fuels (coal, crude oil and natural gas).

The result is today's atmosphere: about four-fifths nitrogen, one-fifth oxygen, and a small but climate-critical amount of carbon dioxide.

Ways to reduce a carbon footprint
  • Use renewable or nuclear energy instead of fossil fuels for electricity and heating.
  • Drive less, switch to electric vehicles, and choose public transport, cycling or walking.
  • Fly less often, especially long-haul flights.
  • Eat less meat (especially beef and lamb) and waste less food.
  • Improve home insulation and use more efficient appliances.
  • Carbon capture and storage, and carbon offsetting through tree planting.

Full reduction is hard because it costs money, needs new technology, may reduce convenience, and depends on global cooperation. Many actions only cut, rather than remove, emissions.

Burning fuels

Atmospheric pollutants from fuels

Complete combustion of a hydrocarbon gives carbon dioxide and water. Incomplete combustion and impurities in the fuel release pollutants that harm health and the environment.

Carbon monoxide

CO

A toxic, colourless and odourless gas from incomplete combustion. It binds to haemoglobin so the blood carries less oxygen, which can be fatal.

Soot (particulates)

C particles

Carbon particles from incomplete combustion. They cause global dimming, make surfaces dirty, and worsen respiratory problems such as asthma and lung disease.

Sulfur dioxide

SO&sub2;

Produced when sulfur impurities in the fuel burn. It dissolves in cloud water to form acid rain, which damages buildings, trees and lakes, and irritates the lungs.

Oxides of nitrogen

NO and NO&sub2;

Formed when the high temperature in an engine makes nitrogen and oxygen from the air react. They cause acid rain and respiratory problems, and contribute to smog.
