Geography · A-level · Edexcel 9GE0 · Superpowers

What makes a superpower, and how is global power shifting?

Teaching aim: to model the pillars of superpower status, compare leading powers and emerging powers across those pillars, and trace the shift between unipolar, bipolar and multipolar worlds. Use the comparator to evaluate where geopolitical power is concentrating and contesting.

1The pillars of power

A superpower is a state or bloc with the capacity to project dominant influence anywhere on Earth across multiple dimensions at once. Edexcel frames superpower status through several pillars. Hard power rests on economic and military strength; soft power on culture, ideology and the ability to co-opt rather than coerce. Smart power blends the two.

💰
Economic strength
GDP, share of world trade, financial centres, reserve currency and TNC headquarters.
⚔️
Military & geopolitical reach
Defence spending, nuclear arsenal, blue-water navy, overseas bases and alliances.
🏛️
Political influence
Diplomatic weight, UN Security Council seat, agenda-setting in IGOs.
🎥
Cultural influence (soft power)
Media, language, brands, education, the appeal of values and the diaspora.
👥
Demographic & physical size
Population, workforce, internal market, territory and strategic location.
⚡
Access to resources
Energy, minerals, water, food security and control of supply chains.

4Theories that explain superpower roles

Edexcel expects you to use development theory to explain why power and wealth are unevenly distributed and how that imbalance is reproduced.

Dependency theory
Andre Gunder Frank

The periphery is kept underdeveloped because its surplus is extracted by the core. Poverty is not a starting point but an actively maintained outcome of unequal trade, debt and the colonial legacy.

World systems theory
Immanuel Wallerstein

The world economy is one system of core, semi-periphery and periphery. The core captures high-value activity; the semi-periphery (for example the BRICS) can rise or fall over time, making the system dynamic.

Modernisation model
Walt Rostow

Countries pass through five stages from traditional society to high mass consumption, with a take-off driven by investment. Criticised for assuming a single Western pathway and ignoring colonial constraints.

6Tensions and the cost of power

Superpower status generates contest. Edexcel highlights competition over resources, trade and the physical environment, alongside ideological and military tension and the sheer cost of maintaining global reach.

⛓
Resource competition
Rare earths, energy, water and contested zones such as the South China Sea and the Arctic.
💲
Trade tension
Tariffs, sanctions, technology controls and rival trade blocs reshaping supply chains.
🌎
Physical environment
Disproportionate emissions, contested climate leadership and pressure on global commons.
🛡️
Ideological & military
Democracy versus authoritarian models, proxy conflict and a renewed arms race.
💸
The cost of reach
Defence budgets, overstretch and domestic trade-offs (the "imperial overstretch" thesis).
🔄
Rebalancing
Emerging powers building alternatives (BRICS bank, new currencies, regional blocs).

7Exam-style questions

Edexcel 9GE0 style. Plan a response, then reveal the indicative content.

Assess · 12 marks
Assess the relative importance of economic strength compared with cultural influence in determining superpower status.
Reveal indicative content

For economic strength: economic power underpins everything else. It funds military reach, enables aid and debt leverage, hosts TNCs and controls the reserve currency. The USA's dollar dominance and China's manufacturing scale show economic weight converting into geopolitical leverage.

For cultural influence: soft power (Nye) lets a power co-opt rather than coerce. US cultural dominance (media, brands, universities, the English language) sustains influence far beyond its trade share, while economically large powers can still lack appeal.

Judgement: the strongest answers argue interdependence: economic strength is necessary but not sufficient, and smart power blends the two. The relative weight shifts by context and time period.

Evaluate · 20 marks
Evaluate the view that the world is becoming increasingly multipolar.
Reveal indicative content

Supporting: the rise of the BRICS and the G20, China's GDP convergence with the USA, the EU as an economic bloc, India's demographic and economic ascent, and new institutions (BRICS bank, regional trade blocs) all suggest diffusing power.

Challenging: the USA retains unmatched military reach, the dollar still dominates global finance, and US soft power and alliance networks (NATO) remain potent. Emerging powers are uneven across the pillars; Russia is strong militarily but economically narrow.

Use of theory: Wallerstein's semi-periphery explains how powers rise and fall; the comparator above shows China closing the economic and military gap while still trailing on soft power.

Judgement: the system is partially multipolar (economic) but remains closer to unipolar in military and financial terms, so "increasingly multipolar" is defensible but qualified.

Explain · 6 marks
Explain how intergovernmental organisations help maintain the power of existing superpowers.
Reveal indicative content

IGOs embed superpower advantage. The IMF and World Bank attach conditions (structural adjustment) reflecting donor priorities; the WTO sets trade rules; the UN Security Council gives permanent members a veto. Voting weight in the IMF and World Bank is tied to economic size, so the core sets the agenda. Dependency theorists argue this reproduces core-periphery inequality.
