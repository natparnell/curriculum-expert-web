Religious Language

Teaching aim: to evaluate whether statements about God are cognitively meaningful, by testing them against the verification and falsification principles, and by weighing the responses that defend religious language: the via negativa, analogy, symbol and language games.

1

The problem of religious language

Can language drawn from finite human experience say anything true about an infinite, transcendent God?

When a believer says "God is good" or "God loves us", ordinary words (good, love, father) are being applied to a being who is, by definition, beyond the world those words were built to describe. The central question of this topic is whether such utterances are meaningful at all. The first move is to ask what kind of claim a religious statement is making.

Cognitive language

Makes a factual assertion that is either true or false, and so can in principle be known. It aims to describe how reality is.

Treats "God exists" as a fact-claim, like "water boils at 100°C".

Non-cognitive language

Does not assert a fact but does something else: expresses an attitude, a commitment, a way of seeing, or a moral stance.

Treats "God is love" as an expression of trust or a commitment to live a certain way.

Why the distinction matters for the exam

The cognitive/non-cognitive distinction is the hinge of the whole topic. Logical positivists (Ayer) and falsificationists (Flew) treat religious language as cognitive, then argue it fails as factual assertion because it can be neither verified nor falsified.

The most influential defences (Hare's bliks, Tillich's symbol, Wittgenstein's language games) work instead by denying that religious language is straightforwardly cognitive in the first place. So whether you read "God is good" as a fact-claim or as something non-cognitive often decides whether the positivist's attack even lands.

How the verification principle works (strong vs weak)

The Vienna Circle held the verification principle: a statement is meaningful only if it is either a tautology (true by definition, like "all bachelors are unmarried") or empirically verifiable (checkable by sense experience).

Strong verification demands that a statement be conclusively established by observation. Ayer rejected this because it would make universal scientific laws and statements about the past meaningless. He proposed weak verification in Language, Truth and Logic (1936): a statement is meaningful if some observations would count as relevant evidence for or against it.

On either version "God exists" fails: no sense experience could even count as relevant evidence for a transcendent God, so for Ayer the statement is not false but literally meaningless. Religious utterances are neither true nor false; they are pseudo-propositions.

How the falsification principle works (the parable of the gardener)

Flew adapted Karl Popper's idea that a genuine assertion must rule something out. Borrowing John Wisdom's parable, Flew describes two explorers who find a clearing. One says a gardener tends it; no gardener is ever seen, no fence detected, no dogs disturbed, yet the believer keeps qualifying the claim: "an invisible, intangible, eternally elusive gardener".

Flew's charge is that the believer allows nothing to count against the claim. The original assertion "dies the death of a thousand qualifications". If no conceivable event would make the believer give up "God loves us" (not even a child dying of cancer), then the statement asserts nothing and is meaningless.

4

Defending religious language

The classic responses to logical positivism and falsificationism.

The via negativa

Apophatic way · Pseudo-Dionysius, Maimonides

We cannot say what God is, only what God is not: not finite, not changing, not a body. Positive language drags God down to the level of creatures, so we approach the divine by negation.

Strength: it guards God's transcendence and avoids anthropomorphism. Weakness: a stack of negatives gives little positive content, and believers clearly do want to say God is loving, not merely not-hateful.

Analogy

Thomas Aquinas

Words about God are neither univocal (same meaning as for us) nor equivocal (wholly different), but analogical. Aquinas gives two forms.

Analogy of attribution: a quality in creatures derives from its source in God, as healthy urine signals a healthy animal. Analogy of proportion: a being has a quality in proportion to its nature, so God's goodness is goodness fitting an infinite being.

Symbol

Paul Tillich

Religious language is symbolic, not literal. Unlike a mere sign, a symbol participates in the reality it points to and opens up levels of being otherwise closed to us. "God" points to the ground of being (being-itself).

Strength: it preserves depth and emotional power. Weakness (Hick, Edwards): how do we check what a symbol discloses, and Tillich's own "being-itself" looks like a non-symbolic literal claim.

Language games

Ludwig Wittgenstein

Meaning is use. Each "language game" has its own rules within a "form of life". Religious language is meaningful within the religious form of life; the positivist's mistake is to judge it by the rules of the scientific game.

Strength: it respects how believers actually use language. Weakness: it can drift into fideism, making faith immune to outside criticism, and seems to bar believers from making real truth-claims.

Hare's bliks and Mitchell's partisan (responses to Flew)

R.M. Hare conceded that religious language may not be cognitive, then argued this does not make it meaningless. He coined blik: an unfalsifiable but meaningful way of seeing the world. His parable of the paranoid student convinced all dons want to murder him shows that bliks shape how we interpret everything and have real consequences, so they matter even though no evidence touches them. Weakness: it seems to make any blik (sane or insane) as valid as another.

Basil Mitchell accepted that religious statements are assertions and can be counted against by evidence such as suffering, but the believer does not abandon faith at the first difficulty. His parable of the partisan and the stranger in occupied territory shows a trust that holds firm through ambiguous evidence: the believer takes suffering seriously as a problem yet keeps faith. This answers Flew directly: faith is not blind, but it is committed.

6

Essay-structure scaffold

A reliable shape for a 30-mark Eduqas evaluative essay (AO1 + AO2).
1. Introduction approx 5%

Define the key terms (cognitive vs non-cognitive) and state your line of argument in one sentence. Signal which thinkers you will weigh.

2. Set out the challenge (AO1) approx 25%

Explain the verification principle (Ayer, weak vs strong) and the falsification principle (Flew, the gardener) accurately. Show why religious statements appear to fail both.

3. Set out the defences (AO1) approx 25%

Explain the responses precisely: bliks (Hare), partisan (Mitchell), analogy (Aquinas), symbol (Tillich), language games (Wittgenstein). Use the right technical vocabulary.

4. Evaluate (AO2) approx 35%

Argue. Is the verification principle self-refuting (it is neither a tautology nor empirically verifiable)? Does treating religious language as non-cognitive concede too much? Weigh each defence against its critics, reaching a justified judgement, not just listing views.

5. Conclusion approx 10%

Return to your thesis and confirm it with your strongest reason. Do not introduce new material. A confident, supported judgement is what AO2 rewards.

Common evaluation lines that score well

Against the positivists: the verification principle fails its own test (it is not a tautology and cannot be empirically verified), so it is self-refuting; it would also rule out meaningful ethical and aesthetic statements; Flew's demand assumes religious language is cognitive, which the non-cognitivists deny.

Against the defences: the via negativa leaves God contentless; analogy assumes the very relation between God and world it sets out to license; symbol struggles to be checked and Tillich smuggles in a literal claim; language games risk fideism and seem to forbid genuine truth-claims about God.
