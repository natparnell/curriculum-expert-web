How the repeat structures behave (and what to write about them)

The same motif reads very differently depending on how it tiles. Examiners reward textile work that shows you understand surface pattern as a system, not a decoration.

  • Full drop: motif sits on a straight grid. Clean and formal, but can create obvious vertical and horizontal "tramlines" the eye follows.
  • Half drop: every other column shifts down by half a tile. Breaks the grid, gives a diagonal rhythm, and hides the repeat. The workhorse of furnishing and fashion print.
  • Brick: alternate rows shift sideways, like brickwork. Horizontal emphasis, good for borders and wallpaper.
  • Mirror: alternate tiles flip, creating symmetry and "kaleidoscope" joins. Useful for ogee and damask feels.
  • Rotational: tiles rotate around points, dissolving any single direction. Energetic and all-over, common in art textiles and contemporary digital print.

Linking to the formal elements

In textiles the formal elements are line, shape, form, tone, colour, texture and pattern. A strong AO3 study records how your choices move across these: how scale alters tone density, how colourway shifts mood, how stitch introduces real texture and a third dimension.

2 Critical & Contextual Study Explorer

Your Personal Investigation must be informed by a critical understanding of sources: designers, makers, cultures and movements. This explorer is illustrative; choose practitioners who genuinely connect to your theme. AO1 rewards analysis and informed connections, not biography.

What AO1 actually rewards in contextual study

AO1 is "develop ideas through investigation, demonstrating critical understanding of sources." High marks come from making your sources work for your project.

Do

  • Analyse how a maker uses the formal elements and processes, in your own visual language and through your own samples.
  • Show informed connections: place a designer within a movement, a culture or a material tradition (for example block printing, indigo dyeing, the Bauhaus weaving workshop).
  • Let the study change your work; show the influence visibly in subsequent sampling.

Avoid

  • Copied biography or downloaded text with no personal analysis.
  • A single artist studied once and never returned to.
  • Pastiche: imitating a style without understanding its intent or context.

The related written element: what it is and what it rewards

For A-level Textile Design, the Personal Investigation includes a related written element of a minimum of 1000 and a maximum of 3000 words. It is integral to the project, not bolted on, and the same four assessment objectives apply.

What it should do

  • Frame a genuine line of enquiry that runs through your practical work.
  • Use specialist vocabulary accurately: the formal elements, textile processes (resist dye, devore, applique, felting, digital print), and contextual terms.
  • Reference and analyse sources, then connect them to your own samples with visual evidence.
  • Reach reasoned, personal conclusions that show the journey of your thinking.

Common pitfalls

  • Pure description with no analysis or personal voice.
  • Writing that floats free of the practical work, never referencing your own sampling.
  • Unreferenced quotation or downloaded passages.

Word count is a window, not a target: 1000 focused, evidenced words beat 3000 unfocused ones.
