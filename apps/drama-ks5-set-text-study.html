Set Text Study Studio

Aim: work as a theatre maker across the three Eduqas prescribed set texts. Build a directorial concept for a key extract, watch your staging, design, sound and performer choices realise the text for an audience, then rehearse the kind of analytical questions set in the written examination.

As You Like It

William Shakespeare, c.1599 · pastoral comedy

The play moves from the harsh, usurped court into the green world of the Forest of Arden, where disguise sets the rules. Rosalind, banished, becomes the young man Ganymede and uses that disguise to school Orlando in love. For a modern audience the questions are live: how do you stage the court / forest contrast, and how do you handle gender, performance and the layered fiction of a woman playing a man playing a woman? A director chooses the world; a designer builds the two contrasting spaces; a performer negotiates the irony of disguise.

Forest of Arden Disguise & gender Rosalind / Ganymede Pastoral / court contrast Style: heightened, presentational verse

Love and Information

Caryl Churchill, 2012 · non-naturalistic

Churchill writes more than fifty very short scenes with unassigned lines, no named characters and no stage directions. The seven sections must be played in order, but the company chooses the casting, the setting and who speaks. This is the play's central freedom and its central challenge: the director must build a coherent world and emotional through-line from fragments, deciding the visual logic, transitions and rhythm that hold a contemporary audience across rapid, disconnected human moments about how we know, feel and connect.

Fragmentary structure Unassigned lines No fixed characters Director's freedom Style: non-naturalistic, episodic

The Curious Incident of the Dog in the Night-Time

Simon Stephens, 2012, from Mark Haddon · physical / multimedia

Christopher, a fifteen-year-old who experiences the world differently, narrates his investigation into a dog's death and his journey to London. The original National Theatre production (Frantic Assembly movement) staged his perspective directly: a grid-marked box set, projection and LED, sound design and an ensemble who lift, carry and become the world around him. The making question is how to represent a neurodivergent experience truthfully and theatrically, using multimedia and physical theatre rather than naturalistic illusion, so the audience sees through Christopher's eyes.

Christopher's perspective Multimedia & projection Physical theatre / ensemble Frantic Assembly Style: physical, multimedia, presentational

    Three texts, three theatrical styles

    Eduqas asks you to think as a theatre maker across very different theatrical languages. Place each set text against the others: where it sits between naturalistic illusion and overt, presentational theatricality changes every design and performance decision.

    All three sit toward the theatrical end of the spectrum, but for different reasons: Shakespeare through presentational verse and disguise, Churchill through fragmentation, Stephens through physicalised perception. None depends on the illusion of a sealed naturalistic room.

    Working as a theatre maker for the written examination

    In Eduqas A-level Drama and Theatre, the prescribed set texts are studied both as written examination material and from the perspective of a theatre maker. You are asked to make and justify directorial, design and performance decisions for a chosen extract, always rooting them in the text and in their effect on a contemporary audience. The studio above models that loop: a choice, its consequence on stage, its meaning for an audience.

    As a director

    Set the staging configuration, the concept and the world. Justify how your reading clarifies theme: the disguise game in Arden, the human fragments in Churchill, Christopher's perception in Stephens.

    As a designer

    Use set, lighting and sound to build atmosphere and signpost meaning. Explain the intended effect: contrast, transition, focus, the felt texture of a moment.

    As a performer

    Choose vocal and physical qualities, proxemics and intensity. Show how an actor's choices land the line and shape audience response.

    Texts and extracts here are illustrative teaching material to rehearse exam-style theatre-making decisions, not a substitute for the prescribed editions.
