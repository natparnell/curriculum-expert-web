02

How They Form and Develop

Tropical storms form over warm tropical oceans in late summer and early autumn, when the sea is warmest. The sequence below shows how warm, moist air rising over the ocean releases huge amounts of energy.

The formation sequence

  • Warm, moist air rises rapidly over an ocean heated above 27°C, creating an area of intense low pressure.
  • As the air rises it cools, condenses and forms towering cumulonimbus clouds, releasing latent heat that powers the storm.
  • The rising air draws in more warm, moist air from the surface, so the storm strengthens and grows.
  • The Coriolis effect deflects the inrushing air, making the whole system spin (anticlockwise in the northern hemisphere).
  • The storm tracks westward on the trade winds, then often curves poleward, weakening once it reaches land or cooler water and loses its energy source.

The role of the Coriolis effect

  • The Earth's rotation deflects moving air: to the right in the northern hemisphere and to the left in the southern.
  • This deflection makes the inflowing winds spiral rather than flow straight into the low pressure centre.
  • The Coriolis effect is zero at the Equator and too weak below about 5° latitude, so storms cannot start to spin there.
  • Above about 30° the ocean is usually too cool, so the formation zone sits in the 5° to 30° band.

Structure and Features

A mature tropical storm has a clear structure. This labelled cross-section is drawn live; the eye, eyewall and rain bands are the features you should be able to name and describe in an exam.

The eyeCalm centre, 30 to 50 km wide, with gently sinking air, light winds, clear skies and the lowest pressure.
The eyewallA ring of towering cumulonimbus around the eye with the strongest winds and heaviest, most intense rainfall.
Rain bandsSpiralling bands of cloud and rain reaching out from the centre; winds and rain ease with distance from the eyewall.
04

Climate Change and Tropical Storms

A warming climate is expected to change tropical storms. The evidence is clearer for some impacts than others, so use cautious, evaluative language in exam answers.

Distribution

  • As more oceans warm beyond 27°C, the area where storms can form may widen.
  • The formation zone may spread further from the Equator, so some regions could be affected for the first time.

Frequency

  • Evidence on the total number of storms is uncertain; many studies suggest the overall count may stay similar or fall slightly.
  • The proportion of the most intense storms is widely expected to rise.

Intensity

  • Warmer seas give storms more energy, so average wind speeds and rainfall may increase.
  • A warmer atmosphere holds more moisture, raising flood risk; higher sea levels worsen storm surges.

Named Example: Typhoon Haiyan

Case study · Philippines · November 2013

Typhoon Haiyan

Wind speed ~315 km/h Category 5 Storm surge ~5 m Deaths ~6,300 Affected ~14 million
Primary effects
  • Around 6,300 people killed, many by the storm surge.
  • Over 1 million homes damaged or destroyed.
  • The city of Tacloban was devastated by winds and flooding.
  • Power lines, roads and the airport were damaged.
Secondary effects
  • Flooding and landslides triggered by heavy rain.
  • Shortages of food, clean water and shelter.
  • Outbreaks of disease and the spread of infection.
  • Lost crops, fishing and jobs damaged the economy.
Immediate responses
  • Evacuation of about 800,000 people before landfall.
  • International aid, rescue teams and field hospitals arrived.
  • Emergency supplies of water, food and medicine distributed.
  • A state of national emergency was declared.
Long-term responses
  • Rebuilding of homes, roads and the airport.
  • A "build back better" programme using stronger designs.
  • New storm-surge defences and a no-build coastal zone.
  • Improved warning and evacuation planning.

07

Reducing the Effects

The risk from tropical storms can be managed in three main ways. AQA wants you to explain monitoring and prediction, protection, and planning.

Monitoring and prediction

  • Satellites and aircraft track storms and measure their strength.
  • Computer models predict the track and likely landfall.
  • Warnings are issued so people can prepare or evacuate.

Protection

  • Storm-proof buildings with reinforced roofs and shutters.
  • Sea walls and flood defences against storm surge.
  • Houses raised on stilts in flood-prone areas.

Planning

  • Evacuation routes and emergency shelters prepared.
  • Education so people know what to do.
  • Stockpiles of food, water and supplies ready in advance.

Exam-Style Questions

Try each question before revealing a model answer. Marks are shown in the style of AQA Paper 1.

[2 marks]
State two conditions needed for a tropical storm to form.
Any two of:
  • Sea surface temperature above about 27°C (to a depth of around 70 m).
  • A latitude of roughly 5° to 30° (away from the Equator) for enough Coriolis effect.
  • Low wind shear so the storm can build upwards.
  • Warm moist air / unstable, rising air over the ocean.
[4 marks]
Explain the role of the Coriolis effect in the formation of a tropical storm.
Model points:
  • The Earth's rotation deflects moving air (to the right in the northern hemisphere).
  • This deflection makes the inflowing winds spiral around the low pressure centre rather than flowing straight in.
  • The spinning concentrates the energy and helps the storm organise and intensify.
  • The effect is too weak within about 5° of the Equator, which is why storms do not form right at the Equator.
[6 marks]
"The effects of a tropical storm are worse in lower income countries." Using a named example, assess this statement.
How to structure a Level 3 answer (using Typhoon Haiyan, Philippines, 2013):
  • Primary effects: around 6,300 deaths and over a million homes destroyed, partly because many buildings were not storm-proof.
  • Secondary effects: disease, food and water shortages and economic damage were severe because resources to respond quickly were limited.
  • Balance: warning and evacuation of about 800,000 people did save lives, showing planning can reduce impacts even in a lower income country.
  • Conclusion: effects are often more severe in lower income countries because of weaker buildings, infrastructure and capacity to respond, but the level of preparation and the storm's strength also matter.
[4 marks]
Suggest how monitoring, prediction and planning can reduce the effects of tropical storms.
Model points:
  • Satellites and aircraft monitor storms and computer models predict the track, so warnings can be issued early.
  • Early warnings give time to evacuate and protect property, reducing deaths and injuries.
  • Planning emergency shelters, evacuation routes and supply stockpiles means the response is faster and more organised.
  • Education means people know how to act, lowering the human impact.
