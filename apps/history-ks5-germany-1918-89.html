Germany and West Germany, 1918 to 1989

Teaching aim: to develop the synoptic skill of analysing change over time across an extended chronology. You will track three breadth themes (the strength of democracy, the state of the economy, and the level of state repression and control), locate the period's decisive turning points, and weigh continuity against change to construct a sustained, substantiated breadth argument.

Assessment focus: AO1 (analytical, substantiated judgement on change over a long period) and the demands of the breadth essay on the relative significance of factors.

2 How the period actually moved

A breadth study rewards the candidate who can see the long arc, not a string of disconnected events. The summary below is the model against which to test your mapper.

Democracy

From fragile republic to anchored federal state

The Weimar Republic was democratic in form but unstable from birth: proportional representation produced coalition churn, Article 48 normalised rule by presidential decree, and the system collapsed into the Nazi one-party state by 1933. After 1945 the occupying powers and the 1949 Basic Law deliberately engineered a more durable democracy in the Federal Republic, with a 5 per cent threshold, a constructive vote of no confidence, and a strong constitutional court. Adenauer's chancellor democracy, Brandt's Ostpolitik and Kohl's long stewardship embedded it, so that by 1989 West German democracy was secure and outward-facing.

Economy

Hyperinflation, slump, recovery, miracle

The economy lurched: the 1923 hyperinflation destroyed savings and trust; the Stresemann years (1924 to 1929) brought a fragile, loan-dependent recovery; the Depression after 1929 produced mass unemployment that the Nazis exploited. Nazi rearmament restored employment but distorted the economy towards war. The post-war Wirtschaftswunder, driven by Erhard's social market economy, Marshall Aid, the 1948 currency reform and export strength, turned West Germany into Europe's economic powerhouse by the 1960s.

Repression and control

From contested freedoms to total terror and back

Weimar permitted broad political freedoms despite paramilitary street violence. After 1933 the Nazi regime built a terror state: the SS, Gestapo, concentration camps and the elimination of opposition created near-total control. Defeat in 1945 dismantled this apparatus in the west. The Federal Republic restored civil liberties, though Cold War anxieties produced episodes such as the 1956 KPD ban. The contrast with the GDR's Stasi state to the east sharpened the West's self-image as a free society by 1989.

Continuity and change

Reading the synoptic arc

The strongest breadth answers resist a single straight line. Democracy and freedom move together but the economy follows its own rhythm: it recovered under Stresemann while democracy stayed fragile, and it boomed after 1948 while democracy was still being rebuilt. Repression is close to the inverse of democracy, peaking exactly where democracy collapses. The 1945 caesura is the deepest break in all three themes; 1923, 1929 and 1933 cluster as the crisis that ends Weimar; 1949, 1961 and 1989 chart the consolidation and eventual transcendence of division.

3 Interpretations to weigh

Breadth answers are sharpened by an awareness of how historians have framed change in this period. Use these positions to qualify your own judgement.

Structuralist reading

Weimar was doomed from the outset

The Republic carried "structural flaws" (proportional representation, the army's reservations, the burden of Versailles) that left it without reliable defenders when crisis came.

Useful for the political track around 1929 to 1933, but criticised for understating contingency: the recovery of 1924 to 1929 shows the system could function.

Contingency reading

The Depression, not design, killed Weimar

Without the slump of 1929 the Republic might have endured; the economic shock, not an inevitable decay, opened the door to the Nazi seizure of power.

Strong on the economy track, and explains why your democracy and economy lines should both plunge after 1929. Risks downplaying the regime's deeper unpopularity.

Functionalist reading of the Nazi state

Polycratic chaos, not a monolith

The "one-party state" was a tangle of competing agencies "working towards the Fuhrer", so terror radicalised cumulatively rather than from a single master plan.

Refines the repression track between 1933 and 1945: control was near-total in effect, but not centrally rational in design.

The success of the Bonn Republic

A deliberately stabilised democracy

The Federal Republic was engineered to avoid Weimar's faults; its economic miracle and constitutional safeguards made it "the democracy that worked".

Anchors the upward democracy and economy lines after 1949. Test it against the 1956 KPD ban and the radical decree controversies of the 1970s.
