How to analyse a film for the AQA essay

Read the film, do not retell it

The examiner has seen the film. Marks come from analysis and evaluation, not narration. Every point should answer the question and earn its place. Treat scenes as evidence the way you would treat a quotation in a literature essay: name the moment, show the technique, then explain what it means for the theme.

Themes, characters and their development

Track how a theme (for example identity, injustice, the family, memory) is introduced, complicated and resolved. Follow a character arc: where do they begin, what changes them, where do they end. The strongest essays show development across the film rather than a single static idea.

The director's techniques (mise-en-scene, cinematography, sound, editing)

Cite technique, not just content. Note the mise-en-scene (setting, costume, lighting, composition), the cinematography (camera angle, movement, framing), the sound (dialogue, music, silence, diegetic vs non-diegetic) and the editing (cuts, pace, montage). Read the technique reference panel for definitions, then link each one to meaning.

Structuring the target-language essay

Open with an introduction that states a clear thesis (your line of argument). Write analytical paragraphs using PSTAL: point, scene evidence, technique, analysis, link. Include a counter-argument ("on pourrait soutenir que...", "se podria argumentar que...") then refute it to show evaluation. Finish with a conclusion that returns to the thesis and judges it. Accuracy, range of vocabulary and complex structures all carry marks alongside the argument.
