🧲 Magnet Playground

Teacher aim: Explore magnetic attraction, repulsion and poles; sort magnetic from non-magnetic materials; compare friction on a ramp. (KS2 Forces and magnets)

Which materials are magnetic?

Drag the bar magnet so its end touches an object. The magnet only pulls objects made of certain metals. Sort all six into the right group to win.

Teacher notes and questions
  • Magnetic metals here: iron nail, steel paperclip, steel scissors. Non-magnetic: aluminium can, copper coin, plastic brick.
  • Key idea: not all metals are magnetic. Only iron, steel, nickel and cobalt are attracted to a magnet.
  • Ask: "The coin and the can are both shiny metal. Why does only some metal stick?"

Feel the poles: push or pull?

Drag the blue magnet near the fixed red magnet. Like poles push apart, opposite poles pull together. Flip your magnet to swap its poles and feel the force change.

Keyboard: Tab to the blue magnet, move with arrow keys, press F to flip it.

Poles facing each other

Teacher notes and questions
  • N and S attract (pull together). N and N, or S and S, repel (push apart).
  • The force gets stronger as the magnets get closer: notice the feedback wording change from "gentle" to "strong".
  • Ask: "Without touching, how can you make two magnets push each other away?"

Friction ramp lab

Friction is a force that slows things down. Choose a surface and a ramp height, then release the block. A rough surface has more friction, so the block slows sooner and stops nearer the top.

Teacher notes and questions
  • More friction (carpet) means a shorter slide; less friction (ice) means a longer slide.
  • A taller ramp gives the block more speed, so it travels further before friction stops it.
  • Ask: "Which surface would let the block travel the furthest, and why?"
