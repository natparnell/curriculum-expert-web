How to structure writing for different purposes ▾

Strong writers make deliberate choices. Before you write, decide why you are writing (purpose) and who will read it (audience). Everything else (structure, sentence length, vocabulary, tone) follows from those two answers.

To persuade (article, letter)Clear stance up front. Topic-sentence paragraphs. Rhetorical questions, triplets, direct address. Build to a call to action.
To describe (descriptive piece)Zoom from wide to close. Sensory detail and figurative language. Vary sentence length to control pace and mood.
To inform (formal letter, report)Logical order, objective tone, formal register. Cohesive devices to link points. No slang or contractions.
To entertain (short story)Hook, rising tension, turning point, resolution. Show not tell. Dialogue and pace shifts to hold the reader.

Organising ideas into paragraphs

Each paragraph should carry one main idea, usually opened by a topic sentence and developed with detail or example. Start a new paragraph for a new point, a new time, a new place or a new speaker.

Cohesive devices and connectives

These stitch your writing together so it flows: however, furthermore, consequently, in contrast, meanwhile, similarly, as a result, on the other hand. Use them to signal how one idea relates to the next.

Varying sentences for effect

  • Vary openings: start with an adverb, a subordinate clause or a participle, not always the subject.
  • Vary length: long sentences build detail; a short one lands a point. It works.
  • Vary structure: mix simple, compound and complex sentences for rhythm.

Ambitious, precise vocabulary

Choose the exact word, not just a longer one. Replace tired choices (nice, got, big, said) with precise alternatives that fit the tone. Precision beats decoration.

The writing process

Plan, draft, edit, proof-read. Drafting is for getting ideas down; editing is for improving cohesion, sentences and word choice; proof-reading is the final hunt for spelling, punctuation and grammar slips. Treat them as separate jobs.
