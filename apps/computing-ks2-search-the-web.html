How search really works

Three big ideas behind every search engine.

🔑

Keywords

The search engine picks out the most important words in your question. Small joining words such as "how", "do", "the" and "for" are often ignored. Choosing clear, specific keywords gives sharper results.

🔢

Ranking

Pages are put in order. A page scores higher when it matches your keywords well, when many other trusted pages link to it, and when it comes from a source the engine treats as reliable. The best match is shown first.

🔎

Be discerning

Being near the top does not always mean true. Watch for adverts (someone paid to be there), out-of-date pages, and biased pages that only give one side. Check who wrote it, when, and why.

Quick check

Think about your answer, then reveal it.

1. A page is shown at the very top of the results. Does that prove it is true?

No. The top result is only the engine's best guess at the best match. It might be an advert, or simply a popular page. You still need to check who made it and whether it is accurate.

2. You search "best volcano slime toy buy now". Why might the top results be less useful for a science project?

Words like "buy now" and "best" tell the engine you want to shop, so it returns shops and adverts. For facts, search "volcano facts" or "how volcanoes erupt" instead.

3. Which is usually the most reliable source for a factual answer: an advert, an official museum or science site, or someone's personal opinion blog?

The official museum or science site. Adverts are trying to sell something, and opinion blogs give one person's view. Official organisations usually check their facts.

4. Why should you check the date on a web page?

Information can go out of date. An old page might give numbers or facts that have since changed. A recent page from a trusted source is usually safer.
