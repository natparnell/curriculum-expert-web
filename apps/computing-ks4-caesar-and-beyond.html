Caesar and Beyond

Teacher aim: pupils learn how a Caesar (shift) cipher protects a message, then use frequency analysis to crack an intercepted message, before linking encryption to the wider threats and countermeasures in network and system security.

Stage 1: Turn the wheel, shift the alphabet

A Caesar cipher replaces each letter with one a fixed number of places along the alphabet. Drag the inner ring (or use the arrow keys) to set the shift, type a message, and watch it scramble. This is symmetric encryption: the same key (the shift) encrypts and decrypts.

Stage 2: Crack an intercepted message

You have intercepted an encrypted message. You do not know the key. Frequency analysis is the weakness of simple ciphers: in English, some letters appear far more often than others. Compare the message's letter counts to typical English, find the shift that lines them up, then read the decrypted message and confirm your answer.

Stage 3: Threats and countermeasures

A Caesar cipher is easy to break, so real systems use far stronger encryption. Encryption is just one countermeasure. Networks and systems face many threats; each has a sensible defence. Match each threat to its best countermeasure.

Malware

Software written to damage or take control of a device, for example a virus, worm or ransomware.

Phishing

Fake emails or messages that trick a user into giving away passwords or bank details.

Social engineering

Manipulating people, for example pretending to be IT support, to gain access or information.

Interception

Reading data as it travels across a network, also called eavesdropping or a sniffing attack.
