Theory note: attribution, learned helplessness and mastery orientation

Bernard Weiner (1979) classified the reasons performers give for success and failure along two main dimensions: the locus of causality (internal or external to the performer) and stability (stable or unstable over time). A third dimension, controllability, distinguishes attributions the performer can influence from those they cannot.

The four classic attributions

  • Ability: internal, stable, largely uncontrollable.
  • Effort: internal, unstable, controllable.
  • Task difficulty: external, stable, uncontrollable.
  • Luck: external, unstable, uncontrollable.

Learned helplessness vs mastery orientation

Learned helplessness is the belief that failure is inevitable and beyond one's control, typically caused by attributing repeated failure to stable, internal, uncontrollable factors such as low ability. A mastery orientation is the opposite: the performer expects success, sees failure as temporary and attributes it to unstable, controllable factors such as effort or tactics.

Attribution retraining

Coaches use attribution retraining to move performers away from maladaptive ability attributions for failure and toward controllable effort attributions, protecting self-esteem and sustaining motivation. The self-serving bias (attributing success internally and failure externally) is a related protective strategy.

Theory note: Steiner, the Ringelmann effect and cohesion

Steiner's model (1972) states that a team's actual productivity equals its potential productivity minus losses due to faulty processes. Process losses fall into two types:

  • Coordination losses: poor teamwork, timing, communication and tactics. These grow as the sport becomes more interactive.
  • Motivation losses: individual members reducing effort, known as social loafing.

The Ringelmann effect

Ringelmann's rope-pulling studies showed that as group size increased, the average force exerted by each member decreased. Individual effort dropped because performers felt their contribution was hidden within the group.

Reducing social loafing

Highlight individual contributions, give specific roles and statistics, set individual as well as team goals, raise the perceived importance of each player and develop group identity.

Group cohesion

Carron distinguished task cohesion (working together toward a shared goal) from social cohesion (mutual liking and interaction). Task cohesion is the stronger predictor of performance in most sports.

Theory note: leadership theories, styles and Chelladurai

How leaders emerge

  • Trait theory: leaders are born with stable personality characteristics ("great person" view). Largely unsupported on its own.
  • Social-learning theory: leadership behaviours are learned by observing and imitating role models.
  • Interactionist theory: effective leadership depends on the interaction between the leader's characteristics and the situation.

Prescribed vs emergent leaders

A prescribed leader is appointed from outside the group (for example a coach hired by a club). An emergent leader arises from within the group, chosen by its members (for example a player voted captain).

Leadership styles

  • Autocratic (task-oriented): leader makes decisions; suited to large groups, dangerous activities, hostile situations and beginners who need clear instruction and quick decisions.
  • Democratic (person-oriented): leader shares decisions; suited to small groups, cohesive teams and experienced performers who value involvement.
  • Laissez-faire: leader stands back and lets the group decide; risks poor focus, but can suit highly skilled, self-motivated experts.

Chelladurai's multi-dimensional model

Chelladurai argues that performance and satisfaction are highest when three forms of leader behaviour align: required behaviour (what the situation demands), actual behaviour (what the leader does) and preferred behaviour (what the group wants). These are shaped by situational characteristics, leader characteristics and member characteristics.

Theory note: arousal, anxiety, stress and their management

Arousal is the level of physiological and psychological activation. Anxiety is a negative emotional state linked to arousal, split into cognitive anxiety (worry, negative thoughts) and somatic anxiety (bodily symptoms such as a racing heart). Stress is the response to a perceived imbalance between demand and the ability to cope.

Cognitive stress-management techniques

  • Imagery / mental rehearsal: visualising successful performance.
  • Positive self-talk: replacing negative thoughts with constructive cues.
  • Thought stopping and rational thinking: interrupting and challenging unhelpful thoughts.
  • Goal setting using SMART targets to focus attention and build confidence.

Somatic stress-management techniques

  • Progressive muscular relaxation: tensing and releasing muscle groups.
  • Breathing control and centring.
  • Biofeedback: using monitored physiological data to learn control.

Self-efficacy: Bandura's four sources

  • Performance accomplishments: previous success, the strongest source.
  • Vicarious experience: watching others of similar ability succeed.
  • Verbal persuasion: encouragement from coaches and peers.
  • Emotional (physiological) arousal: interpreting arousal as readiness rather than threat.
