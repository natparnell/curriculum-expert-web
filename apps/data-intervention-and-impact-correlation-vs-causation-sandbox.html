Correlation vs Causation Sandbox

Teaching aim: a results dip recovered the term after we launched an intervention. Did the intervention cause the recovery? Drag the confounder and the counterfactual to see how much of the rise the intervention can honestly claim, and why "it went up afterwards" is not proof.

The scenario

Maple Vale Academy ran a Year 11 reading intervention from the spring term. The mean reading age rose over the year. The senior team want to report the impact.

The claim under test: "Our intervention raised reading ages by the full amount of the rise." Use the controls to decide how much of that rise the intervention can fairly take credit for.

🧠 Quick check

For the teacher: how to use this and what it proves

The core message: a rise after an intervention is not evidence the intervention caused it. Three rival explanations can produce the same rise: a confounder that lifts everyone, regression to the mean, and natural maturation. The only honest measure of impact is the gap between what happened and a credible counterfactual.

Run it live: start with the confounder and regression at zero so the observed rise equals the real effect. Then raise the confounder. The observed total climbs but the honest effect does not move. That separation is the whole lesson.

Then flip "Show the counterfactual" off. The verdict warns that with no comparison group the leader can only see the blue line, so any of that rise could be claimed. Turning the counterfactual back on shows how much was never theirs to claim.

"Cherry-pick the lowest starters" models a common trap: choosing the weakest pupils for an intervention guarantees a bounce-back that the programme did not cause.

Correlation: two things move together. Here, the intervention and the rise both happen at once.
Causation: one thing actually produces the change in the other. Far harder to establish.
Confounder: a third factor driving the outcome that is mistaken for the cause.
Counterfactual: what would have happened without the intervention. The benchmark for true impact.
Regression to the mean: extreme results tend to be followed by more average ones, with no cause needed.
