How Spanish gender and articles work

In Spanish every noun (a naming word for a person, animal, place or thing) is either masculine or feminine. This is called its gender. The little word for "the" or "a" must agree with the noun, so it changes depending on whether the noun is masculine or feminine, and whether it is singular (one) or plural (more than one).

The word for "the" (definite article)

The word for "a" or "an" (indefinite article)

MasculineFeminine

A handy pattern for spotting the gender

  • Many nouns ending in -o are masculine, so they take el or un: el libro (the book), un gato (a cat).
  • Many nouns ending in -a are feminine, so they take la or una: la mesa (the table), una casa (a house).
  • This is a helpful guide, but it is not a guarantee. Some words break the rule and you simply have to learn them.

Common exceptions to remember

el día = the day (ends in -a but masculine) la mano = the hand (ends in -o but feminine) el problema = the problem (ends in -a but masculine) el mapa = the map (ends in -a but masculine)

Making the article fit the noun like this is called agreement. The game above gives you a tip after every word to help the pattern stick.

Check what you know

Have a think, then tap each question to reveal the answer.

1. Which article means "the" with a masculine singular noun?
el. For example el coche (the car). The feminine singular version is la.
2. The noun gato (cat) ends in -o. Is it masculine or feminine, and what is "the cat"?
It is masculine (most -o nouns are), so "the cat" is el gato.
3. How do you say "the houses" (plural of la casa)?
las casas. Feminine plural uses las, and the noun adds -s to become plural.
4. Spot the trap: is la mano following the -o pattern?
No. mano (hand) ends in -o but is actually feminine, so we say la mano. It is one of the exceptions to learn.
5. Which words mean "a" or "an"? When do you use each one?
un for masculine nouns (un libro = a book) and una for feminine nouns (una manzana = an apple).
