3D Design Maquette Studio

Teaching aim: develop and refine a three-dimensional idea by building, transforming and viewing a maquette. You will explore how the formal elements in three dimensions (form, structure, surface, scale and space) shape a design, and connect each move you make to the four assessment objectives (AO1 to AO4) used to assess GCSE Art and Design.

A maquette is a small working model used to test a three-dimensional idea before committing to a final piece. Add primitive forms, then transform and rotate the assembly to investigate how its form, structure, scale and surface read from every angle. This is the same iterative thinking a designer-maker records in a sketchbook for AO1 and AO2.

    AQA Three-Dimensional Design spans many disciplines. Each works with the same formal elements (form, structure, surface, scale, space) but answers different problems. Select a discipline to read its core concerns, the materials and methods it favours, and an example starting brief.

    A strong 3D Design project is a journey, not a single object. The four assessment objectives describe that journey. Maquettes and prototypes sit right at the heart of it: they are how you develop ideas (AO1) and refine them through making (AO2). This storyboard maps a typical project against AO1 to AO4.

    Key terms glossary
    Maquette
    A small preliminary model that tests a three-dimensional idea before the final piece is made.
    Prototype
    A working version of a design built to test function, fit or manufacture.
    Form
    The three-dimensional shape and volume of an object, read from all sides.
    Structure
    How parts are arranged and joined so the form stands and holds together.
    Surface
    The finish, texture and material quality of the outer skin of a form.
    Scale
    The size of a form relative to the human body, the site or other parts.
    Space
    The volume an object occupies and the negative space it shapes around and within itself.
    Maker / designer
    An artist who works three-dimensionally, from sculptors to product and architectural designers.
