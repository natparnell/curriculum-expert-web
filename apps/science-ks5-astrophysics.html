Wien's displacement law and the black-body curve

Black-body radiation

A star behaves as a black body. As you raise the temperature, the peak of the spectral curve shifts to shorter wavelengths (Wien's displacement law) and the total radiated power climbs steeply (Stefan's law). Drag the slider and watch the peak wavelength, the colour and the luminosity respond.

Wien's displacement law

λmax × T = 2.90 × 10⁻³ m K. Peak wavelength is inversely proportional to temperature, so hotter stars peak in the blue or ultraviolet and look blue-white.

Stefan's law

L = 4πr² σ T⁴. Luminosity is proportional to surface area times temperature to the fourth power, so a small rise in T greatly increases the power radiated.

Hubble's law: plotting recession speed against distance

Cosmology

Distant galaxies recede from us with a speed proportional to their distance: v = H₀d. Add measured galaxies (each carries realistic measurement scatter), fit a straight line through the origin, then read off Hubble's constant H₀ and the implied age of the universe, t ≈ 1 / H₀. Distances here are in megaparsecs (Mpc).

Red shift spectrum demonstrator

Doppler effect

Absorption lines in a galaxy's spectrum sit at fixed laboratory wavelengths. Because the galaxy recedes, every line is stretched to a longer (redder) wavelength. The fractional shift gives the recession speed through Δλ / λ = v / c (valid for v much less than c). Drag the slider to recede the source and watch the dark lines slide towards the red.

Why this is evidence for the Big Bang

Almost all galaxies are red shifted, and the more distant ones are shifted more (Hubble's law). Rewinding the expansion points to a single hot, dense origin.

Cosmic microwave background

The CMB is the cooled, red shifted remnant of that hot early universe, now a near-perfect black body at about 2.7 K filling all of space.
