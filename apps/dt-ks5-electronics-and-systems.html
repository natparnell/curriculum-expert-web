Systems & Control

Teaching aim: model how electronic and mechanical systems take an input, transform it through a process and deliver an output; show why closed-loop feedback control settles on a target where open-loop cannot; and quantify how cams, gears and linkages change the magnitude, direction or type of motion and provide mechanical advantage.

What you are watching. A thermostat is the classic closed-loop system. The sensor (input) reports the room temperature, the microcontroller (process) compares it with the set point to find the error, and switches the heater (output) through a relay until the error reaches zero. Feedback is what lets it settle on the target and reject disturbances. An open-loop heater just runs flat-out: it cannot sense the room, so it overshoots and never corrects.

Reading the profile. A circular (eccentric) cam gives smooth, sinusoidal-like rise and fall. A pear cam dwells (holds the follower steady) for roughly half a turn, used in engine valve gear. A snail cam rises gradually then drops sharply, used where a sudden return is needed. The graph plots follower displacement against rotation angle: the steeper the line, the faster the follower moves.

The core relationship. Gear ratio = driven teeth ÷ driver teeth. A ratio greater than 1 is a reduction: the output turns slower but with proportionally more torque (higher mechanical advantage), as in a hand drill or a winch. A ratio less than 1 is an overdrive: faster, less torque. Two meshing external gears always turn in opposite directions; add an idler to restore the original direction without changing the overall ratio.
Exam practice

Exam-style questions

AQA A-level Product Design (7552). Attempt your answer, then reveal the indicative content. 4 questions

1. Open-loop vs closed-loop4 marks

A designer is choosing a control strategy for an electric heater in a nursery. Explain why a closed-loop system is more appropriate than an open-loop system.

Indicative content:
  • Closed-loop uses feedback: a temperature sensor reports the actual room temperature back to the controller (1).
  • The controller compares actual temperature with the set point to calculate the error and switches the heater accordingly (1).
  • This lets the system settle on the target and reject disturbances such as a door opening, improving safety and comfort (1).
  • Open-loop has no feedback, so it cannot self-correct: it would overheat or undercool the room, a safety risk for children (1).
2. Driving an output3 marks

A microcontroller output pin can only supply a few milliamps. Explain how a transistor and a relay are used to switch a mains heater.

Indicative content:
  • The small pin current is fed to the transistor base; the transistor acts as a switch and can carry a larger current (1).
  • That current energises the relay coil, whose magnetic field closes the relay contacts (1).
  • The relay provides electrical isolation and switches the high-current mains circuit safely; a flyback diode protects the transistor from the coil's back-EMF (1).
3. Gear ratio calculation4 marks

A driver gear has 15 teeth and meshes with a driven gear of 45 teeth. The driver turns at 900 rpm with a torque of 1.5 Nm. Calculate the gear ratio, the output speed, the output torque and state the direction of the driven gear.

Indicative content:
  • Gear ratio = 45 ÷ 15 = 3 : 1 (reduction) (1).
  • Output speed = 900 ÷ 3 = 300 rpm (1).
  • Output torque = 1.5 × 3 = 4.5 Nm (ideal, ignoring friction) (1).
  • The driven gear rotates in the opposite direction to the driver (1).
4. Motion and mechanisms4 marks

Name the four types of motion and identify one mechanism that converts rotary motion into reciprocating motion.

Indicative content:
  • The four types: linear (straight line), rotary (turning), reciprocating (back and forth) and oscillating (swinging arc) (2).
  • A cam and follower converts rotary motion into reciprocating motion; a crank and slider also does this (1).
  • Credit: the cam profile sets the displacement; a peg-and-slot or rack-and-pinion are acceptable alternatives where appropriate (1).
