The Great Fire of London: 5 Days

Teacher aim: An event beyond living memory that is significant nationally. Pupils advance the fire day by day across 1666 London, read a clue from Samuel Pepys, work out why the fire spread so fast, and discover what changed afterwards.

2nd September 1666

"Some of our maids sitting up late last night to get things ready saw a great fire in the City." From the diary of Samuel Pepys

Why did the fire spread so quickly between the houses?

🏗️ The fire is out. What changed?

The Great Fire burned for nearly five days. Four out of every five buildings in the old City were destroyed, yet very few people died. London had to be rebuilt, and people changed the way they built and protected their city.

Then (before 1666):

Houses were made of wood and straw, squeezed close together with the upper floors almost touching.

Now (after 1666):

New laws said houses must be built of brick and stone, with wider streets between them.

Then:

There was no fire brigade. People fought fires with leather buckets and hooks.

Now:

The first fire insurance and organised fire-fighting teams were started.

Why we remember: A tall stone column called The Monument still stands in London today, close to where the fire began. We remember the Great Fire so we learn how to keep buildings and people safer.
