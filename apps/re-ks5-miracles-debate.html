The Problem of Miracles

Teaching aim: to evaluate competing definitions of a miracle (Hume, Aquinas, Holland), to work through David Hume's argument against the rational credibility of miracle testimony, to weigh the principal responses (notably Swinburne), and to assess what miracles imply for the concept of God.

A01

What counts as a miracle?

There is no single agreed definition of a miracle. Eduqas requires you to distinguish three influential accounts and to recognise that the definition you adopt largely settles the later debate. A realist (violation) definition makes miracles vulnerable to Hume; a significance definition sidesteps the violation problem but raises the question of whether anything genuinely transcendent has occurred.

David Hume (1711-1776)

A miracle is a transgression of a law of nature brought about by a particular volition of the Deity, or by the interposition of some invisible agent. This is a "hard" or violation definition: the event must break a law of nature and be caused by God or an agent.

"A miracle is a violation of the laws of nature."

Thomas Aquinas (1225-1274)

A miracle is an event done by God beyond the order normally observed in nature (praeter ordinem). Aquinas identifies three kinds, ranked by how far they exceed nature's power:

  • 1.
    Events nature could never do, for example the sun reversing its course or standing still.
  • 2.
    Events nature can do, but not in this order, for example recovery from a terminal illness, or living after death.
  • 3.
    Events nature can do, but God does without using nature's principles, for example an instantaneous cure of a sickness that could heal slowly on its own.

R. F. Holland (1923-2013)

A miracle need not break any law of nature. On Holland's contingency (or coincidence) definition, a perfectly naturally explicable event becomes a miracle because of its religious significance for the person who interprets it as one. His famous example: a child playing on a railway line is saved when the train stops, not by intervention but because the driver had fainted at exactly the right moment. Every step is natural; the mother rightly calls it a miracle.

"A coincidence can be taken religiously as a sign and called a miracle."

violation vs significance praeter ordinem contingency miracle laws of nature

Examiner steer: candidates score highly when they show that the choice of definition determines the strength of Hume's attack. Hume only defeats the violation account.

A01

Hume against rational belief in miracles

"A wise man proportions his belief to the evidence."

In An Enquiry concerning Human Understanding (Section X, 1748), Hume mounts two arguments. The first is the argument in principle from the balance of probabilities; the second gathers practical (a posteriori) objections about the quality of actual testimony.

The argument in principle
  • P1
    A law of nature is established by firm, unalterable, uniform human experience. The proof against any violation is therefore as complete as any proof from experience can be.
  • P2
    A miracle is, by definition, a violation of such a law.
  • P3
    We can only believe testimony of a miracle if its falsehood would be a greater miracle than the event it reports.
  • C
    Since the uniform experience supporting the law is always weightier than fallible human testimony, it is always more probable that the witness is mistaken or lying than that the law was violated. So belief in a miracle on testimony can never be rational.
The practical objections (a posteriori)
  • i
    Unreliable witnesses. No miracle has ever been attested by a sufficient number of educated, sensible, reputable witnesses of unquestioned integrity.
  • ii
    The love of the marvellous. Human beings delight in wonder and surprise, which biases them towards believing and spreading extraordinary tales.
  • iii
    Ignorant peoples. Miracle reports flourish chiefly among "ignorant and barbarous" nations, decreasing as societies become more educated.
  • iv
    Conflicting religious claims. Rival religions each report their own miracles as proof; since they cannot all be true, each set cancels the evidential force of the others.
balance of probabilities uniform experience testimony proportion belief to evidence

Note the structure: the practical objections do not strictly need the principled argument. Even if a violation were possible, Hume argues, the actual testimony we have is too weak to establish one.

The weight of uniform experience exceeds the weight of the testimony. On Hume's reasoning the wise person withholds belief: it remains more probable that the witnesses erred than that the law was broken. "A wise man proportions his belief to the evidence."

How to read this scale. Hume's case is comparative, not absolute. He never says a miracle is impossible; he says the evidence for any miracle can never outweigh the standing evidence for the law it would break, so long as you accept his two assumptions: that laws are fixed by exceptionless experience, and that testimony is the only access we have. Swinburne challenges the second by arguing that human testimony and memory are generally trustworthy unless we have specific reason to doubt them, which raises the right-hand pan. The "begs the question" reply challenges the first by denying that a law must be exceptionless. Note that none of these responses prove a miracle occurred; they only show that Hume has not proved belief is always irrational.

A02

What miracles imply about God

Even if miracles occur, they raise sharp questions for the concept of God. A God who breaks the laws he ordained is a God who intervenes in creation. Eduqas expects you to evaluate whether such intervention is coherent and whether it sits well with God's goodness.

If God performs miracles he must act within the created order rather than only sustaining it. For some this confirms a personal, immanent, providential God who answers prayer. For others it implies that creation was left imperfect and needs occasional correction, which sits awkwardly with the idea of an omniscient, omnipotent designer who could have got it right at the outset.

If God can intervene, why heal one cancer patient at Lourdes and not the millions who die elsewhere? Maurice Wiles argued that a God who works occasional, selective miracles while ignoring the Holocaust would be capricious and morally unworthy of worship. Selective intervention seems to make God arbitrary (no principled reason for the choice) and partial (favouring some over others), both of which threaten divine justice and benevolence.

  • Wiles concluded that the only coherent "miracle" is the single act of creation and sustaining itself, not a series of interventions.
  • Defenders reply that we are not in a position to judge God's reasons, and that miracles may serve revelatory purposes we cannot fully see.

If God is the author of the laws of nature, breaking them looks like God acting against his own rational order, which some see as incoherent. Aquinas avoids this by saying God acts beyond nature's order, not against it, since God is not bound by the laws he freely established. Holland avoids it entirely: nothing is broken at all.

A strong AO2 line: the coherence of belief in miracles tracks the definition you adopt. The violation definition gives the richest theological pay-off (a clearly active God) but the heaviest costs (Hume's evidential attack and the arbitrariness problem). Holland's definition escapes both costs but at the price of leaving everything naturally explicable, so critics ask whether God is doing any work at all. The arbitrariness problem is the most serious challenge for a traditional theist, because it threatens not the existence but the goodness of God.

Eduqas

Exam practice (AO1 & AO2)

Eduqas A-level Religious Studies marks knowledge and understanding under AO1 and analysis and evaluation under AO2. Try each question, then reveal the indicative content.

AO1examine

Examine David Hume's understanding of what a miracle is and his argument against belief in miracles. (20 marks)

Indicative content
  • Hume's definition: a transgression of a law of nature brought about by a particular volition of the Deity or an invisible agent.
  • The argument in principle: laws rest on uniform experience; testimony is weaker; it is always more probable the witness errs than that the law breaks.
  • "A wise man proportions his belief to the evidence" and the test that a report is credible only if its falsehood would be a greater miracle.
  • The four practical objections: unreliable witnesses, the love of the marvellous, prevalence among ignorant peoples, conflicting religious claims.
  • Top bands: accurate, well-selected detail with clear, fluent use of technical terms (uniform experience, balance of probabilities).
AO2evaluate

"Hume's argument successfully shows that belief in miracles is never reasonable." Evaluate this view. (30 marks)

Lines of argument (both sides required)
  • For: the balance of probabilities is sound; actual testimony really is weak; conflicting religious miracles do cancel out.
  • Against: Swinburne's principles of testimony and credulity show we are entitled to trust witnesses; Hume begs the question by defining laws as exceptionless; he sets the evidential bar impossibly and inconsistently high.
  • The definition objection: Hume only defeats the violation account, not Aquinas or Holland.
  • A sustained judgement is required, e.g. Hume undermines naive credulity but does not establish that belief is never reasonable.
AO2evaluate

"A God who performs miracles cannot be wholly good." Evaluate this view. (30 marks)

Lines of argument (both sides required)
  • For: Wiles on arbitrariness and partiality; selective intervention seems unjust given the suffering God does not relieve.
  • Against: we cannot judge God's reasons; miracles may have revelatory rather than merely rescuing purposes; Holland's definition avoids the charge since nothing arbitrary is done.
  • Distinguish the existence of God from the goodness of God: the objection targets goodness.
  • Judgement: the force of the objection depends on the definition of miracle assumed.
Essay structure scaffold (AO2)
Frame the claim and define the terms

State the issue and pin down the definition of miracle in play. Flag that the conclusion depends on which definition (Hume, Aquinas, Holland) you adopt.

Strongest case for the statement

Develop the best supporting argument fully (for Hume, the balance of probabilities plus practical objections). Use technical terms accurately.

Strongest case against

Give the most powerful counter (Swinburne's principles; the begging-the-question charge; the definition objection). Engage, do not just list.

Weigh the arguments

Compare the strength of each side directly. Which assumption is doing the work? Is the bar of evidence reasonable?

Reasoned judgement

Reach a defended conclusion that follows from your analysis, not a sudden verdict. A nuanced line (Hume succeeds against X but not Y) scores well.
