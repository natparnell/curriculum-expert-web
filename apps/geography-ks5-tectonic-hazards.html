Tectonic Processes & Hazards

Teaching aim: to understand why physically similar tectonic events produce vastly different human outcomes, applying the hazard-risk equation, Degg's model and the Park response curve to evaluate the roles of magnitude, vulnerability, capacity to cope, development and governance.

Park model: the disaster-response curve

The Park model traces quality of life through the three phases of recovery: relief (hours to days), rehabilitation (days to weeks) and reconstruction (weeks to years). Drag the slider to model a well-managed response (a shallow, narrow dip and a return to or above the pre-event level) versus a poorly-managed one (a deep, prolonged trough).

Higher values mean faster relief, more effective rehabilitation and reconstruction that improves on the original (build back better).

A well-managed response, supported by preparedness and an effective hazard management cycle, limits the depth of the dip, speeds the onset of relief and uses reconstruction to build back better, so the curve finishes above its starting level.

04

Exam-style questions

Edexcel 9GE0 style. Attempt each in full before revealing the indicative content.

Question 1 · 4 marks · AO1
Explain how slab pull and ridge push contribute to plate movement. (4)
Indicative content:
  • Slab pull: at a destructive margin the dense, cooled oceanic plate subducts; its weight pulls the rest of the plate downward into the asthenosphere, dragging it along. Now regarded as the dominant driver.
  • Ridge push (gravitational sliding): at a constructive margin newly formed lithosphere is hot and elevated; as it cools and thickens away from the ridge it slides down the slope of the asthenosphere, pushing the plate.
  • Both operate alongside mantle convection in the asthenosphere, transferring heat from the core and providing the basal drag on the lithosphere.
Question 2 · 6 marks · AO1 / AO2
Using the hazard-risk equation, explain why an earthquake of a given magnitude can produce very different impacts in two countries. (6)
Indicative content:
  • Risk = ( frequency or magnitude of hazard × vulnerability ) ÷ capacity to cope. Magnitude is the physical input only; the human outcome depends on the other terms.
  • Vulnerability varies with development: informal, non-seismic housing, dense low-income settlement and high dependency raise it; enforced building codes and insurance lower it.
  • Capacity to cope depends on governance, wealth, emergency services, early-warning and education; strong capacity converts a potential disaster into a managed event (Degg's model).
  • The PAR model frames this as the progression from root causes (governance, economy) through dynamic pressures to unsafe conditions meeting the hazard.
  • Use of contrasting exemplars (for example Tohoku 2011 versus Haiti 2010) credited.
Question 3 · 8 marks · AO1 / AO2
Assess the usefulness of the Park model in understanding responses to a tectonic disaster. (8)
Indicative content (assessment requires judgement):
  • Strengths: shows quality of life over time through relief, rehabilitation and reconstruction; allows comparison of well-managed versus poorly-managed responses; captures build-back-better where the curve finishes above its start; links clearly to the hazard management cycle.
  • Limitations: generalised single curve hides spatial variation within a country; depends on data on a vague variable (quality of life); assumes a single event rather than compound or secondary hazards; less applicable to slow-onset or repeated hazards.
  • Judgement: most useful as a comparative, conceptual framework for response and recovery, less useful as a precise predictive tool. Best used alongside Degg's and PAR models.
Question 4 · 6 marks · AO1 / AO2
Evaluate the relative importance of prediction, protection and preparedness in managing volcanic hazards. (6)
Indicative content:
  • Prediction: volcanoes are more predictable than earthquakes through monitoring (seismicity, ground deformation, gas emissions), enabling timely evacuation; VEI helps anticipate scale.
  • Protection: lava diversion, exclusion zones, hazard mapping and land-use zoning reduce exposure, though limited against high-VEI explosive eruptions.
  • Preparedness: education, evacuation drills, warning systems and emergency planning build capacity to cope and shorten the Park-model dip.
  • Judgement: for volcanoes prediction and preparedness usually outweigh structural protection, but the balance depends on development, governance and the eruption style.
