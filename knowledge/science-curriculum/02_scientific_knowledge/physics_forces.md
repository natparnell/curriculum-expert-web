# Key Stage 3 Physics: Forces and Motion

## Introduction

Forces and motion form one of the foundational topics in the Key Stage 3 (KS3) physics programme of study. This topic introduces students to the fundamental concepts that explain why objects move (or don't move) and how we can describe and predict motion. The study of forces and motion at KS3 establishes conceptual foundations that students will build upon at GCSE and beyond, making it essential that students develop secure understanding during these crucial years.

The KS3 programme of study for physics requires that pupils be taught about forces in a progressive manner, beginning with qualitative descriptions and simple calculations in Year 7, moving through more complex concepts in Year 8, and culminating in quantitative work with speed, acceleration, and pressure in Year 9. This progression ensures that students develop both conceptual understanding and mathematical skills in tandem.

This document provides a comprehensive account of the forces and motion content at Key Stage 3, drawing upon the National Curriculum statutory requirements, guidance from the Association for Science Education, and insights from physics education research. It addresses common misconceptions, suggests practical approaches, and indicates how this topic connects to the broader physics curriculum and to students' everyday experiences.

## The National Curriculum Requirements

### Statutory Programme of Study

The National Curriculum for Science at Key Stage 3 requires that pupils be taught about forces in the following progression:

**Year 7 (Lower Key Stage 3)**

- Understanding that a force is a push or pull that can change an object's speed, direction, or shape
- Recognising that forces are measured in newtons (N) using a forcemeter
- Identifying different types of forces including gravitational, magnetic, and frictional forces
- Understanding that forces can act at a distance (non-contact forces) or through direct contact

**Year 8 (Middle Key Stage 3)**

- Understanding balanced and unbalanced forces and their effects on motion
- Recognising that when forces are balanced, an object remains stationary or moves at constant speed
- Understanding that unbalanced forces cause changes in speed or direction (acceleration)
- Introduction to the concept of resultant force

**Year 9 (Upper Key Stage 3)**

- Calculating speed from distance and time measurements
- Understanding the relationship between speed, distance, and time
- Introduction to the concept of acceleration
- Understanding pressure as force distributed over an area
- Calculating pressure in simple situations

### Working Scientifically

Throughout the study of forces and motion, pupils should develop their "working scientifically" skills, including:

- **Planning**: Designing investigations to measure forces, speeds, or accelerations
- **Measuring**: Using appropriate instruments including forcemeters, stopwatches, and measuring tapes
- **Recording**: Presenting data in tables, graphs, and charts
- **Analysing**: Identifying patterns in data and drawing conclusions
- **Evaluating**: Assessing the reliability of measurements and suggesting improvements

These investigative skills are not separate from content learning but are developed through practical work on forces and motion.

## Core Concepts: Forces

### What is a Force?

At its most fundamental level, a force is simply a push or a pull. This everyday definition provides a starting point for students' understanding. Forces can:

- **Change an object's speed**: Making it speed up or slow down
- **Change an object's direction**: Causing it to turn or curve
- **Change an object's shape**: Stretching, compressing, or bending it
- **Start or stop motion**: Setting a stationary object moving or bringing a moving object to rest

Students should understand that forces are interactions between objects. When one object exerts a force on another, the second object exerts an equal and opposite force on the first (Newton's Third Law, though this is not formally introduced until GCSE).

### Types of Forces

KS3 students encounter several different types of forces:

**Contact Forces**

These require physical contact between objects:

- **Friction**: A force that opposes motion between surfaces in contact. Friction always acts in the direction opposite to motion (or attempted motion).
- **Air resistance** (drag): A type of friction that acts on objects moving through air. Air resistance increases with speed.
- **Water resistance**: Similar to air resistance but acting on objects moving through water.
- **Normal contact force**: The force exerted by a surface supporting an object. This force acts perpendicular to the surface.

**Non-Contact Forces**

These can act at a distance without physical contact:

- **Gravitational force**: The force of attraction between masses. Near Earth's surface, this causes objects to fall towards the ground.
- **Magnetic force**: The force exerted by magnets on magnetic materials or other magnets.
- **Electrostatic force**: The force between electric charges (though this is less emphasised in the KS3 forces topic).

Understanding the distinction between contact and non-contact forces helps students to identify forces acting in different situations.

### Force Arrows

A key representational tool in forces is the force arrow (or vector diagram). Students learn to:

- Draw arrows to represent forces acting on an object
- Make the length of the arrow represent the size (magnitude) of the force
- Show the direction of the force with the arrowhead
- Label arrows with the type of force and its size in newtons

Force arrows are essential for visualising situations involving multiple forces and for understanding the concept of resultant force.

### Measuring Forces

Students learn to measure forces using a forcemeter (spring balance). Key points include:

- Forces are measured in newtons (N), named after Isaac Newton
- The unit newton is approximately the weight of a small apple (about 100g mass)
- Forcemeters work by measuring how much a spring stretches
- The scale must be read when the forcemeter is stationary

Practical work with forcemeters helps students to develop a sense of scale—what does 1N feel like? What about 10N or 50N?

## Balanced and Unbalanced Forces

### The Concept of Equilibrium

A crucial concept at KS3 is that when forces are balanced (equal and opposite), an object behaves differently than when forces are unbalanced. This concept is often taught through the following progression:

**Stationary Objects**

When an object is at rest, the forces acting on it are balanced. For example:

- A book resting on a table experiences a downward gravitational force (weight) and an upward normal contact force from the table. These forces are equal and opposite.
- If the forces were unbalanced, the book would accelerate (move). Since it remains stationary, the forces must be balanced.

**Moving at Constant Speed**

An object moving at constant speed also has balanced forces acting on it. For example:

- A car travelling at steady speed on a motorway has a forward driving force balanced by backward forces of air resistance and friction.
- If the driving force exceeds the resistive forces, the car speeds up. If resistive forces exceed the driving force, the car slows down.

This is counter-intuitive for many students, who assume that a moving object must have a net force acting on it in the direction of motion. Addressing this misconception is essential.

### Resultant Force

The concept of resultant (or net) force is introduced at KS3:

- When multiple forces act on an object, the resultant force is the single force that has the same effect as all the forces combined.
- If forces act in opposite directions, the resultant is the difference between them.
- If forces act in the same direction, the resultant is the sum of them.

Students learn to calculate simple resultant forces and to predict the effect on motion:

- Resultant force = 0 → stationary or constant speed
- Resultant force forwards → acceleration forwards (speeding up)
- Resultant force backwards → acceleration backwards (slowing down)

### Unbalanced Forces and Acceleration

When forces are unbalanced, an object accelerates. At KS3, acceleration is understood qualitatively as a change in speed or direction:

- Speeding up: The resultant force is in the same direction as motion
- Slowing down: The resultant force is opposite to the direction of motion
- Changing direction: The resultant force acts at an angle to the direction of motion

Students do not calculate acceleration at KS3 (this comes at GCSE), but they should understand the relationship between resultant force and change in motion.

## Friction and Air Resistance

### Understanding Friction

Friction is a contact force that opposes motion between surfaces. KS3 students should understand:

**What causes friction**: Friction arises from the interaction between microscopic irregularities on surfaces and from forces between atoms at the contact points.

**Factors affecting friction**:
- The nature of the surfaces (rougher surfaces generally have more friction)
- The force pressing the surfaces together
- Whether the surfaces are stationary or moving (static friction is usually greater than dynamic friction)

**Useful and problematic friction**:
- Useful: friction enables walking, allows cars to grip the road, enables brakes to work
- Problematic: friction causes wear, wastes energy as heat, slows moving objects

### Investigating Friction

Practical investigations of friction might include:

- Measuring the force needed to drag different surfaces across each other
- Investigating how the area of contact affects friction (it doesn't, for most everyday situations)
- Testing how lubricants reduce friction

These investigations develop students' practical skills while reinforcing concepts.

### Air Resistance

Air resistance (drag) is a form of friction that acts on objects moving through air. Key concepts include:

**Factors affecting air resistance**:
- Speed: air resistance increases with speed
- Shape: streamlined shapes experience less air resistance
- Cross-sectional area: larger areas experience more air resistance

**Terminal velocity**: In simplified terms, when an object falls, it speeds up until air resistance equals its weight, at which point it falls at constant speed (terminal velocity). This concept is introduced qualitatively at KS3.

### Streamlining

Students learn that streamlining reduces air resistance. Examples include:

- Sports cars and aircraft designed with smooth, tapered shapes
- Cyclists adopting crouched positions to reduce their cross-sectional area
- Fish and birds having streamlined body shapes

## Describing Motion

### Speed and Velocity

At KS3, students develop understanding of speed and the distinction between speed and velocity:

**Speed** is a scalar quantity (magnitude only):
- Speed = distance ÷ time
- Units: metres per second (m/s) or kilometres per hour (km/h)
- Speed tells us how fast something is moving but not in what direction

**Velocity** is a vector quantity (magnitude and direction):
- Velocity includes both speed and direction
- An object can have constant speed but changing velocity (if it changes direction, e.g., moving in a circle)

Students calculate speeds from distance and time measurements. They should be able to:

- Calculate speed given distance and time
- Calculate distance given speed and time
- Calculate time given distance and speed

### Measuring Speed

Practical work on measuring speed might include:

- Measuring the speed of a toy car down a ramp
- Timing students running over a measured distance
- Using light gates and dataloggers to measure speed automatically

These activities develop measuring skills and reinforce the relationship between distance, time, and speed.

### Distance-Time Graphs

Students learn to interpret and draw simple distance-time graphs:

- A horizontal line indicates stationary (no change in distance)
- A straight sloping line indicates constant speed
- The steeper the line, the greater the speed
- A curve indicates changing speed (acceleration or deceleration)

While detailed analysis of gradients comes later, KS3 students should understand that the slope of a distance-time graph relates to speed.

### Acceleration

At KS3, acceleration is introduced qualitatively:

**Definition**: Acceleration is a change in speed or direction (or both).

**Situations involving acceleration**:
- Speeding up: positive acceleration
- Slowing down: negative acceleration (deceleration)
- Changing direction: acceleration even at constant speed

Students do not calculate acceleration at KS3, but they understand that:
- Greater resultant force produces greater acceleration (for a given mass)
- For a given force, objects with greater mass accelerate less

These qualitative relationships lay the foundation for Newton's Second Law at GCSE.

## Pressure

### Understanding Pressure

Pressure is introduced at KS3 as force distributed over an area:

**Definition**: Pressure = force ÷ area

**Units**: Pascals (Pa), where 1 Pa = 1 N/m²

Key concepts:

- The same force can produce different pressures depending on the area over which it acts
- Smaller area → greater pressure (for the same force)
- Larger area → smaller pressure (for the same force)

### Examples and Applications

Students explore pressure through everyday examples:

- Sharp knives cut more easily because the force is concentrated on a small area
- Snowshoes prevent sinking by distributing weight over a larger area
- Drawing pins have a wide head (low pressure on the finger) and a sharp point (high pressure on the drawing board)
- Elephants have large feet to reduce pressure on soft ground

### Calculating Pressure

Students perform simple pressure calculations:

- Calculating pressure given force and area
- Calculating force given pressure and area
- Calculating area given force and pressure

These calculations reinforce understanding of the inverse relationship between pressure and area.

## Common Misconceptions

### Force and Motion Misconceptions

Research in physics education has identified several common misconceptions about forces and motion that KS3 teachers should address:

**"Force is needed to maintain motion"**

Many students believe that a moving object must have a force acting on it in the direction of motion. This Aristotelian view persists despite centuries of Newtonian physics. Addressing this requires:
- Explicit discussion of balanced forces and constant speed
- Practical examples of objects moving with no driving force (e.g., a rolling ball slowing down due to friction, not "running out of force")
- Recognition that in everyday experience, friction usually means we do need to keep applying force to maintain motion

**"Heavier objects fall faster"**

Students often predict that heavier objects fall more quickly than lighter ones. While air resistance can make this true for some objects (e.g., a feather vs. a stone), in a vacuum all objects fall at the same rate. Demonstrations (e.g., dropping a heavy and light object simultaneously) can challenge this misconception.

**"Force is a property of an object"**

Students sometimes think of force as something an object "has" rather than an interaction between objects. Forces should always be described in terms of what is exerting the force and on what object.

### Representational Misconceptions

**Force arrows**

Students may:
- Draw force arrows that don't match the magnitude of forces (e.g., equal length arrows for unequal forces)
- Forget to include all relevant forces
- Draw forces acting on the wrong object

Explicit teaching of force arrow conventions and regular practice help to develop these representational skills.

## Practical Work and Investigations

### Key Practical Activities

The following practical activities support learning about forces and motion at KS3:

**Measuring Forces**
- Using forcemeters to measure the weight of different objects
- Investigating how the force needed to pull an object varies with surface
- Measuring the force needed to stretch springs or elastic bands

**Investigating Friction**
- Dragging different surfaces across each other
- Testing how different materials affect friction
- Investigating the effect of lubricants

**Investigating Air Resistance**
- Dropping parachutes of different sizes
- Racing objects of different shapes but similar mass
- Timing falling objects of different mass and shape

**Measuring Speed**
- Timing toy cars over measured distances
- Measuring walking and running speeds
- Using light gates to measure speed automatically

**Investigating Balanced and Unbalanced Forces**
- Using toy cars on ramps to see how slope affects motion
- Investigating how the number of elastic bands affects the speed of a trolley
- Observing objects falling through different fluids (water, oil, air)

### Working Scientifically Through Practical Work

These practical activities develop students' "working scientifically" skills:

**Planning**: Students design fair tests by identifying variables, deciding what to measure, and planning their method.

**Measuring**: Students develop skills in using forcemeters, stopwatches, measuring tapes, and other equipment.

**Recording**: Students record results in tables, choosing appropriate units and headings.

**Presenting**: Students draw graphs (particularly distance-time graphs) to present their data visually.

**Analysing**: Students look for patterns in their data and relate these to the concepts they are learning.

**Evaluating**: Students consider the accuracy of their measurements, identify sources of error, and suggest improvements.

## Connections to Other Topics

### Within Physics

Forces and motion connects to other KS3 physics topics:

- **Energy**: Forces do work and transfer energy; friction transfers energy to thermal energy
- **Electricity**: Electrical forces are introduced; magnetic forces connect to electromagnetism
- **Waves**: Forces are involved in wave motion and in the behaviour of light and sound

### Across Science

Forces and motion also connects to biology and chemistry:

- **Biology**: Friction and air resistance affect animal locomotion; forces are involved in the skeletal and muscular systems
- **Chemistry**: Pressure affects chemical reactions; intermolecular forces are relevant to states of matter

### Real-World Contexts

Forces and motion has numerous real-world applications:

- **Transport**: Design of vehicles, road safety, braking distances
- **Sport**: Performance in running, jumping, throwing, swimming
- **Engineering**: Structural design, materials selection, mechanical systems
- **Space**: Rocket propulsion, satellite orbits, planetary motion

## Assessment at Key Stage 3

### Formative Assessment

Ongoing formative assessment helps teachers to identify and address misconceptions:

- **Questioning**: Probing questions can reveal understanding (e.g., "What forces act on a stationary book?" "What will happen if I let go of this moving object?")
- **Practical work**: Observation of practical skills and understanding
- **Written work**: Explanations, calculations, and graph work
- **Drawings**: Force diagrams and their interpretation

### Summative Assessment

End-of-topic assessments might include:

- **Calculations**: Speed calculations, pressure calculations
- **Graph work**: Interpreting distance-time graphs
- **Explanations**: Explaining motion in terms of forces
- **Force diagrams**: Drawing and interpreting force arrows
- **Practical planning**: Designing investigations

### Progression to GCSE

The KS3 forces and motion topic lays foundations for GCSE Physics/Combined Science:

**GCSE Forces content builds on KS3 by adding**:
- Vector calculations (adding forces at angles)
- Newton's Laws of Motion (formal treatment)
- Acceleration calculations (a = Δv/t)
- Momentum and conservation of momentum
- Moments and levers
- Circular motion and centripetal force

Students who have secure KS3 understanding are well-prepared for the greater mathematical demand and conceptual complexity of GCSE.

## Pedagogical Approaches

### Using Models and Analogies

Models and analogies help make abstract concepts concrete:

- **Forces as arrows**: The force arrow is a powerful visual model
- **Friction as "grip"**: Thinking of friction as helpful grip rather than just resistance
- **Air resistance as "pushing through treacle"**: Imagining moving through a viscous fluid

However, teachers should be aware that all models have limitations and should discuss these with students.

### Addressing Misconceptions

Effective teaching of forces requires explicit attention to misconceptions:

- **Elicitation**: Begin by finding out what students already think
- **Challenge**: Provide experiences that challenge misconceptions
- **Resolution**: Help students develop scientifically accurate understanding
- **Application**: Give opportunities to apply new understanding in varied contexts

Conceptual change takes time, and students may revert to intuitive ideas. Repeated opportunities to apply concepts help to consolidate learning.

### Differentiation

Forces and motion can be differentiated in various ways:

- **Support**: Provide sentence starters, pre-drawn force diagrams, calculation frameworks
- **Extension**: Challenge students with more complex situations, derive relationships, explore research
- **Practical**: Adjust the complexity of practical work, from structured to more open-ended
- **Mathematical**: Vary the level of mathematical demand in calculations and graph work

### Using Technology

Technology can enhance the teaching of forces and motion:

- **Simulations**: PhET simulations allow students to explore forces virtually
- **Datalogging**: Light gates and motion sensors enable precise measurements
- **Video analysis**: Apps allow students to analyse motion from video clips
- **Online quizzes**: Self-marking quizzes provide immediate feedback

However, technology should complement rather than replace hands-on practical work, which develops manipulative skills and brings concepts to life.

## Conclusion

Forces and motion is a foundational topic in the Key Stage 3 physics curriculum. It introduces concepts and skills that students will build upon throughout their science education and that connect to their everyday experiences of moving objects. Effective teaching requires a combination of conceptual development, practical investigation, and mathematical application, with explicit attention to common misconceptions.

The progression from qualitative description in Year 7 to quantitative calculation in Year 9 provides a structured pathway for developing understanding. By the end of Key Stage 3, students should be able to identify forces, explain motion in terms of balanced and unbalanced forces, calculate speeds and pressures, and carry out investigations into forces and motion.

The topic's relevance to real-world contexts—from transport safety to sporting performance—provides motivation for learning and opportunities to apply understanding. Teachers who make these connections explicit help students to see the value and relevance of physics in their lives.
