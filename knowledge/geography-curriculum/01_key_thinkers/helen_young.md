# Helen Young: Primary Geography Specialist and Early Years Expert

## Introduction

Helen Young has established herself as a leading authority in primary geography education, with particular expertise in the Early Years Foundation Stage (EYFS) and Key Stage 1. Her work bridges the gap between the academic discipline of geography and the practical realities of teaching young children, providing invaluable guidance for primary teachers who often lack specialist geographical training but are increasingly expected to deliver the National Curriculum's geography requirements.

Young's contributions centre on making geography accessible, engaging, and developmentally appropriate for young learners while maintaining the intellectual integrity of the subject. Through her publications, training, and curriculum development work, she has helped transform how primary schools approach geography, particularly in the early years where foundational concepts are established.

## Biography and Career

### From Classroom to National Influence

Helen Young's career in education began in primary teaching, where she developed her understanding of how young children learn about the world around them. Her classroom experience in Key Stage 1 and the Early Years gave her firsthand insight into the challenges teachers face in introducing geographical concepts to young learners.

Recognising a gap in support for primary geography, Young increasingly focused her professional development on geographical education, eventually becoming a leading figure in the field. Her career trajectory demonstrates the path from classroom practitioner to national expert, maintaining throughout a commitment to practical, classroom-focused guidance.

### Specialist Roles and Consultancy

Young has held several influential positions that have shaped her contributions to geography education:

**Primary Geography Consultant**

As a consultant, Young has worked with schools, academy trusts, and local authorities to develop their primary geography provision. This work has included:
- Auditing existing geography provision and identifying areas for development
- Supporting curriculum design that ensures progression from EYFS through Key Stage 2
- Delivering training for teachers and subject leaders
- Developing resources and planning materials
- Supporting schools in implementing the National Curriculum's geography requirements

**Geographical Association Leadership**

Young has been closely involved with the Geographical Association, contributing to their primary geography initiatives and publications. The GA's role as the leading subject association for geography has provided a platform for Young to share her expertise with teachers nationally.

Her work with the GA has included:
- Contributing to primary-focused publications and guidance
- Leading professional development sessions at conferences
- Developing the association's Early Years and Key Stage 1 resources
- Advocating for the importance of geography in the primary curriculum

## Key Contributions to Primary Geography

### Early Years Geography Development

Young's most distinctive contribution lies in her work on **Early Years geography**—the geography that takes place within the EYFS framework before formal schooling begins. While the EYFS does not include geography as a discrete subject, the **Understanding the World** area of learning encompasses crucial geographical foundations.

**Understanding the World: Geographical Foundations**

Young has been instrumental in helping early years practitioners recognise and develop the geographical learning that occurs within the EYFS. Her work addresses:

- **Spatial awareness and exploration**: How young children develop understanding of space, place, and their immediate environment through play and exploration
- **The natural world**: Supporting children's observation and understanding of natural features, weather, and seasonal change
- **People and communities**: Developing young children's understanding of the diversity of places and cultures
- **Emergent mapping**: How very young children begin to represent space and location through drawing, model-making, and play

Young's guidance helps early years practitioners recognise that these experiences are not merely general development but specific geographical learning that provides foundations for later study.

**Transition from EYFS to Key Stage 1**

A particular focus of Young's work has been managing the **transition** from the EYFS to the more formal requirements of Key Stage 1 geography. This includes:
- Ensuring continuity between early geographical experiences and National Curriculum requirements
- Supporting Year 1 teachers in building on foundations laid in Reception
- Developing assessment approaches that recognise children's prior learning
- Planning curricula that bridge the EYFS and NC frameworks

### Developmentally Appropriate Geography

Young's work consistently emphasises the importance of **developmentally appropriate** geography—teaching that respects young children's cognitive, emotional, and physical development while still providing genuine geographical learning.

**Sensory and Experiential Learning**

For Young, geography in the early years must be rooted in **sensory and experiential learning**. Young children learn about their environment by:
- Exploring physical spaces through movement and play
- Handling natural materials and observing weather phenomena
- Visiting places in their local area and observing features
- Creating representations through art, model-making, and emergent writing

This emphasis on experience over abstract learning reflects Young's understanding of how young children make sense of the world, while ensuring that these experiences are recognised as genuine geographical learning.

**Play-Based Approaches**

Young has been a strong advocate for **play-based approaches** to geography in the early years and Key Stage 1. This includes:
- Small world play that explores geographical concepts through miniature landscapes
- Role play that enables children to inhabit different geographical contexts
- Construction play that involves creating and navigating spaces
- Outdoor play that provides large-scale geographical experiences

Her work demonstrates how play can be purposeful and educational while remaining enjoyable and age-appropriate.

### Supporting Non-Specialist Teachers

A significant challenge in primary geography is that most primary teachers are not geography specialists. Young's work has consistently addressed this reality, providing support that:
- Explains geographical concepts clearly for non-specialists
- Provides practical activity ideas that do not require extensive subject knowledge
- Offers guidance on common misconceptions and how to address them
- Suggests resources and approaches that are manageable for generalist primary teachers

This practical, teacher-focused approach has made her work particularly valuable for primary schools seeking to improve their geography provision.

### Early Years Guidance

Young's work on Early Years geography has been published in various forms, including:
- Articles in *Primary Geography*, the GA's journal for primary practitioners
- Online guidance and resources for early years practitioners
- Training materials and professional development programmes
- Contributions to curriculum development projects

These publications share a common focus on making geography accessible and practical for early years educators.

### Fieldwork and Local Area Studies

A recurring theme in Young's publications is the importance of **fieldwork** and **local area studies** in primary geography. She has provided guidance on:
- Planning and conducting fieldwork with young children
- Using the local area as a resource for geographical learning
- Developing children's observational skills through local studies
- Ensuring that fieldwork is safe, manageable, and educationally valuable

This emphasis on the local reflects Young's belief that primary geography should begin with children's immediate environment before moving to more distant places.

## Pedagogical Approach

### Starting with the Local

Young's pedagogical approach consistently emphasises **starting with the local**—using children's immediate environment as the foundation for geographical learning. This involves:
- Studying the school grounds and their features
- Exploring the local neighbourhood and its characteristics
- Investigating how the local area has changed over time
- Comparing the local area with other, contrasting locations

This local focus makes geography relevant and tangible for young learners while building the foundational concepts that enable understanding of more distant places.

### Progressive Development

Young's approach emphasises **progressive development** of geographical understanding across the primary years:
- **EYFS**: Sensory exploration and emergent understanding of space and place
- **Key Stage 1**: Development of basic geographical vocabulary and concepts; introduction to maps and simple geographical comparisons
- **Lower Key Stage 2**: Expansion of locational knowledge; development of more systematic enquiry skills
- **Upper Key Stage 2**: More complex geographical thinking; introduction to abstract concepts and wider scales

This progression ensures that geographical learning builds coherently throughout the primary years.

### Integration with Other Subjects

Young recognises that primary geography often needs to be taught in **integration** with other subjects, particularly in topic-based or thematic approaches. Her guidance addresses:
- How to maintain geographical integrity when geography is taught through topics
- Identifying when topics are genuinely geographical and when geography is being used merely as a context
- Ensuring that cross-curricular approaches include genuine geographical learning
- Assessing geographical learning within integrated curricula

This practical guidance helps teachers navigate the complexities of primary curriculum organisation while preserving the distinctiveness of geographical learning.

## Influence on Policy and Practice

### National Curriculum Implementation

Young's work has supported primary schools in implementing the **2014 National Curriculum** for geography. Her contributions have addressed:
- Interpreting the statutory requirements for primary phases
- Ensuring coverage of prescribed content while maintaining coherence
- Addressing the increased emphasis on locational knowledge
- Managing the requirements for fieldwork in primary settings

This support has been particularly valuable given the significant changes introduced in 2014 and the limited initial guidance provided for implementation.

### Ofsted and Inspection

Young's work has also supported schools in preparing for **Ofsted inspection**, particularly regarding:
- The deep dive methodology that inspectors use to evaluate geography
- Demonstrating progression in geographical learning
- Showing how the curriculum addresses the breadth of geographical content
- Evidence of fieldwork and practical geographical activities

Her guidance helps schools present their geography provision effectively while maintaining focus on genuine educational quality rather than mere compliance.

## Key Themes and Principles

### The Distinctiveness of Geography

Throughout her work, Young has emphasised the **distinctiveness of geography** as a subject. Even when taught through topics or integrated approaches, geography has its own concepts, methods, and forms of knowledge that must be preserved. This includes:
- Spatial thinking and understanding of location and distribution
- The relationship between physical and human environments
- The concept of place and how places are distinctive
- Environmental understanding and sustainability
- Geographical enquiry and fieldwork methods

Young's insistence on maintaining geographical distinctiveness has been important in countering pressures to dilute geography into general topic work.

### Inclusion and Accessibility

Young's work consistently addresses **inclusion and accessibility** in primary geography:
- Ensuring that geographical learning is accessible to all children, regardless of background or ability
- Recognising and valuing the geographical experiences that children bring from their home lives
- Providing for children with special educational needs in geographical activities
- Addressing the diverse experiences of children in different settings (urban, rural, coastal, etc.)

This inclusive approach ensures that geography education serves all learners.

### Environmental Awareness

A recurring theme in Young's work is the development of **environmental awareness** through primary geography. This includes:
- Helping children understand their relationship with the natural environment
- Developing awareness of environmental issues at an age-appropriate level
- Supporting children as active citizens who can contribute to their environment
- Building foundations for more sophisticated environmental understanding in secondary education

This emphasis on environment reflects both the National Curriculum requirements and broader educational concerns about sustainability.

## Practical Applications for Teachers

### For Early Years Practitioners

Drawing on Young's work, early years practitioners might:
- **Recognise geographical learning** in children's play and exploration
- **Provide rich environments** that invite geographical investigation
- **Use the outdoors** as a primary site for geographical learning
- **Develop emergent mapping** through treasure hunts, trail-making, and model-building
- **Read geographical stories** that expand children's understanding of places

### For Key Stage 1 Teachers

For Key Stage 1, Young's work suggests:
- **Begin with the immediate environment** before moving to distant places
- **Use stories and literature** to bring distant places to life
- **Develop mapping skills** progressively from 3D to 2D representations
- **Ensure regular outdoor learning** for geographical exploration
- **Make comparisons** between the local area and contrasting locations

### For Subject Leaders

Primary geography subject leaders can draw on Young's work to:
- **Audit current provision** and identify areas for development
- **Support non-specialist colleagues** with clear guidance and resources
- **Plan for progression** across the primary years
- **Ensure curriculum coherence** while respecting teacher autonomy
- **Advocate for geography** within the school's priorities

## Conclusion

Helen Young's contributions to primary geography education have been transformative, helping to establish geography as a valued and viable subject in the primary curriculum. Her work on Early Years geography has been particularly significant, recognising and developing the geographical learning that occurs before formal schooling begins.

The principles that characterise her work—developmental appropriateness, practical classroom focus, support for non-specialists, and insistence on geographical distinctiveness—remain essential for primary geography. As schools continue to navigate curriculum requirements and inspection pressures, Young's guidance provides a foundation for maintaining high-quality, engaging geographical education for young learners.

Her legacy lies in the thousands of primary classrooms where geography is now taught with greater confidence and purpose, and in the children who develop their understanding of the world through well-planned, meaningful geographical experiences.
