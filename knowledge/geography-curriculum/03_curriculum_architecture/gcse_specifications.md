# GCSE Geography Specifications Overview

## Introduction

GCSE Geography is one of the most popular optional subjects at Key Stage 4 in England, consistently attracting high numbers of students due to its relevance to contemporary global challenges and its reputation as a facilitating subject for further education and employment. The current GCSE Geography specifications were reformed for first teaching in September 2016, following the Department for Education's (DfE) 2014 subject content consultation and subsequent 2015 publication of revised subject criteria.

This knowledge base entry provides RE teachers and curriculum leaders with a comprehensive overview of GCSE Geography specifications across all major awarding bodies. It covers the statutory requirements, the structure and content of each specification, assessment arrangements, fieldwork requirements, and guidance on selecting and implementing a specification within your school context.

## Statutory Framework and Subject Content

### DfE Subject Content Requirements (2014)

The Department for Education sets the statutory subject content that all GCSE Geography specifications must follow. The current content document (DFE-00345-2014) establishes minimum requirements that awarding bodies must include, while allowing flexibility in how content is organised and additional topics are selected.

**Core Requirements (all specifications must include):**

**Physical Geography Topics:**
- The challenge of natural hazards (tectonic hazards, weather hazards, climate change)
- The living world (ecosystems, tropical rainforests, hot deserts or cold environments)
- Physical landscapes in the UK (coastal landscapes, river landscapes, glacial landscapes – at least two)

**Human Geography Topics:**
- Urban issues and challenges (urbanisation, urban growth in LICs/NEEs, UK cities)
- The changing economic world (development, global variations, the changing UK economy)
- The challenge of resource management (food, water, energy – at least one)

**Geographical Skills:**
- Cartographic skills (map reading, grid references, scale, direction)
- Graphical skills (interpreting graphs, charts, diagrams)
- Numerical skills (statistical analysis, data manipulation)
- Literacy skills (extended writing, geographical terminology)
- GIS (Geographical Information Systems) skills

**Fieldwork Requirements:**
- Two contrasting fieldwork experiences in different environments
- One physical geography enquiry
- One human geography enquiry
- Fieldwork must include data collection, presentation, analysis, and evaluation

### Assessment Objectives

All GCSE Geography specifications are assessed against three Assessment Objectives (AOs):

**AO1: Demonstrate knowledge of locations, places, processes, environments and different scales** (15% of marks)

**AO2: Demonstrate geographical understanding of concepts and how they are used in relation to places, environments and processes; and the interrelationships between places, environments and processes** (25% of marks)

**AO3: Apply knowledge and understanding to interpret, analyse and evaluate geographical information and issues to make judgements** (35% of marks, including 10% applied to fieldwork context)

**AO4: Select, adapt and use a variety of skills and techniques to investigate questions and issues and communicate findings** (25% of marks, including 5% applied to fieldwork context)

## AQA Specification (8035)

### Overview

AQA is the largest awarding body for GCSE Geography, with their specification (8035) being the most widely adopted. The AQA specification emphasises a thematic approach balanced between physical and human geography.

### Structure

| Component | Title | Weighting | Duration |
|-----------|-------|-----------|----------|
| Paper 1 | Living with the Physical Environment | 35% | 1 hour 30 minutes |
| Paper 2 | Challenges in the Human Environment | 35% | 1 hour 30 minutes |
| Paper 3 | Geographical Applications | 30% | 1 hour 15 minutes |

### Paper 1: Living with the Physical Environment

**Section A: The Challenge of Natural Hazards**
- Tectonic hazards (earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, tsunamis)
- Weather hazards (tropical storms, UK extreme weather)
- Climate change (evidence, causes, impacts, management)

**Section B: The Living World**
- Ecosystems and biomes
- Tropical rainforests (characteristics, biodiversity, deforestation, sustainable management)
- Hot deserts OR cold environments (case study required)

**Section C: Physical Landscapes in the UK**
- Coastal landscapes (processes, landforms, management)
- River landscapes (processes, landforms, flooding, management)
- Glacial landscapes (processes, landforms, tourism, management)

### Paper 2: Challenges in the Human Environment

**Section A: Urban Issues and Challenges**
- Urbanisation trends globally and in the UK
- Urban growth in lower-income countries (LICs) and newly emerging economies (NEEs)
- Urban sustainability (transport, waste, energy, urban greening)

**Section B: The Changing Economic World**
- Measures of development (HDI, GNI, etc.)
- Global variations in economic development and quality of life
- Strategies for reducing the development gap
- The changing UK economy (deindustrialisation, tertiary/quaternary sectors, science parks)

**Section C: The Challenge of Resource Management**
- Global resource consumption patterns
- Food, water, OR energy resource management (student studies one)

### Paper 3: Geographical Applications

**Section A: Issue Evaluation**
- Pre-release booklet issued 12 weeks before examination
- Students analyse and evaluate a contemporary geographical issue using secondary sources

**Section B: Fieldwork**
- Questions based on students' own fieldwork experiences (both physical and human enquiries)
- Requires understanding of data collection, presentation, analysis, and evaluation methods

### AQA Fieldwork Requirements

- Two contrasting fieldwork enquiries (one physical, one human)
- Minimum of two days of fieldwork outside the classroom
- Data collection must include both primary and secondary sources
- Students must demonstrate understanding of:
  - Selecting appropriate fieldwork techniques
  - Presenting data (maps, graphs, diagrams)
  - Analysing and interpreting data
  - Evaluating fieldwork methods and findings

## Edexcel Specifications

Edexcel offers two distinct GCSE Geography specifications: Specification A (thematic approach similar to AQA) and Specification B (issues-based approach).

### Edexcel Specification A (1GA0)

**Structure:**

| Component | Title | Weighting | Duration |
|-----------|-------|-----------|----------|
| Paper 1 | The Physical Environment | 37.5% | 1 hour 30 minutes |
| Paper 2 | The Human Environment | 37.5% | 1 hour 30 minutes |
| Paper 3 | Geographical Investigations: Fieldwork and UK Challenges | 25% | 1 hour 30 minutes |

**Paper 1: The Physical Environment**
- Topic 1: The changing landscapes of the UK (coasts OR rivers – student choice)
- Topic 2: Weather hazards and climate change
- Topic 3: Ecosystems, biodiversity and management

**Paper 2: The Human Environment**
- Topic 4: Changing cities (urbanisation, UK city case study, sustainable urban development)
- Topic 5: Global development
- Topic 6: Resource management (energy OR water – student choice)

**Paper 3: Geographical Investigations**
- UK Challenges: Investigating dynamic UK landscapes and changing UK cities
- Fieldwork: Questions on both physical and human fieldwork enquiries

### Edexcel Specification B (1GB0)

Specification B takes an issues-based approach, examining geographical topics through the lens of contemporary challenges and decisions.

**Structure:**

| Component | Title | Weighting | Duration |
|-----------|-------|-----------|----------|
| Paper 1 | Global Geographical Issues | 37.5% | 1 hour 30 minutes |
| Paper 2 | UK Geographical Issues | 37.5% | 1 hour 30 minutes |
| Paper 3 | People and Environment Issues – Making Geographical Decisions | 25% | 1 hour 30 minutes |

**Paper 1: Global Geographical Issues**
- Topic 1: Hazardous Earth (tectonic hazards, tropical storms, climate change)
- Topic 2: Development dynamics (development gap, emerging countries, aid, development futures)
- Topic 3: Challenges of an urbanising world (LIC/NEE cities, UK city case study)

**Paper 2: UK Geographical Issues**
- Topic 4: The UK's evolving physical landscape (coastal OR river landscapes)
- Topic 5: The UK's evolving human landscape (changing economy, population, urban/rural changes)
- Topic 6: Geographical investigations (fieldwork)

**Paper 3: People and Environment Issues**
- Topic 7: People and the biosphere (ecosystems, global biomes, deforestation)
- Topic 8: Forests under threat (tropical rainforest OR deciduous woodland case study)
- Topic 9: Consuming energy resources (energy security, renewable vs. fossil fuels)
- Making Geographical Decisions: Students evaluate options for managing a resource issue

### Edexcel Fieldwork Requirements

- Four days of fieldwork (increased from two days in earlier specifications)
- One physical geography enquiry
- One human geography enquiry
- Two contrasting environments
- Assessment through examination questions on Paper 3 (Spec A) or Paper 2 (Spec B)

## OCR Specification (J383)

OCR's GCSE Geography specification (J383) is structured around engaging themes and contemporary issues, with a focus on interconnections between physical and human geography.

**Structure:**

| Component | Title | Weighting | Duration |
|-----------|-------|-----------|----------|
| Paper 1 | Living in the UK Today | 40% | 1 hour 30 minutes |
| Paper 2 | The World Around Us | 40% | 1 hour 30 minutes |
| Paper 3 | Geographical Skills | 20% | 1 hour 30 minutes |

**Paper 1: Living in the UK Today**
- Landscapes of the UK (coastal and river landscapes)
- People of the UK (population, urbanisation, changing cities)
- UK environmental challenges (flooding, water stress, energy security)

**Paper 2: The World Around Us**
- Ecosystems of the planet (tropical rainforests and polar environments)
- People of the planet (development, global inequalities, emerging economies)
- Environmental threats to our planet (climate change, extreme weather, tectonic hazards)

**Paper 3: Geographical Skills**
- Geographical skills and fieldwork (OS maps, GIS, data analysis)
- Two days of fieldwork (one physical, one human)
- Fieldwork questions based on students' own experiences

### OCR Fieldwork Requirements

- Two contrasting fieldwork enquiries
- Assessment through Paper 3
- Questions require students to apply knowledge of fieldwork techniques to unfamiliar contexts as well as their own fieldwork

## Eduqas (WJEC) Specifications

Eduqas offers two GCSE Geography specifications: Specification A and Specification B.

### Eduqas Specification A (C111)

**Structure:**

| Component | Title | Weighting | Duration |
|-----------|-------|-----------|----------|
| Paper 1 | Changing Physical and Human Landscapes | 40% | 1 hour 30 minutes |
| Paper 2 | Environmental and Development Issues | 40% | 1 hour 30 minutes |
| Paper 3 | Applied Fieldwork Enquiry | 20% | 1 hour 30 minutes |

**Paper 1: Changing Physical and Human Landscapes**
- Landscapes and physical processes (coasts OR rivers)
- Rural-urban links (urbanisation, rural change, sustainability)

**Paper 2: Environmental and Development Issues**
- Weather, climate, and ecosystems
- Development and resource issues
- Tectonic landscapes and hazards

**Paper 3: Applied Fieldwork Enquiry**
- Fieldwork skills and techniques
- Two contrasting fieldwork enquiries

### Eduqas Specification B (C112)

Specification B adopts a thematic approach organised around geographical concepts.

**Structure:**

| Component | Title | Weighting | Duration |
|-----------|-------|-----------|----------|
| Paper 1 | Investigating Geographical Issues | 40% | 1 hour 45 minutes |
| Paper 2 | Problem Solving Geography | 30% | 1 hour 30 minutes |
| Paper 3 | Applied Fieldwork Enquiry | 30% | 1 hour 30 minutes |

**Key Themes:**
- Theme 1: Changing Places – Changing Economies
- Theme 2: Changing Environments
- Theme 3: Environmental Challenges

### Eduqas Fieldwork Requirements

- Two contrasting fieldwork enquiries in different environments
- Assessment through Paper 3
- Emphasis on understanding and applying fieldwork methodologies

## Fieldwork Requirements: Detailed Guidance

### Statutory Requirements

All GCSE Geography specifications require:
- Two contrasting fieldwork enquiries
- One physical geography investigation
- One human geography investigation
- Data collection in different environments
- Use of primary and secondary data

### COVID-19 Modifications (2020-2022)

During the pandemic, Ofqual introduced temporary modifications:
- Fieldwork activities still required but could be conducted in school grounds
- Examination questions about students' own fieldwork were removed
- These arrangements have ended; full requirements are restored

### Recommended Fieldwork Locations

**Physical Geography:**
- Coastal landscapes: Beach profiles, cliff surveys, sediment analysis
- River landscapes: Channel cross-sections, velocity measurements, bedload analysis
- Glacial landscapes: Landform mapping, tourism impact assessment

**Human Geography:**
- Urban areas: Land use mapping, environmental quality surveys, pedestrian counts
- Rural areas: Services mapping, deprivation surveys
- Economic geography: Tourism impact assessment, retail surveys

## Selecting a Specification: Curriculum Leaders' Guide

### Factors to Consider

**Student Cohort:** Prior attainment, interest in physical vs. human geography, fieldwork accessibility

**Staff Expertise:** Specialisms, experience with assessment styles, fieldwork organisation

**Resources:** Access to physical environments, fieldwork budget, local geography

**Assessment Structure:** Number of papers, fieldwork weighting, pre-release components

### Specification Recommendations

**Choose AQA if:**
- You want the most widely adopted specification
- You value the pre-release issue evaluation component
- You want flexibility in fieldwork location choice

**Choose Edexcel A if:**
- You want flexibility in topic choice (coasts OR rivers)
- You prefer a thematic structure
- You can accommodate four days of fieldwork

**Choose Edexcel B if:**
- You want an issues-based approach
- You value decision-making exercises
- You want clear UK/global paper divisions

**Choose OCR if:**
- You want a dedicated geographical skills paper
- You prefer clear UK/global paper divisions
- You value skills-based assessment

**Choose Eduqas if:**
- You are in Wales or have Welsh connections
- You prefer a conceptual approach (Spec B)
- You want integrated UK/global content

## Teaching and Learning Strategies for GCSE Geography

### Differentiation and Inclusion

Effective GCSE Geography teaching requires careful differentiation to meet the needs of all learners:

**For Lower-Attaining Students:**
- Use structured writing frames for extended responses
- Provide keyword glossaries and visual supports
- Focus on core case studies rather than extensive breadth
- Use frequent retrieval practice to secure foundational knowledge
- Break complex processes into sequential steps

**For Higher-Attaining Students:**
- Encourage independent research beyond specification content
- Develop evaluative and synoptic thinking skills
- Introduce geographical theory and academic perspectives
- Require analysis of conflicting evidence and interpretations
- Stretch through application to unfamiliar contexts

### Assessment for Learning

Regular assessment supports progress in GCSE Geography:

**Formative Assessment Strategies:**
- Knowledge quizzes on key terminology and case study facts
- Eight-mark question practice with peer assessment
- Fieldwork technique demonstrations and evaluations
- Map skills drills with immediate feedback
- Synoptic thinking exercises linking physical and human geography

**Summative Assessment:**
- Past paper practice under examination conditions
- Mock examinations in Year 11
- Assessment of fieldwork portfolios
- Regular testing of case study knowledge

### Developing Geographical Literacy

Geographical literacy is essential for success at GCSE:

**Reading Geography:**
- Interpretation of OS maps at various scales
- Analysis of satellite imagery and aerial photographs
- Reading and critiquing academic geographical texts
- Understanding newspaper articles on geographical issues

**Writing Geography:**
- Structured extended responses using PEEL or similar frameworks
- Effective use of geographical terminology
- Integration of case study evidence into explanations
- Balanced evaluation of geographical decisions

**Speaking Geography:**
- Debates on geographical controversies
- Presentations on fieldwork findings
- Discussions linking local and global geographical issues
- Use of precise geographical vocabulary in oral contributions

## Common Misconceptions in GCSE Geography

### Physical Geography Misconceptions

**"Earthquakes are caused by weather"**
- Clarify that earthquakes result from tectonic plate movement, not atmospheric conditions
- Distinguish between earthquakes and weather-related hazards

**"Rivers flow from north to south"**
- Emphasise that rivers flow from higher altitude to lower altitude regardless of compass direction
- Use contour map exercises to reinforce this understanding

**"Climate change and global warming are the same thing"**
- Explain that global warming refers specifically to rising temperatures
- Climate change encompasses broader changes including precipitation patterns, extreme weather, etc.

### Human Geography Misconceptions

**"Development is only about money"**
- Broaden understanding using HDI and other composite indices
- Discuss development in terms of health, education, and quality of life

**"All people in LICs are poor"**
- Address inequality within countries using the concept of development gaps
- Discuss emerging economies and changing economic patterns

**"Urbanisation only happens in poor countries"**
- Explain that urbanisation is a global phenomenon
- Discuss counter-urbanisation and re-urbanisation in HICs

### Fieldwork Misconceptions

**"Primary data is always better than secondary data"**
- Discuss the reliability and representativeness of different data types
- Explain how secondary data can provide valuable context and comparison

**"Fieldwork is just about collecting data"**
- Emphasise the full enquiry process: question, hypothesis, method, data collection, presentation, analysis, evaluation

## Progression from GCSE to A Level

Students considering A Level Geography should develop:

**Knowledge Foundation:**
- Secure understanding of core GCSE topics
- Familiarity with key concepts (systems, place, space, scale)
- Experience of synoptic thinking

**Skills Foundation:**
- Confidence in independent fieldwork design
- Ability to write extended analytical responses
- Experience of using and interpreting GIS
- Statistical competence

**Academic Dispositions:**
- Curiosity about geographical issues
- Willingness to engage with complexity and uncertainty
- Interest in current affairs and global issues
- Ability to think critically about sources and evidence

## Conclusion

GCSE Geography offers students a rigorous, relevant, and engaging qualification that develops both substantive geographical knowledge and disciplinary skills. The choice of specification should be made carefully, considering the needs of your student cohort, the expertise of your staff, the resources available to you, and the geographical characteristics of your locality.

All specifications meet the DfE's statutory requirements and provide a sound foundation for further study or employment. The key to success lies not in the choice of exam board but in the quality of teaching, the richness of fieldwork experiences, and the development of students' geographical thinking.

By understanding the similarities and differences between specifications, curriculum leaders can make informed decisions that best serve their students and ensure that all young people have the opportunity to develop into informed, engaged, and geographically literate citizens.
